Lesson 15

Topic: Welcoming The King (Palm Sunday)

Text: Luke 19:28 – 40, Matthew 21:1 – 11

Central Truth:

Jesus Christ is the King of kings whose reign has no

beginning and no end.

Memory Verse: Luke 19:38

Lesson Outline

I. The King Revealed (Luke 19:28-35)

II. The King Received (Luke 19:35-40).

Lesson Introduction

The title and words of the old spiritual “Ride on, King Jesus” recall the day when Jesus was received into Jerusalem as king. The welcome of Jesus into the holy city on the Sunday before His crucifixion has long been called the triumphal entry. This event is found in all four of the Gospels, each having some unique details. Although the Gospel does not make any reference to this event as a “triumph,” it does bear some relationship to the ancient custom of welcoming a victorious king or general back to his home city. The city of Rome had a tradition of staging triumphal processions. This parade would include the Roman legions, enemy prisoners, wagons loaded with booty, and the victorious general in a special chariot. Sometimes a new triumphal arch would be created. This custom was revived by the French emperor Napoleon Bonaparte, whose Arc de Triomphe in Paris was commissioned in 1806 for the glory of the French army. The triumphal entry of Jesus was a way of recognizing Him as king. What does it mean for us today? This week’s lesson will examine some of the implications involved in recognizing Jesus as King in our lives.

Lesson Commentary

I. The King Revealed (Luke 19:28-35).

The focus of Christianity is on Jesus Christ. The only begotten Son of God, who came to redeem humanity from corruption and eternal damnation of the fallen world, granting us the hope and gift of eternal life. The Bible is full of references to the revealing of Jesus as the Messiah. Isaiah declared that the Saviour would come seeking after His own people, to cleanse and purify them. He would also give reward to the faithful (Isaiah 62:11). Zachariah portrays Him as the King coming to comfort His people (Zech. 9:9).

The visit of Jesus to Jerusalem was a graceful one. It was very significant because of the events that followed. It was so glamorous that people trooped out to catch a glimpse of the great King. A short influential man, Zaccheaus had to climb a tree just to have an opportunity of seeing the approaching King (Luke 19:1-4). The crowd also struggled to see Him.

Jesus had come from Jericho, a short distance from Jerusalem at Bethphage and Bethany. When it was time for Him to go up to Jerusalem, He prepared His way for the entry. He stopped at a point to prepare a means of entering the city that people would know He was presenting Himself as the Messiah. He commanded two of His disciples to find a donkey and a colt and bring them to Him. If anyone questioned their actions, they were to say the Lord needed them. As Messiah, He had the right to request whatever He needed. Matthew mentioned in 21: 4-5, that this act fulfilled divine prophecy: Zechariah 9:9 (cf. Isa 62:11), which spoke to the nation of Israel about the coming of her King in a gentle manner riding on... a colt, the foal (lit., son) of a donkey. This was not the normal manner in which kings arrived, for they usually came as conquerors riding on horses. A colt was a symbol of peace.

After the disciples got the animals, they threw their garments on them to make saddles, and people in the large crowd spread their cloaks and tree branches on the road. Most of these people were pilgrims from Galilee on their way to Jerusalem to celebrate the Passover. They were familiar with Jesus and the many miracles He had performed in Galilee.

II. The King Received (Luke 19:35-40).

What a glorious and happy day, the King of kings, the Lord of lords openly rode on the common street of Jerusalem. He was greeted with high funfair and salutation, "'Blessed is the King who comes in the name of the Lord!' Peace in heaven and glory in the highest!" (Luke 19:38). He was riding on a colt in a royal majesty and humility as a caring King. “Then, as He was now drawing near the descent of the Mount of Olives, the whole multitude of the disciples began to rejoice and praise God with a loud voice for all the mighty works they had seen,” (Luke 19:37). The coming of the King was a consolation, to give comfort to His people.

The crowd in Jerusalem welcomed Jesus. He was hailed as the Messiah on what Christians call "Palm Sunday." Jesus' enemies were aroused by this event, and renewed their attacks against Him. Jesus silenced them and in turn boldly condemned their wickedness and hypocrisy. Jesus responded that there must be some proclamation that He is the Messiah, if not, even inanimate objects (stones) would be called on to testify for Him.

As the people walked along, some before Jesus and some behind Him, they were probably singing some of the pilgrim psalms. Matthew 21:15 noted that the people, including children, shouted the words of Psalms 118:26, “Blessed is He who comes in the name of the Lord…” To Him they shouted, Hosanna to the Son of David. "Hosanna" is from the Hebrew, meaning, "Save (us), we pray," taken from Ps 118:25. It came to be a note of praise as well as petition.

While the crowd did not fully understand the significance of this event, they seemed to be acknowledging that this One is the promised Seed of David who had come to grant them salvation. Both their actions and words bestowed honour on this One coming into the city, at last presenting Himself publicly as their King. This event marked the official presentation of Jesus Christ to the nation of Israel as the rightful Son of David.

Conclusion

The Old Testament is a shadow of the New Testament. It points to the coming of the Messiah. Many O/T writers wrote about Him as the promised Messiah. Everything about His life and ministry were predicted. The Gospel of Matthew did a good justice to this, pointing out every reference with, “that it might be fulfilled …”

As Jesus advanced down the west side of the Mount of Olives towards the city, He was praised by the crowd as their Messiah. The act of spreading their cloaks on the road in front of Jesus was a sign of respect. The whole crowd of people began joyfully to praise God... for all the miracles (evidences of spiritual power) they had seen. The Pharisees understood the meaning of what was going on, for they told Jesus to rebuke His followers, so they would stop calling Him Messiah or King. Jesus responded that there must be some proclamation that He is the Messiah. If not, even inanimate objects (stones) would be called on to testify for Him. All history had pointed towards this single, spectacular event when the Messiah publicly presented Himself to the nation, and God desired that this fact be acknowledged.

Life Application

The triumphal entry of Jesus into the city of Jerusalem caused the whole city to stand still, a symbol of respect and honour to a great King / authority. Jesus must be honoured and welcome in our lives, homes, cities and in everything.

He was heralded with great cry; riding on a colt which signifies royalty, humility, meekness and simplicity. Pride comes before a fall because it is an abomination before God. We must emulate the simplicity, meekness and humility of Jesus.

His popularity among the people stirred up the jealousy and indignation of the Pharisees who tried to stop the people from praising and honouring Jesus. This teaches us that our conviction of faith in Jesus should make us stand steadfast even in the midst of persecution. We should remain steadfast and immovable as children of God.

Jesus did not take action as a military conqueror to prove that His kingdom is spiritual. We don’t need to show-off, but allow God to have His way in our lives. We should also be moderate and temperate in all things.

Questions

  1. What was the response of the Jews (people and Pharisees) about the entry of Jesus to Jerusalem?
  2. What is the significance of His riding on a colt?
  3. What do you think will be the response of the world today if Jesus should appear now?

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

  • Jesus entering into Jerusalem was predicted by prophet Zachariah (Zech. 9:9).
  • Riding on a colt to Jerusalem publicly testifies to the prediction that He was Israel’s King and Messiah.
  • The use of colt symbolizes humility and care. It also signifies that Jesus did not come into this world (Israel) as a forceful /violent ruler, showing that His kingdom was not of this world.
  • That everybody wanted to see Him signifies His importance and influence.
  • People laying down their clothes signifies His royalty, majesty, power, authority and dominion.
  • The cry, "'Blessed is the King who comes in the name of the Lord!' Peace in heaven and glory in the highest!" (Luke 19:38). shows:

- Recognition and acceptance of Jesus as King of the Jews.

- His coming was with the authority of God.

- His coming will bring peace, comfort and succour to the people.

- His coming brings glory and honour to God, and blessings to humanity.

Lesson 16

Topic: Great Loss and New Life (Easter Sunday)

Text: John 16:16-24; 20:1-18; 30-31

Central Truth:

Jesus Christ was crucified, dead and buried, He rose from the dead and lives forevermore.

Memory Verse: Luke 23:6

Lesson Outline

I. The Preparation (John 16:16-24)

II. The Empty Tomb (Resurrection) (John 20:1-18)

III. The New Life (John 20:30-31)

Lesson Introduction

The cornerstone of the Christian faith is the teaching on the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ. It is absolutely essential to individual salvation and the formation of the church. Yet this truth was the most difficult for the disciples of Jesus to grasp. Whenever He introduced the subject to prepare them, He met either vigorous objection or total lack of understanding. The lack of comprehension still existed as Jesus approached the cross. He had to explain to the Twelve what lies beyond His death. Even after His resurrection, He had to counter the unbelief of Mary Magdalene and other loyal followers. May our faith be strengthened by the truths He taught them.

Lesson Commentary

I. The Preparation (John 16:16-24)

Great men or leaders used to prepare for their departure from earth by setting things and their houses in order. This is done mainly to ensure sanity, peace and harmony among those they leave behind. Modern days permit and even encourage the writing of ‘Will’. Many families has suffered, even still suffering untold hardship, conflict, division and enmity due to the demise of their benefactor, who died intestate. When King Hezekiah was sick unto death, God sent a prophet to advise him to set his house in order (2 Kings 20:1). We need to prepare for the eventualities.

Jesus Christ, as a great leader, made concerted efforts to prepare His disciples for His mission on earth – to suffer persecution, die on the cross, and be raised again on the third day. He desired that His disciples would have an understanding of what would befall Him, so that they would not be taken unawares, but their understanding was darkened. They were blindfolded by unbelief.

In John 16:16-22, Jesus was referring to His death, which was a few days away, and His resurrection three days after. At the garden of Gethsemane, Jesus prayed for Peter that his faith be strengthened and he should be able to strengthen others. Despite Jesus’ attempt at making His disciples have spiritual knowledge of divine agenda concerning Him, they rejected it because it didn’t sound rational; probably because it involved suffering, pain and reproach, hardship and death. A great lesson we need to learn is that because something does not sound pleasing, appears unattractive, involves suffering or challenges, does not mean God is not involved. James admonishes us to count it all joy when we fall into diverse temptations. Trials and temptations give faith the opportunity to grow & develop (James 1:2-4). Faith is not faith until it is tried. Proverb 24:10 says , “If thou faint in the day of adversity, thy strength is small.” (Prov. 24:10).

II. The Empty Tomb (John 20:1-18)

Jesus was crucified according to the predetermined counsel of God, as the Lamb that takes away the sins of the world. The resurrection took place on the third day according to predictions. However, the arrest, trials and crucifixion of Jesus put the disciples into a state of confusion and uncertainty, as a result of their lack of understanding of the Messianic prophecy being fulfilled in their sight. To walk with God, we need divine revelation and insight.

On the resurrection morning, the first day of the week (Sunday), Mary Magdalene was at the tomb, only to find it empty. The stone had been rolled away, for all to see clearly that Jesus was no longer in the grave. Peter and John also came to witness to it. Peter entered the tomb to discover the linen wrappings lying there, which was a pointer to the fact that Jesus had resurrected. (John 20:8-9).

The concept of resurrection is a mystery. A first time hearer of the word is likely to disbelieve, regarding it as fallacy or fable. If he experiences visiting the empty tomb, he may come to a state of confusion. However, if he has an encounter with the resurrected Christ, like Mary Magdalene, he may be convinced. But if he commits himself in seeking God and serving Him, his faith would be established, and become a sure candidate of God’s eternal kingdom (John 8:31).

III. The New Life (John 20:30-31)

The redemptive plan involves the death, burial and resurrection of Jesus Christ. The fall of man in the Garden of Eden brought blighting curse on humanity. “Wherefore, as by one man sin entered into the world, and death by sin; and so death passed upon all men, for that all have sinned:” (Rom 5:12). The Scripture goes further to say, “For the wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.” (Rom. 6:23).

The substitutionary work of Jesus Christ resulted in:

  1. Salvation: Redemption & Righteousness (, 2 Cor. 5:21).
  2. New Birth: Spiritual rebirth & Renewal (Rom. 12:2, 2 Cor. 5:17, Titus 3:5).
  3. Sonship: Relationship & Fellowship restored (John 1:12, Gal. 3:26-28, 4:6).
  4. Eternal inheritance: Eternal life granted (John 3:16, Mat. 19:29).

Conclusion & Life Application

During His last days of earthly ministry, Jesus Christ was preparing His disciples for His departure, “A little while, and ye shall not see me: and again, a little while, and ye shall see me, because I go to the Father.” (John 16:16). The statement was bewildering to the disciples. The questions they asked themselves indicated that they were confused. However, only His death, resurrection, post-resurrection ministry, and ascension would make it all clear.

It indicates that we need spiritual discernment to comprehend divine instruction, leading and guidance. We should always seek for clarity of vision to succeed in our walk with God. God speaks sometimes in parables, symbols, imageries, etc. Hence, we need divine understanding to comprehend His message. The purpose of God is revealed per time and we need to move with the leading of the Spirit in order to keep pace with divine agenda.

Jesus made an illustration of pain replaced by joy with the pain of child birth, which is followed by the joy of new life when a child is born. We need perseverance, endurance and patience in our daily walk with God. The present problems and challenges will become history tomorrow. When the disciples saw Jesus after His resurrection, their joy erupted. ‘Joy that will never end since He died to sin once but now lives forevermore’ (Rom. 6:9-10, Luke 24:33-52, Hebrews 7:24-25).

Mary Magdalene came to the tomb early on Sunday morning. ‘Her devotion to Jesus, in life and in death, was based on her gratitude for His delivering her from the bondage of Satan. She had been an observer when Jesus was nailed on the cross and now the first person at the grave. Jesus’ proverb comes to mind , “Wherefore I say unto thee, her sins, which are many, are forgiven; for she loved much: but to whom little is forgiven, the same loveth little.” (Luke 7:47)

Questions & Discussion:

  1. What do you think Jesus Christ meant when He said,

“A little while, and ye shall not see me: and again, a little while, and ye shall see me, because I go to the Father.” (John 16:16).

  1. The disciples ran to the tomb to find the stone rolled away and the tomb empty. If you were one of them, what would be your reaction?
  2. Discuss the disposition of Mary Magdalene at the grave side.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

The statement of Jesus in preparing the disciples for His departure was not immediately understood as they questioned among themselves the meaning. The Master understood their confusion, but did not give clarity, knowing that they would come to understanding as the event unfolds. Rather, He gave solemn prediction that their coming grief would be followed by joy. His death would be bitter agony for them but the world would be happy over it. Nevertheless, His death would bring gladness to them eventually, as their grief would turn to joy (1 Cor. 1:23, 2:2). Furthermore, He gave an illustration of a woman in travail of birth, “A woman when she is in travail hath sorrow, because her hour is come: but as soon as she is delivered of the child, she remembereth no more the anguish, for joy that a man is born into the world.” (John 16:21).

All the Gospels relate the story, stressing certain aspects of the event of the empty tomb. However, John reveals a personal faith in the resurrection by considering the evidence found in the open tomb (John 20:1-9).

On Sunday, the first day of the week, Mary Magdalene (also called Mary of Magdala) came early in the morning to the tomb, but found it opened. The tomb was closed with a large rock door (Mark 16:3-4), and sealed by the authority of the Roman governor, Pontius Pilate (Matt. 27:15-16).

It was amazing to see the tomb opened and empty. They might have assumed that grave robbers had desecrated the tomb.

Peter and John ran to the tomb. When Peter rushed in, he saw the grave clothes and burial cloth separated. The position of the grave clothes and burial clothes ruled out the possibility of grave robbery. The opened tomb was not to allow the body of Jesus to go out, but an evidence to let the disciples and the world see that Jesus had indeed risen as predicted.

Lesson 17

Evangelism and Witnessing

Text: Ezek. 3:17-21; Dan.12:3; II Tim.4:1-2; John 4:35-37; Mat. 9:36-38.

Supplemental References:

Matt. 28:18-20; Mk. 16:15-18; Luke 24:46-49; John 20:21-22.

Central Truth:

The Great commission is a mandate for all Christians and there are many ways to reach people with the Gospel of Jesus Christ.

Memory Verse: II Tim.4:2

Preach the word! Be ready in season and out of season. Convince, rebuke, exhort, with all longsuffering and teaching .

Lesson Outline

I. The Nature and Hindrances to the Gospel

II. Authority and Scope of the Great Commission.

III. Types of Evangelism

IV. The Results and Effects of the Witnessing

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that evangelizing and making disciples is the primary “business” of the church; every hindrance to worldwide evangelism should be removed (James 5:20; Mark 16:15).

Today’s lesson is to stir up our hearts to fresh commitment to this important business of God which is gradually being consigned to few brethren. It must be stressed that evangelism is a general mandate that concerns all believers. Unfortunately, today’s believers are being encumbered by a lot of activities in the church which more or less give little or no room for evangelism. Many believers are very fearful that the available opportunities and platforms for them to reach out to others, especially in the market place are not utilized; others do not even know how to ‘preach’. It is often imagined that evangelism has to involve expert knowledge of the Bible. It is important for this lesson to clearly articulate God’s intention for mankind to be reached by the gospel of our Lord Jesus Christ, which does not require expert knowledge of man but desire to reach the Lost, simplicity and dependence on the Holy Spirit for unction.

As a matter of fact, our business as believers is to be sensitive to discern who to reach, when and where to do so and God’s business is to save the Lost.

Lesson Commentary:

I. The Nature and Hindrances to the Gospel

The greatest enemy of the Gospel is the devil. He can do anything to ensure the gospel which is the power of God unto salvation to all men is not preached. The devil does not mind to give anything as long as the soul is not liberated. When the devil appeared to the Lord, he showed Him the kingdoms of the world and the glory thereof and promised to give Him everything, if only He could bow to worship him. (Matt. 4:8-9) Of course, the Lord rebuked him. Today, many believers are losing grounds as the devil is busy trading their souls with mundane things of this world. The love of many is waxing cold and preaching the gospel is becoming burdensome. There is need for every Christian to wake up and dismantle the instrument of wickedness that Satan is employing to stop the preaching of the gospel. It is very necessary for us to understand that some of the methods employed by our arch enemy, Satan, include the following:

A. Disobedience

This is a great tool in the hand of the devil against the preaching of the Gospel. Many believers today do not realize that when God gives a commandment, it must be strictly obeyed.

In the military, it’s a great offence that attracts severe punishment for an order given by a superior officer to be flouted. There is need to understand that our God is sovereign and supreme. One of the Commandments given by God in the Bible is “Go ye into all the world and preach the gospel unto every creature” (Mark 16:15). It is almost inconceivable that an order given by a senior to a junior in in rank would not be obeyed.

We need to break down the wall of self will and begin to make important what God commanded in the bible. Evangelism is not optional, it is compulsory for all Christians to go out and reconcile the sinning world to Christ (II Cor. 5:18-19).

B. Fear and Intimidation

Many Christians are afraid of going out because of fear of being attacked or harassed. Some are even afraid that they do not know what to say. The Bible is also clear on some of these things. The truth is that, he that loves his life will lose it. (John 12:25). Also, there is no fear in love, for perfect love castest out fear because fear has torment and he that feareth is not made perfect in love (1John 4:18). Whenever you are going out to preach, all you need to do is pray and depend on God and He will take charge of the situation. God has promised to fill our mouth when we open it. (Ps. 81:10). We are in a world that is hostile and wicked, but God is able to protect us from the forces of darkness. There is need to be filled with the Holy Spirit which gives boldness.

C. Pride

Many consider evangelism as menial and should be done by people that are not old or positioned like them. Of course, pride makes a man feel too conscious of himself rather than what God is demanding. God indeed resists the proud but gives more grace to the humble (James 4:6). It is time to dismantle this unnecessary self-consciousness of our status and position and humble ourselves to work like the Lord Jesus did.

D. Distraction

We are in a world where distraction has become a big tool the devil uses to prevent Christians, especially young people from serving God. Today, the world of internet, television, social media and communication has made so many people to lose interest in going out to preach the gospel as instructed by the Lord. Many of the communication gadgets have become too addictive. Today, Christians are setting wrong priorities.

II. Authority and Scope of the Great Commission

A. We are backed by the Greatest Authority .

God is the power behind the preaching of the gospel. There is no limitation to what God can do when the gospel is preached. Jesus declared that All power in heaven and on earth have been given unto Him. In this effect, our business is to obey, while God’s business is to save. God can through the preaching of the gospel save, heal and even deliver. He may decide to encounter the worst of sinners and destroy the stronghold ruling communities. Simon the sorcerer was saved when Philip decided to preach in Samaria. This very manipulative sorcerer was saved and baptized and even began to follow the apostles (Acts 8:9, 18).

B. We are to preach the gospel all over the world.

The gospel is to be preached from village to village until it reaches the entire world. One of the cardinal factors that will herald the coming of the Lord is the preaching of the gospel round the entire world. (Matt. 24:14). We believers have been entrusted to carry out this important assignment. The best approach is to start from our Jerusalem and progress gradually to our Judea, and then to Samaria and finally, to the entire world. The implication is that some may have to preach in the villages, whilst some may preach in the cities and others may have to take the gospel abroad. The light of the gospel must shine through all nooks and cranny.

III. Types of Evangelism

A. Out Door Evangelism

Every opportunity must be utilized to spread the gospel by every believer. Usually, the platform has to involve some form of strategy to reach some people who may never even step into the church on their own. There is need to intentionally reach out to them. Like fishers of men that we are as believers, we need to spread our dragnets to catch all kinds of fish. Whatever method we use in Outdoor Evangelism, there is need to stress that Jesus must be preached. We must never lose the consciousness of this important fact. Some of the modes of Outdoor evangelism include the following: Door to Door, Medical Missions, Open Air Crusades, Road shows, Bus, School, Hospital, Prisons, Old People’s, Home and Social Media Evangelism.

B. Market and Work Place Evangelism

The work place is a veritable platform to preach the gospel. Every believer that desires to effectively preach the gospel in the market place must stand out. Indeed, Christians are God’s epistles written by the Spirit and not ink, but read by all men. Therefore, market evangelism should start with a conscious desire to stand out as God’s representative in that market and place of work. There may be need for the believers to start from “one on one” basis, by preaching to our colleagues, subordinates, superiors and even clients as the case may be. Do not devote the time for work in the office to do this. Rather, set aside time, especially your break period for the assignment to avoid being accused of spending employers time on preaching. Where the atmosphere is conducive either in the market or workplace, you may prayerfully establish a lunch hour fellowship. In most markets in Nigeria, early hours of Thursday have been set aside for cleaning of the markets. You may have to target how you can get a fraction of the time to do the Master’s business.

IV. The Result and effects Of Witnessing

Indeed, witnessing would bring great yield on the church, the society at large with longer implication on eternity.

A. Witnessing would bring many souls to church. Many of our churches today are empty because we have discounted this important assignment. The more evangelism we carry out, the more souls would be won for the Lord, and ultimately, these souls would become part of the church for discipleship. There is need for the church to wage war against empty chairs and go out to the highways and hedges to bring men into God’s house.

B. Witnessing would bring a saner society. Today’s society is full of wickedness. Every day, the Newspapers scream of atrocities committed by men. The truth is that when God removes the heart of stone from a man and implants the heart of flesh, the man becomes transformed. The greatest change agent in any society is the spread of the gospel. In our country today, able bodied men are jobless and many of these things resort to frustration which can best be managed through the gospel.

C. Witnessing would save sinners and believers from the wrath of God.

Conclusion & Life Application:

Today, the world is full of evil. In many communities, people can hardly sleep in the night because of the men of the underworld. The day also has her fair share of wickedness. We are in a generation where blood shedding is no more conscionable. Killings, rituals, abortions, kidnappings are all prevalent in our world. The way out is for the light of God to shine to remove darkness from the hearts of men. Our lesson is a confirmation that God is depending on the church to shine the light by preaching Jesus. The church must be ready to give this assignment the attention that it deserves without which the world would continue to wallow in self destructive attitude that has both serious earthly and eternal consequences on both the sinning world and the uncaring church.

Questions & Discussions:

1. Why do you think evangelism is so important to God.

2. Discuss the weapons that Satan uses to prevent the preaching of the gospel.

3. What are the likely consequences of refusing to preach the gospel in our communities and work places.

Lesson 18

Topic: Evangelism and Missions

Text: John.10:15-16; Matt.8:11; Acts 13:1-4; 16:9-10, 25-34; Rom.1:14-16; 15:20-21.

Central Truth:

Mission is the heartbeat of God, and the family is His primary focus.

Memory Verse: Romans 1:14-16

Lesson Outline:

I. Evangelism within the Family

II. Evangelism within the Church/Missions Outreach

Lesson Introduction

We believe that evangelizing and making disciples is the primary “business” of the church; every hindrance to worldwide evangelism should be removed (Jam. 5:20; Mk. 16:15). Our family members are also inclusive in our commission of evangelizing the world.

Lesson Commentary

I. Evangelism within the Family

The family unit has ever been the focus in evangelism. The church itself is a family. It comprises members of God’s family (Believers). The Church is the body of Christ. I Corinthians 12:12-14 says, “For as the body is one and has many members, but all the members of that one body, being many, are one body, so also is Christ. For by one Spirit we were all baptized into one body — whether Jews or Greeks, whether slaves or free — and have all been made to drink into one Spirit. For in fact the body is not one member but many.” Evangelism can be described as calling into the body of Christ those who are destined for redemption (i.e. calling together the members of Christ’s family). Going out to call and bringing them into the fold.

The creation story started with one family (Adam and Eve) in the Garden of Eden. When God destroyed the then existing world with water, He saved a family (Noah) and He used the family to repopulate the world (Gen. 7:1, 8:1, 9:1). In the same pattern, the plan for redemption began with one family (Abraham, Gen. 12:1-3). The easiest way to populate the church is to win families: focusing on family evangelism.

One of the methods of evangelism is to reach out to members of our family. In John 1:40-42, Andrew introduced his family member to Jesus , “He first found his own brother Simon, and said to him, "We have found the Messiah" (which is translated, the Christ). And he brought him to Jesus…” The big question is, “How many members of your family have you brought to Jesus? Joshua declared, “… But as for me and my house, we will serve the Lord" (Joshua 24:15).

II. Evangelism within the Church/Missions Outreach

The Great Commission by the Master demands, “Go therefore and make disciples of all the nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all things that I have commanded you; and lo, I am with you always, even to the end of the age" Amen. (Matt 28:19-20)

The church has the divine mandate to reach the unreached and to perfect the saints. Different means and methods are employed in evangelism.

1. Mass Evangelism: This involves a large scale means of evangelism. It includes open air crusade e.g. AWAKE programme, Holy Ghost Refreshing Vigil, Prayer or Musical Concert.

2. Medical Outreach: Where free medical services are provided for people. Doctors are made available to render free consultation services, free drugs are provided, sometimes free simple tests are conducted (e.g. blood pressure, sugar level, blood group, eye test etc.), free recommended glasses could be supplied.

3. Give them food to eat: Sometimes called “Full Gospel” or “Stomach Infrastructure”. Free food is provided; food stuff and edible provisions are given out.

4. Social Welfare: It includes community services, humanitarian services, sponsorship programme, financial aids/assistance, Educational Trust Fund (ETF), empowerment training, etc., meant to add value to people’s lives are used as means of attracting people to the Kingdom of God.

5. External Ministries: It involves outreaches to places outside the church wall. The idea is to take the gospel to the people where they are. This requires some understanding and training to engage in these ministries/mission, e.g. Hospital Ministry, Prison Ministry, Schools & Colleges, Orphanages, Old People’s Homes, Foster Homes, Welfare Centres (Matt. 25:31-46).

6. One on One Evangelism: It involves reaching out to people one on one. It allows for personal touch, freedom of expression and feelings. The Master Himself introduced this method when He sent His disciples out (Matt. 10:1; Luke 9:1-2). “After these things, the Lord appointed seventy others also, and sent them two by two before His face into every city and place where He Himself was about to go” (Luke 10:1).

7. Special Programme: Special evangelistic or revival programme could be organized in the church that appeals to special need, attention or attraction of the community. E.g. Traditional Sunday, Unusual Sunday, Revival Programme, Special Healing Service, Guest Sunday, Empowerment Programme, Mission Sunday.

Conclusion & Life Application

Every believer is called to preach the gospel. The Great Commission is a commandment for all to go and not a suggestion. God has given man (not angels) the mandate and responsibility to populate His kingdom here on earth through evangelism. Every believer must have an obsession for evangelism. It must fill your heart constantly. Think it, talk it and act it. It should be our heart beat as it is of God’s. We must seize every opportunity to share the gospel. We are saved to do the ministry of reconciliation (Eph. 4:11-13; 2 Cor. 5: 18-20).

The will of God is for all men to be saved [John 3:16; Rom 5:8; 1Tim 2:1-4; 2Pet. 3:8-9]. The focus of the Great Commission is to bring souls to the kingdom of God, “... but I chose you and appointed you that you should go and bear fruit, and that your fruit should remain ...” (John 15:16). For our fruit to remain, evangelism, witnessing, visitation and follow-up must go hand-in-hand.

Lesson 19

The Great Commission

The Great Commission is a mandate for all Christians.

Memory Verse: Matthew 28:18-20

And Jesus came and spoke to them, saying, "All authority has been given to Me in heaven and on earth. Amen.

II. The Great Outpouring.

I. The Great Mandate;

The mandate we received is from the Lord. It was given at the twilight of His ascension. It is therefore a very important message that must not be ignored. The task of preaching the gospel cannot be delegated. The church must live to the expectation of the Lord. However, there are some important aspects of this mandate that must be addressed.

The task is to reach out to the entire world bringing the light of the gospel through effective evangelism. We need to be strategic as soldiers of the Lord. Before the soldiers of this world would prosecute any war, they would naturally plan, delineate and map out areas based on accessibility and other key factors. They will also consider available resources that may enhance performance on the battle field. It is also imperative for the church as soldiers of Christ to strategize, delineate and consider the resource at our disposal. The work can be categorized into two at the first instance which may include the areas of immediate influence i.e our communities, schools, workplace and neighbourhood. The second category may include places outside our areas of immediate influence. Today, our evangelical tools and approaches will depend on how we categorize the environment. It may be possible to reach out immediate vicinity with door to door evangelism, crusades and medical missions. However, for areas delineated as outside our immediate influence, we may need to deploy other tools such as media evangelism.

The believers must be ready to start the work in our little corners. Every Christian has a Jerusalem. There is a place God has planted us to exert some measure of influence. This may be our neighbourhood, schools, workplace or communities where we live. Every church also has a Jerusalem. The community where the church is sited is our immediate area of influence. It is necessary to start in our Jerusalem. It is the model for the early church. They started in their Jerusalem but didn’t remain permanently there. The work entails preaching the gospel of Christ by whatever mode that is most effective while ensuring nurture and discipleship of souls won to maturity. The souls must also be encouraged to begin to work for the Master.

C. We are to progressively move from our Jerusalem to other areas (Worldwide):

The work of the church in evangelism is expected to start in our Jerusalem but it must not end there. The early church

started in Jerusalem and attempted to stop and continue in fellowship and breaking of bread but when persecution came, they were scattered abroad preaching the gospel wherever they went (Acts 8:1).

We need to learn our lessons, the work of evangelism for a church must not stop at our Jerusalem. We must ensure it progressively moves from our Jerusalem to our Judea, our Samaria and the uttermost parts of the world. Though it is good to evangelize and disciple souls in our churches and communities, we must also embark on mission work to unreached areas outside our immediate areas of influence. This could be done through mobilization for places that the Lord may be leading us. We can encourage our brethren to be part by giving financial and other resources that will assist the work. We can also be part of the work by groaning and praying consistently with burden for communities that are still under the yoke of the devil to receive the glorious light of the gospel. Finally, we can also be part of mission work by going either on temporary or permanent basis. To go on temporary basis is to set aside time, especially our holidays to travel to Muslim and other unreached fields to encourage brethren on the frontline. However, if we have a leading to go on permanent basis, it means like Paul of old, we have counted all things for loss to fully commit to the work of the Master in the field.

It is a fact that we are not sufficient on our own (John 15:5; II Cor. 3:5). The enormity of the task must never be contemplated with our natural strength. The scriptures counselled that kings should never go to war without considering the resources at their disposal (Luke 14:28) whether it is sufficient to match that of the adversary. Graciously, the Lord has given us sufficient resources that will help us to effectively do the work. The good thing is that our enemy, the devil, does not have strength to stand against what the Lord has graciously given to the church. Let us examine this in details:

Lesson 20

Topic: Celebrating Mothers (Mother’s Day)

Text: Genesis 17-20; Genesis 24-27; 1 Samuel 1-2; Luke 1

Central Truth:

Motherhood is a challenging responsibility but with great reward, therefore celebrate your mother.

Memory Verse: Luke 1:46-48.

Lesson Outline

I. Isaac's mother, Sarah - an older mother

II. Moses' mother, Jochebed - a protective mother

III. Hannah, mother of Samuel - a mother who battled infertility.

IV. Mary, mother of Jesus - the most honoured mother

Lesson Introduction

There is no doubt that the influence of a good mother is an often highlighted aspect in the lives of great Bible characters. Think of Moses, Samuel and Jesus himself, and the importance their mothers had in their upbringing. The spiritual influence of a mother can never be underestimated. So this Mother's Day, take the opportunity to celebrate mothers in line with the following Bible characters. They experienced the same problems mothers do today.

Lesson Commentary

I. Isaac’s Mother, Sarah – An Older Mother (Gen. 17-20).

Sarah was Abraham’s wife and mother of Isaac. Her name was originally “Sarai” but was changed to Sarah by God, who also changed her husband’s name, “Abram” to Abraham (father of many nations). She was half-sister of Abraham who was ten years her senior. She was about sixty-five (65) years old when she left Haran with Abraham (Gen. 12:5, 17:7). Despite the promise of God that Abraham would become the father of a chosen nation, Sarah was barren. At the age of seventy-five (75) she decided to offer her Egyptian maidservant, Hagar to her husband, by whom she could have a child. Truly, Hagar bore a son named Ishmael but that was not the plan of God (Gen. 16).

However, God visited her at the age of ninety (90), when she thought she was far beyond childbearing age, and she gave birth to a son, Isaac, the child of promise (Gen. 21:1-7). Isaac means laughter. Sarah, who had been mocked for many years as a barren woman, now had a cause to laugh. Prophet Isaiah declared Abraham and Sarah as the father and mother of the Hebrew people (Is. 51:2). Apostle Paul pointed to the faith of Abraham despite the deadness of Sarah’s womb (Rom. 4:19, 9:9). Apostle Peter pointed out the qualities of Sarah an as example of holy women who trusted God, possessed inward spiritual beauty, and were submissive to their husbands (1 Pet. 3:5-6). She was listed in the book of Hebrews as one of the qualified heroines in the roll call of the faithful (Heb. 11:11).

A mature and experienced woman. she had the testimony of being a submissive wife. This must have accounted for the upbringing of Isaac and the members of her household. God even said concerning Abraham, “Seeing that Abraham shall surely become a great and mighty nation, and all the nations of the earth shall be blessed in him? For I know him, that he will command his children and his household after him, and they shall keep the way of the Lord, to do justice and judgment; that the Lord may bring upon Abraham that which he hath spoken of him.” ( Gen 18:18-19). As the saying, “behind a successful man, there is a woman”. Hence, Sarah is obviously a co-beneficiary of this Divine commendation.

II. Moses’ Mother, Jochebed – A Protective Mother (Gen. 24-27).

Jochebed, the mother of Miriam, Aaron and Moses was a daughter of Levi and married from the same tribe of Levi to Amram. She gave birth to Moses when Pharaoh, the king of Egypt, made a decree that every male child born by the Hebrews should be killed. She initially hid him for three months to protect Moses from being killed. She later placed him in an ark of bulrushes on the (bank of Nile) river. When Pharaoh’s daughter came to bathe in the river, she discovered the baby. She was attracted by the cry of the baby and her feminine compassion was aroused. She eventually rescued the baby. By divine orchestration, Jochebed (Mother of Moses) became his paid nurse.

Jochebed was noted among the heroines of faith (Heb. 11:23). Her name means, “Jehovah is honour” or “Jehovah is her glory”. God actually covered her with His glory and honoured her faith in keeping her son, Moses, alive. The courage and faith of this tender and protective mother in exhibiting bravery despite the dangerous consequences of Pharaoh’s edict (Exodus 2:1-10), was alluded to in Hebrews 11:23. Her faith and courage were significantly rewarded by divine providence, to honour her pious example and precepts. The future lawgiver doubtless owed her much of that integrity which so eminently characterized him.

III. Hannah, Mother of Samuel – A Mother Who Battled Infertility (1 Sam. 1-2 ).

Hannah was a wife of Elkanah, a Levite of the Kohathite branch of the priesthood (1 Sam 1:1-2:21). She was regarded as a barren woman because she was unable to bear children. Hannah suffered ridicule from Elkanah's second wife, Peninnah, who bore the priest several children. She was so depressed that during the feast in Shiloh, she prayed fervently to God and vowed that if she were to give birth to a son, she would devote him to the Lord's service. The Lord answered her prayers, and she gave birth to Prophet Samuel.

Hannah means “Favour” or “Gracious”. She was faithful to her promise by making what must have been a heart-rending sacrifice. Hannah took Samuel to the house of the LORD in Shiloh after he was weaned, there to "remain forever" (1 Sam. 1:21). However, God rewarded her faithfulness with three more sons and two daughters. Hannah's beautiful thanksgiving psalm (Ps. 2:1-10) is similar to the song of Mary when she learned she would be the mother of Jesus (Luke 1:46-55).

Samuel and his parents parted. He was left at Shiloh to minister "before the Lord." And each year, when the parents came up to Shiloh, Hannah brought him "a little coat" (which denotes priestly robe, a long upper tunic (Ex 28:31, 1Chron 15:27), in which to minister in the tabernacle (1 Sam 2:19; 15:27; Job 2:12). The scripture says, "And the child Samuel grew before the Lord." (1 Sam. 2:21, 26).

IV. Mary, mother of Jesus – the most honored mother (Luke 1).

Mary gained much popularity as the mother of Jesus (Luke 1-2). Nothing was known of Mary's background other than that she was a peasant and a resident of Nazareth, a city of Galilee. She was from the tribe of Judah and probably in the line of David (Luke 1:32). Although the genealogies in Matthew chapter 1 and Luke chapter 3 do not say so, because they trace Joseph's genealogy rather than Mary's. We do know also that Mary's cousin, Elizabeth, was the mother of John the Baptist.

The angel Gabriel appeared to her, calling her "highly favoured one" and "blessed...among women". Mary was pledged to be married to Joseph the carpenter at that time. (Luke 1:28). She was greatly surprised when the angel announced that she would give birth to a son who would be called the Messiah. After Gabriel explained how such a thing could be possible, Mary said, "Let it be to me according to your word" (Luke 1:38). That Mary "found favour with God" and was allowed to give birth to His child indicates she must have been of high character and full of faith.

When Jesus was born in Bethlehem of Judea, Mary "wrapped him in swaddling cloths, and laid Him in a manger" (Luke 2:7). By divine providence, Mary witnessed the visits of the shepherds and the wise men and "pondered them in her heart" (Luke 2:19). During the dedication of the baby Jesus in the temple, Simeon (an old man waiting for the consolation of Israel) made a prophecy of a sword that would pierce through Mary’s soul (Luke 2:35). The birth of Jesus became a threat to Herod and his kingdom. So, Joseph and Mary fled to Egypt to escape Herod's decree to murder all male children under two years old (Matt 2:13-18).

Mary was present at Jesus' first open miracle at the wedding ceremony in Cana of Galilee – where He turned water into wine (John 2:1-12). Mary seemed to be asking her Son to use His power to meet the crisis. We see Mary again at the foot of the cross (John 19:25-27). No mention is made of Joseph; he had likely been dead for some time. Mary must have held fast to her belief in His messiahship -even though it appeared to be ending in tragedy. While on the cross, Jesus gave Mary over to the care of the beloved disciple, John. The last mention of Mary is in the upper room in Jerusalem, with the one hundred and twenty (120) disciples waiting for the coming of the promised Holy Spirit (Acts 1:14). We do not know how or when Mary died. Her tomb is believed to be in the Valley of Kidron in Jerusalem, southeast of the Temple area.

Conclusion & Life Application

The impact of mothers over their children cannot be over emphasized. Their role is significant in the lives of their children. As home makers and role models, mothers have great influence on their children, who pick a lot from them; hence the adage, “like mother, like daughter”. The Bible admonishes the proper training of children as the heritage of God. Sarah was a model of humility and a submissive wife. Isaac must have learned respect and obedience from her. Moses was raised by his mother, who happened to be his nurse. Moses maintained composure in pharaoh’s palace that he did not betray the confidence reposed on him. He grew up to understand his divine mission as deliverer of the Jews from the captivity in Egypt. His faith and vision must have been influenced by his mother. Samuel’s destiny must have been sharpened by this mother’s faith and devotion to the living God. Mary was regarded as the most honoured mother because of her faith, trust and confidence in God. She pondered all prophetic declarations concerning her and the Messiah in her heart. God gives much honour to women; He demands their husbands to nourish and cherish them; to handle them with care and understanding. He also commands children to honour their parents with the promise of long life if they obey.

2. What impact is your mother having in your life?

3. Discuss the role of mothers in the lives of their children with case studies of people around you.

Lesson 21

Topic: Giving: Tithing and Offerings

Text: Genesis 14:17_15:1; Matt. 25:14-30; Philip. 4:14 -18

Supplemental References: Deut. 15:7-8, 1Chron. 29:2-9, 14

Central Truth: No one can o ut-give God

Memory Verse: Luke 6:38

Lesson Outline:

I. Abraham the Giver

II. Tithes and Offerings in the Old Testament

III. Tithes and Offerings in the New Testament.

Lesson Introduction

We believe that tithing (giving 10 percent of earnings) and offerings [amount determined by the individuals] are God’s instruments to carry on His ministry, spread the gospel, and release personal blessing (Malachi 3:10, 1Corinthians 16:1-2).

Lesson Commentary:

Giving is the act of presenting or bestowing an item on someone as a favour. This is done on numerous occasions and for a variety of purposes. For instance, Daniel 2:48 tells us that giving is a form of reward for faithful service and a motivation to ensure future loyalty. Esther 9:22 also shows that giving is a form of relief for the poor, bearing in mind that the poor will never cease from the land (Deut. 15:11). Giving is also to support God’s work. This kind of free will giving often results in joy for the giver. Giving is scriptural- it is a command for all believers. Thus, by giving, we are being obedient to our God, who gives us richly all things to enjoy (1Thomthy 6:17). By giving, we are also demonstrating that all things come from God, and we are giving back what belongs to Him (1Chron. 29:14). What is being said, in other words, is that by giving, we are just returning our resources to their rightful owner- God Himself.

I. Abraham the Giver (Gen. 14:17, 15:1)

Abraham learnt that his nephew Lot had been captured with all his possessions. He therefore mobilized his three hundred and eighteen (318) men to rescue Lot. Upon his victorious return, the King of Sodom paid Abraham respect for his victory over Chedorlaomer and the kings with him. Melchizedek, king of Salem also brought bread and wine for the refreshment of Abraham and his solders.

For all encomiums showered on Abraham, so to say, Abraham gave Melchizedek TITHES of all the spoils of the war. This action of Abraham demonstrated a unique act of gratitude to the king of Salem, the Priest of God Most High. The import of this must not go unstressed. This is to teach us that those who receive kindness must also show kindness. For, it is said that gratitude is one of nature’s laws. Thus, Abraham’s action shows tithe as an offering vowed and dedicated to the Most High God, which was subsequently put in the hands of Melchizedek His priest.

II. Tithes and Offerings in the Old Testament (Lev. 27:26-34, Deut. 12:1-18, Mal. 3:7-12)

1. Meaning: Tithe means tenth part of one’s income offered to God for church purpose. For instance, in the Old Testament, Abraham presented a tithe of war booty to the Priest-King of Salem, Melchizedek (Gen. 14:18-20).

Offering, on the other hand, has diverse meanings, some of which will be briefly examined here:

a) It is used in the sense of a gift, which is given by one person to another. (Gen. 32:13-15). The story of Cain and Abel in Gen. 4:3-5 is also apposite.

b) It is also used in the sense of tribute, paid to a king or an over-lord. (2Sam. 8:2).

c) It is equally used to designate “contributions” to God, but which were set apart specifically for priests (Nos. 5:9).

d) It is used in the sense of “contributions”_ also referred to as “Heave offerings” or gifts which consisted of produce of the ground, thus reflecting the agricultural character of early Israel (Deut. 12:1-18).

e) It is used in the sense of a special ‘levy’ ordained by God. (Ex. 25:3-9, Ex. 35:5-8).

f) Finally, it is also construed as a ‘tax’ -an obligatory assessment of every Israelite male of 20 years old and above. (Ex. 30:11-16). This is meant to provide financial support for the sanctuary.

2. Command to Give: Tithes and offerings were ordained and ordered to be given by God. This is why God designated a specific place to which the Israelites should bring all their offerings (Deut. 12:10-11). Their tithes, and heave offerings of their hands e.g. their first fruits, their vows, and free-will offerings, whether to be used by themselves or by the priests and Levites, must be brought to the place which God would choose. We are, by this, also taught to honour the Lord with our substance (Prov. 3:9-10).

3. Purpose/Uses:

a) It is to honour God as the great provider.

b) It is to support and maintain His ministry, as seen earlier.

c) It is also to provide financial and material support for the sanctuary and the work of the ministry generally.

d) It is to glorify God.

e) It is to edify the giver in return.

4. Refusal to give and its Effects (Mal. 3:7-9): The book of Malachi tells us of people who refuse to pay tithes and give offerings. The wide ramification of this unashamed robbery are:

a) Robbing God is a heinous crime.

b) God is robbed of His honour.

c) God is robbed of that which was devoted to Him.

d) The robbery also encompasses employment in His service.

e) The robbery includes Sabbath time.

f) It also encompasses that which is for the support of religion.

g) It entails not giving God His dues out of their estates.

h) The robbery entails denial of priests and Levites the required means of maintaining them and their families.

i) The priests are also defrauded by the robbery, which also denied them of paying their tithes too.

j) The robbery entails not paying the full tithes, or not making full remittances of our resource dedicated to God like the case of Ananias and Sapphire (Acts 5: 1-11)

5. Obedience to give and its Good Effects (Benefit) (Mal. 3:10-12)

The charge to bring in all the tithes has its good effects. These are:

a) God will open the windows of heaven, as He has in His hands the key of the clouds, and there shall be seasonable rain.

This is in line with the words of our Lord Jesus Christ in Acts 20:35, viz “It is more blessed to give than to receive”. Scripture also informs us that our charity and benevolence should be free, that is, without compulsion, and cheerful. Hence, it is aptly stated that God loves a cheerful giver (II Cor. 9:7). This also applies to our tithes and offerings.

It is said that seed time and harvest time shall never cease in the land (Gen. 8:22). Therefore, we should give unto the Lord God with all our hearts, as and when due. By so doing, we are merely returning our resources to their rightful owner, God Himself. This will make our heavenly Father glad, because it demonstrates our act of obedience and loyalty, which is indicative of a life that is right with Him in every way.

The disciples of Jesus Christ also knew that giving is biblical and that it emanates from a desire to advance the work of God’s kingdom, not the intention of men. Tithes and offerings are expressions of our servant-hood to God and His people.

Lesson 22

Topic: Kingdom Champions (Children’s Day)

Text: Exo.20:12; Prov. 22:6; Ephesians 6:1-4

Central Truth: Parents are custodians of God’s Kingdom champions,

so they should handle and train them well.

Memory Verse: Prov. 22:6

Lesson Outline

I. The Law - Necessity For Training

II. How To Train A Child

III. Benefits Of Child Training

Lesson Introduction

As Abraham trained up his children, and those born in his house, in the way of the Lord, in the paths of justice and judgment; which are the ways in which they should go, and which will be to their profit and advantage; so shall all parents do even today. We must train them in the nurture and admonition of the Lord. by praying with them and for them, by bringing them under the means of grace, the ministry of the word, by instructing them in the principles of religion, teaching them their duty to God and man, and setting them good examples of a holy life and conversation; and this is to be done according to their capacity, and as they are able to understand and receive the instructions given them.

Lesson Commentary

I. The law - necessity for training

In Exodus 20, God delivered ten (10) basic moral laws to Moses for the children of Israel. The laws on the first tablet focus on human relationship with God. The laws on the second tablet focus on our relationship with each other. The fifth law, the first in the second tablet, declares, "Honour your father and your mother, that your days may be long upon the land which the Lord your God is giving you.” (Ex 20:12)

Law is one of the most important biblical concepts. But its nature and purpose must be clearly grasped. Law reveals the character and the standards of God, and marks the way in which human beings can express love for God and for each other. Furthermore, the Law reveals something of the righteousness of God. Exodus 20:12 commands children to obey their father and mother which indicates that respect of parents leads to knowing God. Disobedience to parents is contravention of the commandment of God which leads to early death.

To help the child to obey the laws of God, he must be properly trained by the parents. “Train up a child in the way he should go: and when he is old, he will not depart from it.” (Prov. 22:6) This is a divine commandment and not a suggestion. Furthermore, the parents are admonished, “And, ye fathers, provoke not your children to wrath: but bring them up in the nurture and admonition of the Lord.” (Eph. 6:4).

II. How To Train A Child (Prov. 22:6)

The word "train" is from “Hanak” meaning setting aside, narrowing, or hedging in. Child-training involves "narrowing" a child's conduct away from evil and towards godliness and starting him in the right direction. That is, "according to" or "in accordance with". The "way" means proper way, the path of wise, godly living, which is emphasized frequently in the book of Proverbs - basically the way of wisdom. It is from this proper behavioural pattern or godly lifestyle that he will not turn away from when he is old, that is, when he is grown (attains adulthood). Give necessary instructions, directions and corrections, “The rod and reproof give wisdom: but a child left to himself bringeth his mother to shame.” (Prov. 29:15)

It has been observed, that most children who are brought up in Christian homes, under the influence of godly parents who teach and live by God's standards, follow that training. Therefore, a great duty and responsibility is laid on parents and instructors of children to propagate wisdom and righteous living. Parents are to train up children in this age of vanity, to keep them from the sins and snares of it and to prepare them for what they are designed for. They are to keep them under discipline. Train them as soldiers of the Lord; having faith and confidence in God, to keep rank, and to observe God’s word of command. Train them up, not in the way they would like to go (the bias of their corrupt hearts would draw them aside), but in the way they should go, the way in which, if you love them, you would have them go (Deut. 6:7). The good impressions made upon them will abide with them all their days.

III. Benefits of Child Training

Obedience to God’s commandment has divine promises attached to it, “… that your days may be long upon the land which the Lord your God is giving you.” (Ex 20:12). Obedience to God’s commandments brought the children of Israel out of the land of bondage and their continuous obedience was to keep them safe in the Promised Land. If they did not conduct themselves well, their days would be shortened in that land, both the days of particular persons (who committed offence and the days of their nation (which should be removed out of it). Those that do their duty to their parents are most likely to have the comfort of that which their parents gather for them and leave to them. Those that support their parents shall find that God, the Father of all, will support them. Those who, in conscience towards God, keep this and the rest of God's commandments, may be sure that it shall be well with them, and that they shall live long on earth. This promise is expounded in Ephesians 6:3, “That it may be well with thee, and thou mayest live long on the earth.” Long life in that good land is promised particularly to obedient children.

Training up children in that age of vanity, keeps them from the sins and snares of this world. It prepares and equips them for decent living. It prepares them for what they are designed for and helps in achieving divine purpose. It initiates them into the ways of God and keeps them under discipline. It helps them to know God and walk in His ways (Ps. 119:9-11). It builds up decency, and Christian virtues that enable them live in harmonious relationship in the society.

Conclusion & Life Application:

The laws and commandments of God are meant to bless, benefit and profit us if we obey them. Our relationships would be cordial and produce peaceful coexistence in the society. The fifth commandment concerns the duties we owe to our relations; those of children to their parents specified: Honour thy father and thy mother, which includes, decent respect to their persons (Lev 19:3), giving them reverence (Heb. 12:9), and obedience to their lawful commands (Eph. 6:1-3). Submission to their rebukes, instructions, and corrections; Disposing of themselves with the advice, direction, and consent, of parents; Endeavouring, in everything, to be the comfort of their parents, and to make their old age easy for them, maintaining them if they stand in need of support (Matt 15:4-6).

Lesson 23

Topic: The Meaning of Pentecost (Pentecost Sunday)

Text: Acts 2:1-13

Central Truth:

Pentecost is God’s equipping His church with the power of His Spirit so that He will be glorified among the nations.

Memory Verse: Acts 2:4

Lesson Outline

I. The Old Testament Concept

II. The New testament Concept

Introduction

Pentecost is one of the prominent feasts in the Christian liturgical year commemorating the descent of the Holy Spirit upon the disciples of Christ after the Resurrection. The feast is also called Whitsunday especially in the United Kingdom. Pentecost is celebrated seven weeks (50 days) after Easter Sunday, hence its name.

Lesson Commentary

I. The Old Testament Concept (Feast of Harvest/ Weeks)

Feasts and Festivals were regular assemblies of the people of Israel for the worship of the Lord. They were scheduled at specific times in the annual calendar and they were both civil and religious in nature. Some marked the beginning or the end of the agricultural year, while others commemorated historic events in the life of the nation. All of the feasts were marked by thanksgiving and joyous feasting.

Feast of Weeks, also called the Feast of Harvest or Pentecost, was observed by the Jews early in the third month on the 50th day after the offering of the barley sheaf at the Feast of Unleavened Bread (Passover). It included a holy convocation. Leviticus 23:15-17 says, “And you shall count for yourselves from the day after the Sabbath, from the day that you brought the sheaf of the wave offering: seven Sabbaths shall be completed. Count fifty days to the day after the seventh Sabbath; then you shall offer a new grain offering to the Lord.” Numbers 28:26-31. It prescribes the number and nature of the offering during the festival while Deuteronomy 16:9-12 describes those who were to be invited to this feast.

II. The New Testament Concept (The Birth of the Church)

After the death, burial, resurrection and post-resurrection appearances of Jesus, the disciples were gathered in an upper room because of the fear of the Jews, expecting the promised Holy Spirit. On the day of Pentecost, while the Jews were observing the Feast of Weeks, the Holy Spirit came upon them as a rushing mighty wind (Acts 2:1-2).

Pentecost was a harvest festival when the first fruits of the grain harvest were presented to God (Lev. 23:17). However, on this particular day, about one hundred and twenty (120) disciples of Jesus gathered in one accord in the upper room, “suddenly there came a sound from heaven as of a rushing mighty wind, and it filled all the house where they were sitting. And there appeared unto them cloven tongues like as of fire, and it sat upon each of them. And they were all filled with the Holy Ghost, and began to speak with other tongues, as the Spirit gave them utterance.” (Acts 2:2-4)

This event marked the beginning of the church - fulfilling the words of Jesus. (Acts 1:8, Matt. 16:18-19). The church constituted the body of Christ by means of the Spirit’s baptism (1Cor 12:13). A day set aside for the presentation of the first fruit of crops, turned to a day of harvest of souls (first fruit of the Spirit), “Then they that gladly received his word were baptized: and the same day there were added unto them about three thousand souls.” (Acts 2:41)

The Day of Pentecost:

1. There was occurrence of divine influence. The presence of the third person in Godhead, the Holy Spirit became manifest. The disciples spoke in other tongues and there was repentance and salvation of souls (Acts 2).

2. There was confirmation of divine mission. The disciples were endued with power from God as promised by Jesus (Acts 1:8). They received power, unction and boldness to witness Christ. The first message resulted in three thousand converts.

3. There was endowment of power to overcome opposition. The disciple that locked up themselves because of fear now became bold to preach the Gospel. The presence of the Holy Spirit announced and exposed them.

4. The supremacy of Christ and the Gospel was exhibited. The message of Peter brought conviction upon the Jews, “Now when they heard this, they were pricked in their heart, and said unto Peter and to the rest of the apostles, Men and brethren, what shall we do?” (Acts 2:37)

5. The gift of diverse tongues followed the baptism of the Holy Spirit. People from different nations heard them, “Now when this was noised abroad, the multitude came together, and were confounded, because that every man heard them speak in his own language.” (Acts 2:6) Speaking in tongues is a mystery, “…, we do hear them speak in our tongues the wonderful works of God. Acts 2:11

Conclusion & Life Application

The day of Pentecost was an annual feast that followed the Feast of First-fruits (i.e., seven weeks, or 49 days) and therefore, was called the Feast of Weeks (Lev 23:15-22). The name "Pentecost" (of Greek derivation), means 50 because it was the 50th day after the First-fruits Feast (Lev 23:16).

For God to choose the Day of Pentecost in sending the Holy Spirit to inaugurate the Church must have been very significant. We may wonder why the gift of the Spirit delayed until the day of Pentecost was fully come? No man must irreverently pry into the purposes of Deity, it proves the supremacy of God.

Pentecost was the feast of first-fruits; symbolically, the first-fruits of the Christian church (Lev 23:15,17; Deut. 16:9). The first sheaf of the Christian harvest, the first fruit of the Christian reaping souls into God’s kingdom. Our commission is to harvest souls (Matt. 28:18-20).

Conviction of sin is the prominent idea of the apostolic Pentecost. Peter's sermon resulted in the cry, "Brethren, what shall we do?" Conviction of sin is the prelude to a reformed life.

The first gift of the Holy Spirit on the day of Pentecost -- the day which, in Jewish thought, was specially consecrated to the giving of the law from Sinai -- was specially fitted to the mission of convicting the world in respect of sin (John 16:8).

The first-fruits on the day of Pentecost are typical of the ingathering of all nations to Christ. More foreign Jews attended the Pentecost than any other Jewish feast. In the light of this we are to take the Gospel round the globe.

Teaching Overview/Insight

The Jews observed diverse Feasts & Festivals. — There were daily (Lev 23), weekly, monthly, and yearly festivals, and great stress was laid on the regular observance of them in every particular pattern (Num. 28:1-8; Ex 29:38-42; Lev 6:8-23; Ex 30:7-9; 27:20). On each of these occasions, every male Israelite was commanded "to appear before the Lord" (Deut. 27:7; Neh. 8:9-12). The attendance of women was voluntary (Luke 2:41; 1 Sam 1:7; 2:19).

These festivals, besides their religious purposes, had an important bearing on the maintenance among the people of the feeling of a national unity. The times fixed for their observance were arranged so as to interfere as little as possible with the industry of the people. The Passover was kept just before the harvest commenced, Pentecost at the conclusion of the corn harvest and, the feast of Tabernacles after all the fruits of the ground had been gathered in.

On this significant day of Pentecost, God chooses to baptize His People with the Holy Spirit. We should also note that the filling with the Holy Spirit is separate from the baptism of the Spirit. The Spirit's baptism occurs once for each believer at the moment of salvation (John 11:15-16, Rom 6:3; 1 Cor 12:13; Col 2:12), but the Spirit's filling may occur not only at salvation but also on a number of occasions after salvation (Acts 4:8,31; 6:3,5; 7:55; 9:17; 13:9,52). An evidence of the baptism of the Holy Spirit was speaking in other tongues/languages (Acts 11:15-16).

Lesson 24

Topic: Giving: Time and Talents

Text: Gen. 14:17, 15:1, Matt. 25:14-30, Philip. 4:14 -18

Supplemental References: Deut. 8

Central Truth:

Every giving must be done in the spirit of a willing, gracious and generous expression of love towards God, the giver of life.

Memory Verse: Deut. 8:18

Lesson Outline:

I. The True Test of Giving

II. Why Should Christians Give?

III. What should Christians Give

Lesson Introduction

Jesus Christ created us. He bought us with His precious blood. And God anointed Him as our Lord. Therefore, our whole life, our personality, influence, material substance, our achievement and everything belongs to Him. We should therefore acknowledge Him as the absolute owner of all we are and have.

Lesson Commentary:

God is the giver of all things, and as such nobody can out-give Him. Whatsoever we give is just a token-very infinitesimal-out of the abundance He has given us, no matter how large the sum of money- be it in dollars, pound sterling, gold or silver or diamond and no matter how huge the quantity of materials. By giving, we are merely being appreciative of His bountiful and unquantifiable generosity, proving that we are not ungrateful. For of His own, we are giving (I Chron. 29:14).

I. The True Test Of Giving (Matt. 25:14-30)

The true test of giving is illustrated by the parable of the talents, given to three servants by their Master, shortly before departing on a journey.

A. Meaning or importance of the parable

First, the parable implies a state of business or work as well as a state of expectancy.

Second, the Master here is our Lord Jesus Christ, “the absolute owner and proprietor of all persons and things.”

Third, the servant here implies Christians, employed in the Master’s (Christ’s) work.

Fourth, the Master committed some trust to the servants having employed them to work for Him.

Fifth, the servants received the talents from their Master, which implies a readiness to work for Him.

Sixth, the talents were given to each servant in proportion to the overall ability of each servant.

B. Rendering Account of Stewardship- “Different Performance Levels”:

Two of the Servants Did Well: Upon His return, the Master demanded accountability from His servants.

Reading through these verses, it is manifestly clear that two of the servants did well. They were diligent and faithful. They went and traded with the money, thus putting the money into intended use and made returns on it.

The Third Servant: The third servant was unfaithful and indolent, as well as unenterprising. Rather than trade with what he had, he dug the earth and hid the lone talent given him, for fear of being stolen. He was full of untenable excuses for his crass laziness. This is equally applicable to spiritual gifts. Many have them, but don’t make use of them for the purpose for which they are given.

C. Rewards Meted Out To the Three Servants:

The first two servants who traded and made profits with their talents were commended and rewarded for their diligence and integrity. Thus, if we do that which is good, and do it well, we shall be praised.

The third servant’s account was found to be bad. The lesson for us here is that the smallness of our receiving will not excuse us from reckoning. His excuses and apologies did not stand him in good stead. So it is with those of us who despise little beginnings and small opportunities (Zech. 4:10).

II. Why Should Christians Give? (2Corin. 8:1-15, 9:6-8, 10-20, Matt. 6;19-21)

1. Divine Instruction to Give:

A Christian is obliged to give, because “true Christianity is subject to the gospel, yielding ourselves to the commanding influence of its truths and law.” Hence, a true Christian is expected to demonstrate the sincerity of his subjection to the gospel by works of charity (Deut. 5:11; Matt. 26:11).

2. Encouragement to Give:

In our reference text for this topic, Apostle Paul used several cogent arguments to stir up the Corinthians to the ministry of giving.

The Corinthians were commended for their faith and diligence and love for their ministers. In his commendation, Paul urged them to crave the grace to abound in giving to the poor (II Cor. 8:10).

Manner of Acceptable Giving (II Cor. 9:6-8) :

For our giving to be acceptable, Apostle Paul admonished that such giving must be as commanded. That is, our giving should be bountiful. It should be given deliberately, as consciously proposed in the heart of the giver. It should not be given hastily, not with compulsion..

We are also enjoined to give freely, and not grudgingly, but cheerfully, as God loves a cheerful giver (II Cor. 9:7). The giving should also be liberal with an open hand and open mind and with an open countenance to demonstrate gladness for the ability and opportunity to be charitable.

Benefits of Giving :

Paul noted that givers would not be losers by what they give. He stated further that what is given to the poor in a right manner-freely and liberally- is far from being lost, as the giver will reap it with increase, with good returns.

Acceptable giving is far from impoverishing the giver, rather, it is a veritable means of godly enrichment-truly rich indeed. Therefore, true Christians should not be weary of giving, as giving will rebound to the praise and glory of God (Phil. 4:19; Ps. 112:9).

III. What Should Christians Give?

We should give the following among others.

A. Treasures; Tithes and Offerings:

These are the three areas specifically identified for Christians to give. These areas have been extensively treated in lesson 21 where it was pointed out that Abraham set the ball rolling by giving ten percent of all the spoils of the war.

B. Time and Talent:

In addition to treasures, tithes and offerings, Christians are also to give their precious time and their inalienable talents. In giving our time for God’s work. We must bear in mind that time is an invaluable resource at the disposal of all and sundry, equally and without discrimination. None has monopoly of it, because God is Lord over time. He created and ordained it (Gen. 1:4,5,14-19). Therefore, it is incumbent on all Christians to be conscious of the fact that some times are divinely appointed, especially in carrying out God’s will for a specific mission or assignment at a specific time (Acts 17:26).

Talent, on the other hand, is any natural or special gift, or special aptitude, according to a dictionary definition. It is God-given, and as such, should be used to serve God as a pay-back. Thus, Christians need to consciously identify their respective talents, such as singing, dancing, drumming, acting, teaching etc., which they can give to God by way of joining the respective arms in their churches

Conclusion & Life Application

Pleasing God is to be the first purpose of our life. Therefore, our faithful giving, as well as our full tithes and generous offerings should be uppermost in our mind as a veritable avenue to please God. This was exemplified by the two servants, who faithfully traded with the talents given them by their Master-and made profits when He was away. The third servant, who buried his lone talent in the ground, had nothing to show for it. Thus, while the other two pleased their Master and won accolades for their faithfulness and diligence, the third lazy one incurred the displeasure and wrath of his Master. What he had was even taken away from him and given to his industrious colleagues. This brings about the dictum “use it or lose it”. To win the reward of giving, we should be honest with it, and give as and when due, with open minds. Our giving is not confined to our treasures, tithes and offerings, but is inclusive of our invaluable time and our unique abilities and capabilities. We should, therefore, try to have the same estimate/evaluation of such to enable us put them to good use.

Questions & Discussion

1. Why should a Christian give and what should be the components of the giving?

2a. When it comes to giving, some Christians murmur and grumble about “donor fatigue”. What could be the basis for this?

2b. What are the characteristics of acceptable and unacceptable giving?

Teaching Overview/Insight

The Christian life is a purpose driven life, with serving and giving as its twin neighbours. Whenever we give, we are actually serving God. We are blessed to be a blessing to others around us. We are to strive to put to best use our God-given abilities, i.e. talents, Our motto for this purpose- driven life should be: “Do all the good you can, by all the means you can, in all the ways you can, in all the places you can, at all the times you can, to all the people you can, as long as you ever can” (Source: Rick Warren: Purpose Driven Life, 2002, page 259).

For who can tell a man what will happen to him after under the sun? (Eccl. 6:12) In peroration, our giving should be anchored on the diction “God deserves our best”

Lesson Introduction

Professor, J.I. Parker (a British-born Canadian Christian theologian) said that fathers need to understand how much they lack the knowledge of God. They must learn to measure themselves, not by their knowledge about God, not by how many verses they can spit out by memory, not by their gifts or talents or ministry, but by how they pray, by how they commune with the Lord in His Word, by what goes on in their hearts, and by their level of obedience to what they know. These will help fathers to see their responsibilities as God sees them. Though enormous, but God’s grace will make them succeed if they try.

Lesson Commentary

I. The Father

A father is a male parent. In Bible times, a father was the male parent of a household, charged with the responsibility of providing for the family and giving religious instructions to the children. The social structure described in the Old Testament is known as a "patriarchal" society. The word patriarchy means "the rule of the father." The father commanded a high position in the family; his word was law. The Hebrew word translated into English as husband actually means "lord," "master," "owner," or "possessor" (Gen 18:12; Hos. 2:16).

II. The Role of The Father

As the head of the family, the father was expected to assume certain responsibilities. These responsibilities can be classified into three categories: spiritual, social, and economic.

A. Spiritual Responsibility

He was responsible for the spiritual well-being of the family, as well as the individual members of the family. Furthermore, the father functioned as the priest of his family, sacrificing on their behalf (Gen 12:8; Job 1:5). Later, when a priesthood was established in Israel and the layman no longer functioned at an altar, the father's spiritual role was redefined. He continued to be the religious leader in the home. This involved the training of the children in godliness (Ex 12:3,26-27; Prov 22:6; Eph 6:4).

B. Social Responsibility

The father was responsible for the protection of his family. He was to see that no one took advantage of any member of his family. Those who were not protected by a father were truly disadvantaged persons. The two most common categories of "fatherless" people were widows and orphans. The father’s specific responsibilities according to Jewish tradition were; to have his son circumcised; to pass on his inheritance to his firstborn son; to find his son a wife; and to teach him a trade. A father is responsible for the social well-being of his family.

C. Economic Responsibility

The father was to provide for the needs of the various members of his family. A lazy man who failed to provide for his family would be mocked, shaming him to do what was expected of him (Prov. 6:6-11). Even in the New Testament context, apostle Paul rebuked those who considered themselves Christian but who did not look after the needs of their families (1 Tim. 5:8).

We need to fully understand and appreciate the concept of having God as the believer's Father. He knows all about us, even numbering the hairs on our head (Matt 10:30). He protects His child and rescues him when he gets into trouble (Isa 63:15-16). He teaches him the way that he should go (Hos. 11:1-3) and supplies all of his needs (Matt 6:33). In turn, the Father expects honour from His children, although He does not always receive it (Mal 1:6).

Conclusion & Life Application

The father has been charged with awesome responsibility of providing, protecting and instructing members of his household. He is the priest of the house, dictating the spiritual tone, development, service and worship of his family. As the breadwinner, he is to provide the needs of his household. Apostle Paul views this with seriousness, “But if anyone does not provide for his own, and especially for those of his household, he has denied the faith and is worse than an unbeliever.” (1 Tim 5:8) He has the responsibility to see to the smooth running of his family. He must teach, instruct, guide and monitor the progress and well-being of every member of his family.

Considering the fatherly role of God to believers, we need to re-examine our own role as fathers today. We need to be alive to our responsibilities to the family. A father should know all about his children, by creating time for them, relating and communicating with them. He should understand their state, problems, needs and challenges. God knows even the numbers of the hairs on our head (Matt 10:30). A father should be the defender of members of his household. God protects His children and rescues them when they get into trouble (Isa 63:15-16). A father should teach his family the way they should go (Prov. 22:6) as the priest in the house and also provide for their needs.

Questions & Discussion

1. Who is a father?

2. What are the attributes of a responsible father?

3. Discuss the fatherly roles of God over the believer.

Lesson 26

Topic: Moderation

Text: Ephesians 4; 14-15

Supplemental References: Phil. 4:5, 1 Corinth. 13:5; 14:33; 14:40; 3:21-23; 9:25-27; 10:23-24, 32-33 Rom. 14:5. Col 3;12-13.

Central Truth:

Every Christian behaviour must be guided by love, discipline and self-control.

Memory Verse : Rom. 14:3-5

Lesson Outline:

I. Moderation of our Attitudes

II. Moderation of our Physical Outlook

III. Moderation of our Actions

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that Christians should avoid excesses and that their moderation should be obvious to others. Their commitment to Christ should be deep and sincere, but they should not indulge in or lead other people into extremes or fanaticism. Christians should be well thought of, balanced, humble and self-sacrificing, reflecting Christ’s grace and character in all areas of their lives (Colossians 3:12-13, Philippians 4:5).

Lesson Commentary

Generally, life is made up of people with different attitudes. On one end, we have people who are carefree while on the other extreme, we have those who do things in excess. God did His work of creation in six days but rested on the seventh day. He gave the same directive to the Israelites. In other words, while God abhors laziness, He also detests the workaholics. Therefore, in our attitude to life and other life engagements, we are expected to apply moderation. It should be noted here that moderation should not be seen only in relation to our dressing, but reflected in the totality of life in Christ. Moderation could be defined as not being excessive, or avoidance of extremes.

I. Moderation of our Attitude to Life:

Godly attitudes are to be guided under moderation. Jesus wants us to avoid being lukewarm believers. Yet, he does not want us to become fanatics who are reckless and unreasonable. The old King James Version of Phil 4:5 says “Let your moderation be known unto all men. The Lord is at hand”. Although the word “moderation” here could also be translated as "gentleness”, the fact is clear that we must keep our attitude in check as Jesus Himself was “gentle and meek” to avoid embarrassing Him. Our attitude must always be befitting unto the Lord in line with life of the Antioch Christians (Act 11:26). To be good ambassadors of Christ, we must always display attitudes of decency and orderliness (1 Cor. 14:40).

II. Moderation in Physical Outlook

Our physical outlook is very important to God. So, it must be moderated appropriately. It is true that we don’t recommend dressing code, yet, a Spirit filled believer should understand the difference between worldly and godly outlooks. This includes the shape of our hair, eyelids, lips, nails and dresses. The bible says, “Everything is permissible but not everything is beneficial. Everything is permissible but not everything is constructive” (1 Cor. 10:23). Apart from specific biblical instructions, believers must also seek the socio-cultural dictates of their environment or community to moderate their conduct. No wonder, the above verse ended with the warning that “nobody should seek his own good, but the good of others.”

III. Moderation of Our Actions:

Believers are to moderate their actions, consciously and otherwise. It is basically instructive that as believers, we must act moderately so as not to “cause anyone to stumble” (1 Cor. 10:32). We are encouraged to seek financial breakthrough, but we must avoid love of money which is the root of all evils (1 Tim. 6:10). We are to be angry but not sin (Eph. 4:26). Also, we are to be quick to listen but slow to speak and slow to become angry (James 1:19). While we are to eat well, we must moderate our appetite to avoid gluttony (Prov. 23:2; 28:7 and Titus 1:2). No matter how much we respect our leaders, we must moderate our reverence for them so that they don't become another idol competing with God in our lives or causing disunity (1 Cor. 3:3-7). Indeed, when we are in the Spirit and prophesying in the fellowship, our actions must be guided by moderation because “the spirits of prophets are subject to the control of prophets” and ”God is not a God of disorder but of peace as in all the congregations of the saints” (1 Cor. 14:33-34).

Conclusion

Moderation must be the guiding principle of any believer who wishes to please the Lord. It should not only be observed in dressing but in our general life engagements.

Life Application

We are to speak but must not become talkative. We are to eat but not become gluttons. We are to sleep but must not become another Eutychus (Act 20:9-10). We must discipline our children but always in moderation. We are to fast but be mindful of our health and age. We are to relate with the opposite sex but moderated by godliness and righteousness. Every believer must ensure moderation in their offices and avoid turning official hours to prayer sessions and evangelism programmes. It must include the level of our involvements in cultural organizations, our speed limits when driving and level of accumulation of financial and material possessions here on earth at the detriment of our eternity.

Questions & Discussion:

1. How can moderation be reflected in our prayer life?

Prayers should be done as led by the spirit of God. However, when our noise is disturbing our roommates or co-tenants, we have to moderate our voice and sound. Also, when our church is causing noise pollution in the community, it should be moderated.

2. Is there any moderation in love?

Yes, our love for people must not compete with that of God to avoid turning them to idols

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

Moderation must be a guiding principle for Christians in all ramifications. The teacher should guide the students to understand that moderation is not just about dressing but about all areas of our existence. It should be observed in the way we talk in and outside the church. It should reflect in the way we pursue our goals and life ambitions. Our physical outlook and social carriage are not excluded. A practical discussion on seemingly unnoticed areas like driving culture, eating habits, social engagements, social media practice, fellowship involvements of students on campuses etc. should be initiated by the teacher.
