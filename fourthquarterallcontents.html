Lesson 40

Topic: Let us Be Patriotic! (Nigerian Independence)

Text: 2 Chron. 7: 1-14; Rom.13:1-7

Central Truth: Righteousness exalts a nation and sin is a reproach.

Memory Verse: 2 Chronicles 7:14

Lesson Outline

I. Being Patriotic! How far?

II. Patriotism! The Christ Followers!

III. Patriotism! Celebrating our Nationhood .

Lesson Introduction:

Should a Christian be patriotic? The answer to this question depends on the meaning of the word “patriotic.” As with many words, there are different nuances of meaning, and different people use the word in different ways. For example, at its simplest meaning being patriotic simply means “loving one’s country.” As long as that love for country does not supersede one’s love for God, and if it is kept in proper perspective, there is nothing wrong with a Christian being patriotic.

However, another definition of “patriotic” implies that the individual should place the interests of the nation above his or her personal and group interests. Carried to this extreme, patriotism can become a form of idolatry, particularly if one’s love for his country is greater than his love for God and God’s plan of redeeming people from “every tribe, tongue and nation.”

1st October every year, we celebrate the founding of our nation, which we can trace to the amalgamation of 1914. We have evolved through many historical phases before we got to this present democratic dispensation of 2016.

My prayer is that our leaders will use this teaching to remind the congregations, as well as friends and families, as to the Christian standard expected of us to stand up for the good of the country which we proudly call our own.

Lesson Commentary

I. Being Patriotic! How Far?

As far as a Christian’s responsibility towards government, we know from Romans 13:1-7 that we are to be subject to the governing authorities and to honor them, even when they are not honorable, because it is ultimately God who has placed them in authority over us. So, as Christians, we are under obligation to God to be model citizens, subject to the governing authorities over us by obeying laws, paying taxes, etc. However, our responsibilities are first and foremost to be obedient to God.

In a democratic republic where individual citizens have the ability to change and influence government by voting or by being politically involved when appropriate and inappropriate ways, part of being a good citizen is voting and having whatever positive influence we can on government.

All individuals and families naturally and inevitably belong to larger communities: a local community (the town, city, or the country), perhaps some intermediate community such as a local government areas or state, and a country or nation. Patriotism is love for one’s country or nation and loyalty to it.

Patriotism itself must be distinguished from immoderate nationalism, because there are some specific responsibilities flowing from patriotism. But since love for and loyalty toward the smaller, local community is analogous to patriotism, it can be applied with appropriate qualifications to one’s attitude and duties toward both the local government and any intermediate community, such as the state or the whole country.

II. Patriotism! The Christ Followers!

Patriotism has significant emotional components, but centrally it should be a volitional love: the steady willing of the true good of one’s nation.

Patriotic feelings must be shaped by sound judgment and good will. Provided it is integrated with the steady willing of the true good of the community, emotional attachment to one’s nation is a powerful motive for fulfilling the responsibilities of authentic patriotism. Unless patriotic feelings are shaped by sound judgment and integrated with an upright will, however, they easily lead to moral failings of various kinds.

The followers of Christ should be seen as a people who are sojourners, strangers, exiles, and refugees in this world - Hebrews 11:13; 1 Peter 2:11; Philippians 3:20. A happy, peaceful, loving people who swear allegiance to a foreign king, Jesus Christ, and to no other.

Our patriotism as Christians must be seen as a people who reside in every nation but whose all-determining citizenship is in heaven, from which we await our King and Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ. We must live, believe in Christ for the world to see us as the freest of all peoples in the world. Free from fear and greed because the kingdom to which we belong cannot be shaken - Hebrews 12:28, and because our true fatherland is heaven - Hebrews 11:16), and the city of our destiny has God as its builder and maker - Hebrews 11:10.

We are a free people, we have our liberty in Christ and because our minds are not conformed to this age but are transformed by the mercies of God, so that we are not enslaved by fashion or fad or any other form of covetousness, our strong desires are not shaped by the persuaders of this world but shaped by the messages coming from the Holy Spirit. O for a church with a single and radical allegiance to the King of kings who said, "My kingdom is not of this world" (John 18:36).

III. Patriotism! Celebrating Our Nationhood!

As Christians we are expected to lead and live by example, unfortunately many end-time manifestations have confirmed that not everyone who says ‘Lord’ are truly and genuinely born again Christians.

However, for us as members of The Foursquare Family, we should celebrate our nationhood, we must appreciate where God has put us, we must appreciate that every nation has their peculiarities, nations evolve in stages, our national symbols and ceremonies that reflect significant events of our life must be honored, we must imbibe attitudes that tend to bind us into unity than those that divide us. Our patriotism towards our country must therefore be respected alongside the complex process of adopting, altering, abolishing, re-establishing and inventing our national values.

Our national flags, our national anthem, the national pledge of allegiance to our country under God are central to our nationhood and are used to represent, justify and glorify our pride for our nation. The use of our national flags and participation in our nation’s ceremonies help symbolize commonality and the oneness of our nation.

We must endorse every engagement, every activity, every ceremony that will bring about the unity of the country without compromising our faith, we must avoid such ceremonies that have no relationship with the Bible, we must avoid their unholy sacrifice and still yet demonstrate our love to every one especially those that are in need.

Conclusion & Life Application

Perhaps the most important distinction to be made by Christians is that our first love must be the kingdom of God, over and above any love of country, no matter how pure and honorable that love might be.

It's easy for Christians to begin to worship their country as an idol. In The Four Loves, C. S. Lewis puts the matter quite succinctly. He notes that love of country "becomes a demon when it becomes a god." In short, we can too easily allow our celebration of nation to intertwine with and pervert our love of God, and in many cases, usurp our love for God. We see how humanity has twisted almost every gift of God into an idol that can sinfully replace him. Money, sex, work, food, drink—each of these (among others) have become damaging idols for too many Christians. It wouldn't take long for any of us to think of examples of when Christians have placed love of country above love of God—the end result is disastrous.

Questions

1. Should a Christian be patriotic to one’s country?

2. What biblical example can we lay claim to of patriotism to encourage our nationhood in Nigeria?

3. Should we as Christians obey and honor our national flag?

Teachers’ Site.

(Teaching the Lesson)

Teaching Overview/ Insight

In countries where Christians have no say in the decisions of their leaders, it is more difficult to be patriotic. It is very hard to love oppressive governments. However, as Christians we are still obligated to obey and pray for our leaders - 1Timothy 2:1-4. God will honor our obedience to this command, and in His perfect timing, He will judge leaders who turn away from Him.

Should a Christian be patriotic? Within reason, yes. At the same time, a Christian’s ultimate faith, love, and obedience are to be reserved for God alone. How, then, should Christians distinguish between patriotism and nationalism? Well, as in most things, it's best to know what Jesus did.

In Luke 19, there's a peculiar story about Jesus. In the midst of the pomp and glory of the triumphal entry into Jerusalem, Jesus begins to weep. He weeps because the city-and, by extension, all Israel—failed to recognize his lordship, and because he knew the impending destruction of Jerusalem. The scene echoed a previous lament over Jerusalem -Luke 13:31-35. Jesus, it seems, loved his people, his city, his nation—so much so that he cried over it.

Jesus' example of patriotism perfected can provide a guidepost to Christians. It's an example that's shot through with justice, truth, and love. It's not a nationalistic patriotism—it's a love for nation that doesn't pit it against other nations. Instead, it’s recognition of love followed by a mournful recognition of shortcomings.

Lesson 41

Topic: Divine Healing

Text: Ex.15:26; Ps.103:3-4; Matt.8:17; Mk.17:17; Acts 4:29-30;

James 5:14-16; 11King 4:17-31, 5:1-14; Isaiah 38:1-21

Supplementary References: - Gen.22:14; Isaiah 53:5; 1Cor.11:23-30; Lk4:40; Mk.11:23; Ezekiel 48:35; Jer.23:6

Central Truth:

Our God is the Jehovah Rapha of the then and the great Physician of the now.

Memory Verse: Exodus 15:26; James 5:9

Lesson Outline

I. The Redemptive Name Of God

II. Jesus Christ, Our Healer

III. The Methods Jesus Employed

Lesson Introduction

We believe that Jesus Christ heals people who are sick. God does not change; He is still willing and able to heal the body, as well as the soul and spirit, in response to faith (Matt.16:17-18; James 5:14-16).

Lesson Commentary

The ministry of healing is a key part of the gospel of the Kingdom. The great truth that God heals runs all through the Bible. God is a healing God (Ex.15:26). Christ is a healing Christ (1Peter 2:24). The Word is a healing Word (Psalm 107:20). The Believers Church, New Testament Church was a healing community (Acts 15:12). God wants His Church to be an instrument of healing to the people in this generation. Both Old and New Testaments give witness to God’s provision for the healing of our sicknesses and diseases. A considerable portion of Christ’s earthly ministry was concerned directly with ministering physical healing to the sick and afflicted. The history of the Church is full of account of great healings and miracles done in the name of Jesus.

Divine healing is the natural and supernatural processes by which the Almighty God repairs the body, soul and spirit. A single act of disobedience plugged the human race into a dark fall from the presence of God: Eating the fruit of the tree which God had said; “but of the tree of knowledge of good and evil, thou shalt not eat of it” . Gen. 2:16-17 brought not only spiritual death to the human race, but also ultimately physical death. Sickness entered into the world when Adam fell. Today, sin still causes sickness. Apart from sickness and diseases caused by sin, demonic powers and neglect of health can also cause sickness. But through God’s redemptive provision, healing for the body, soul and spirit became accessible to all who call upon the name of Jesus for salvation .

I. The Redemptive Name of God

The redemptive name of God is a name used in conjunction with the name of Jehovah, which expresses the particular aspect of redemption He has provided.

a. Jehovah Jireh

Meaning ‘the Lord will provide’ (Gen. 22:14). How wonderful to know that God has committed Himself to provide for us a Redeemer, one who dies in our place.

b. Jehovah Rapha

Meaning ‘I Am the Lord that healeth thee’ (Ex.15:26). Not only that God revealed Himself as Saviour and Deliverer, but also reveals Himself as Healer.

c. Jehovah Nissi

Meaning ‘The Lord our Banner or our Victor’ (Ex.17:15). This revelation came after God had given a great victory to Israel over the Amalekites.

d. Jehovah Shalom

Meaning ‘The Lord our Peace’ (Judges 6:23). The book of Ephesians 2:14 says, Jesus is our peace; which literally means fullness of peace, the possession of all things necessary for one’s peace.

e. Jehovah Raah

Meaning ‘The Lord my Shepherd’ (Ps.23:1). David considered the great mercies, security, protection and provision of God made available to him and he declared: “The Lord is my Shepherd, I shall not lack”…….

f. Jehovah Tsidkenu

Meaning “The Lord our Righteousness” (Jer.23:6). The righteousness that God demands to relate with us Jesus made it possible for us. He bore the penalty, shame and judgment of God in our stead. Jesus does not only ‘save’ us; he declared us righteous before God (1Cor.1:30).

g. Jehovah Shammah

Meaning ‘The Lord is Present’ (Ezekiel 48:35). Due to Adam’s disobedience, human race was driven out from the presence of God, while Jesus who is the second Adam, brought us back to the presence of God.

II. Jesus Christ, Our Healer

Every blessing, including healing, comes to us through Christ from God’s presence. Surely He hath borne our griefs and carried our sorrows (Isaiah 53:4). One of the things mankind lost was health and strength which is restored through the atonement.

a. Jesus’ physical suffering paid for our healing. Isa.53:5; Matt.8:16-17, 1Cor.11:23-24,29-30; 1Peter 2:4

Jesus’ death on the cross met and satisfied every demand for perfect remedy for the whole man- body, soul and spirit.

b. Healing was part of Jesus’ ministry. Matt.4:23-24; 9:25; 12:15

Jesus Himself is our best guide to the ministry of healing. He healed when healing was needed to prove His divine assignment and authority over all circumstances. He healed to convey God’s mercy and to destroy the works of the devil. Phil.2:27; 1Jn.3:8

c. Healing promises are still valid today. Heb.13:8; Mk.16:17-18; John 14:12-14; 1Cor.12:7-9; James 5:14-15

Jesus did not only come to show and introduce us to God’s mercy, but also left the healing mission for us his believers to continue where He stopped.

III. The Methods Jesus Employed

Jesus employed different methods to heal the people in His ministry on earth. Let us consider briefly a few of these methods:

a. He Spoke The Word Of Authority.

The Roman officer, as an army officer, was accustomed to giving and receiving orders. He demanded that Jesus should just “speak the word only and my servant shall be healed”. We also have authority in Jesus name. Mark 11:23

b. He Laid Hands Upon The Sick.

Jesus had to lay hands on the sick because of their cultural background; people had great faith in laying on of hands. Mk.5:21-23, 6:5, Lk.4:40.

c. He Rebuked Sickness. Lk.4:33-37, 38-39

Jesus demonstrated His power and authority over sickness and diseases.

d. He Touched People. Matt.8:3, 9:29, Lk.22:51

Jesus expressed His compassion for the sick and manifested the glory of God. John 11:40; Lk.13:10-17.

e. People Touched Him. Matt. 14:35-36; Mk.5:25-29.

People demonstrated their faith in Jesus’ redemptive power to heal.

Questions & Discussion

1. In view of the study on this lesson, discuss the practical steps in obtaining healing.

2. Why did Jesus Christ heal or why was healing part of His ministry?

Conclusion

Sickness entered the world when Adam fell. Sin and demonic powers still cause sickness today. Sickness can also come through neglect of health; also through aging which reduces one’s physical strength. Our heart’s passion for the readers is that God would grant them grace to do all that is necessary and give them divine healing when we are sick in Jesus name (Amen).

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

It is God’s will to heal. God prefers not to afflict you, He’d rather heal (Ps.22:24). No sickness no matter how serious it is, is difficult for God to heal. In order to obtain and benefit from the healing of God, you should begin with a clean heart; confess any unconfessed sin so that it will not hinder your faith to receiving healing. Prepare by memorizing promises and find authority in God’s word and faith will come of itself. Ask in faith, nothing wavering. Stand your ground in faith and trust, because real faith puts into action what it believes and thank God for healing and answering your prayer.

Lesson 42

Topic: The Spirit-Filled Life.

Text : Ephesians 3:16,20; 4:30; 5:18-19; 6:18; Galatians 5:16, 25; I Cor.6:19,20; Romans 5:5; 8:11; 4:17; I Thess.5:19.

Supplementary References: Jer 17: 9; Gal 5: 17; Eph 2: 1; 1 Cor 2: 14; Rom 8: 9; Jn 15: 5; Col 2: 6; 1 Pet 5: 8; 1 Jn 4: 4; Jn 16: 12 – 14; Acts 1: 1 – 5, 2: 1 – 4; Gen 1: 2; Gal 2: 20;

Central Truth:

A spirit-filled life is ever victorious and never defeated.

Memory Verse: (Ephesians 5:18-19)

Lesson Outline

I. Trouble with the Natural Man

II. Temptations of the Carnal Man

III. Triumph of the Spiritual Man

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that born-again believers should be people who pray and who allow the Holy Spirit to shape their values and behaviours every day; their lives should exemplify love, honesty and sincerity (Ephesians 4:30-32).

Lesson Commentary

It is good to remind ourselves that the subject matter of this lesson is being considered in relation to mankind and within a Christian context. Since the Spirit is our Enabler, the doctrine of the infilling of the Holy Spirit is therefore critical to fellowship with God and obedience to Him. Without an understanding of this doctrine, the believer cannot properly function in the Christian life. As a background to this lesson, we will consider briefly the three types of human nature that can be identified all around us.

The Natural man : - He represents everyday man, born with a very high propensity to sin. His "heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked ..." Jeremiah 17:9. Living in sin is his natural instinct.

The Carnal man - is a natural man who encounters Christ through the working of the Holy Spirit, he becomes a new man born of the Spirit. His life begins to take a new shape with the help of the Holy Spirit who indwells him. But to the extent that he easily compromises with his human nature, he remains a carnal man. Such is the case with many backsliders, and they are at risk of relapsing to the state of a natural man.

The Spiritual man - is the one, who having encountered Christ, enjoys the grace of having the Holy Spirit dwelling in him as well as having total control of his life to the extent that he yields himself continually to his counsel.

In view of this background, a spirit-filled life, the centre point of our lesson, will be more like the spiritual man, which our outline is out to address in greater details.

I. Trouble with the Natural Person – Jer. 17: 9; Ro 5: 5; 8: 9; 1 Co 6: 19 - 20;

Until one is regenerated, "... the sinful nature desires what is contrary to the Spirit and the Spirit what is contrary to the sinful nature ..." (Gal 5: 17). The natural man misses the hope which "does not disappoint" and "the love of God" that has been poured out in believers "hearts by the Holy Spirit ..." There's no love connection between him and God (Ro 5: 5). Little wonder therefore that he also misses the point of the function of his body as "the temple of the Holy Spirit ... given ... by God" who bought him "with a high price" so that he "must honour God" with it (1Cor 6: 19 - 20). To this end, he becomes a victim of defilement, deformity and destruction - not having Christ, unsaved, spiritually dead (Eph. 2: 1), and alienated from the Spirit of God.

Commenting on Gal 5: 17, Beth Moore in Living Beyond Yourself asserts that one of human biggest reasons for failure in daily struggles is our vain attempt to balance the Spirit and the flesh - to make them function as co-workers. They can't work together because they are polar opposites. A natural man is a willing tool in the hands of Satan who operates in partnership with the world systems to promote the interest of their offspring - flesh.

A. He's self ruled - 1 Corinthians 2: 14

The "... natural man does not receive the things of the Spirit of God", they are" just beyond him. (1 Co 2: 14). He simply follows his heart. The phrase: "follow your heart" is a common one in the civilized world today, but it has been exported to other parts of the world. Need one be apologetic in saying that it has a clear expression in the life of a natural man? Of course, when one places it side by side with the Scriptures (Jer 17: 9), one can rightly guess what to expect, since it is beyond argument that out of the abundance of the heart the mouth will speak.

  1. His life excludes Christ – Romans 8: 9; John 15: 5

“... Now if anyone does not have the Spirit of Christ, he is not His.” (Rom 8:9). As a self-willed individual, the natural man "... does not belong to Christ". He has no space for Him in the Inn - that has been occupied by the rudiments of the world, philosophies and traditions of men (Col. 2:6). As a natural man who has no connection with Christ (John 15: 5), he can only do NOTHING! He has "no means by which to be truly victorious over sin ... God expects unredeemed people to sin". This may explain why revival has nothing to do with unsaved people.

  1. He stands the risk of engaging in self-destruction - Romans 5: 5, 8: 11

As earlier inferred, the natural man operates in the midst of three pretty strong foes - the world, the devil and the flesh - real wolves! So, the lions are saved the trouble of "seeking whom to devour". (1 Pet 5: 8). Unfortunately and quite unlike the Spiritual man who is blessed with someone "greater in" him "than he that is in the world", the natural man has no such in him and therefore he is spiritually defenceless and incapacitated (1Jn 4: 4). Putting it another way, he stands the risk of eternal death because "the Spirit of Him who raised Jesus from the dead" does not dwell in him. Therefore, he misses the grace to have life given to his mortal body" to quicken it back to eternal life! (Ro 8:11, 4: 17).

II. Temptations Of the Carnal Man - Eph 4: 30, 6: 18, 1Thess 5: 19

A. Life is directed by Self

“There is therefore now no condemnation to those who are in Christ Jesus, who do not walk according to the flesh, but according to the Spirit” (Romans 8:1). It seems very clear from this scripture that being in Christ and walking in the Spirit are two separate issues, both of which are essential to living a spirit-filled life. The carnal man often derails at the point of various temptations posed by the world systems. Living by personal strength, He easily gives in to the sinful passions of the flesh. This could not be made any clearer than how the Holy Spirit puts it across through Paul that "those who live according to the flesh set their minds on the things of the flesh, but those who live according to the Spirit, the things of the Spirit... the carnal mind is enmity against God; for it is not subject to the law of God, nor indeed can be.” Rom 8:5-7. The only way of escape here will be by "being watchful to this end" and “praying always with all prayer and supplication in the Spirit…" (Eph 6: 18). But by the way a carnal man lives, he is weak in prayer, and weak enough to fail in his duty not to bring sorrow to God’s Holy Spirit nor "grieve the Holy Spirit of God". (Eph 4:30). The Spirit is grieved whenever any person rebels against God's word that is revealed through the Spirit, or when men despise the preaching of the truth or disciples disobey God. Only God knows how often a carnal man stifles or "quenchs the Spirit.” (I Thess 5:19). The Greek word used here for quench is sbennumi, which in this context means "to dampen, hinder, repress as in preventing the Holy Spirit from exerting His full influence". Simply put, to quench the Holy Spirit is to say no to God. And this is easily identifiable in the life of a carnal man.

B. Limited space for Jesus - John 16: 12 - 14

Even when an individual is born again, except he or she yields totally to the Holy Spirit, such a person cannot have enough of Jesus to direct her/his life. So it's much easier for a carnal man to promote self-glory. Jesus operates in a believer's life through the Holy Spirit whom He had delegated all responsibilities to as He went to the Father. “I still have many things to say to you, but you cannot bear them now. However, when He, the Spirit of truth, has come, He will guide you into all truth; for He will not speak on His own authority, but whatever He hears He will speak; and He will tell you things to come. He will glorify Me, for He will take of what is Mine and declare it to you” (John 16:12 – 14). An interesting thing to note about the activities of a spirit filled life is that it glorifies Christ. This is because the Holy Spirit does not glorify Himself but Christ!

III. Triumph of the Spirit-filled Man – Eph. 3: 16, 20; 5: 18 - 21.

One of the essential ministries of the Holy Spirit is to grant an inner strength which allows a spirit-filled man to triumph over various daily temptations being orchestrated by the three dreadful foes (the world, the devil and the flesh) of the redeemed people (Eph. 3: 16, 20). That is why God wants every believer to be filled with the Spirit, because it is the only way to triumph over the lusts of the flesh and of the eyes, and the pride of life. But like someone said, many Christians are not sure what this means or what it looks like. It is believed that an attempt to help Ephesian believers understand that whatever fills them controls them, makes Paul cite drunkenness as a negative example of "filling" and quickly warns them to avoid it [Ephesians 5:18-21]. Every believer is indwelt by God's Spirit, but the extent of His rule is determined by the Christian's freedom to comply.

  1. Longing for the Spirit - Acts 1: 1 - 5

The longing in the heart of a man to please God, followed by the right steps taken, can activate the fulfilment of the promise of in-filling of the Spirit in his life. This longing will enable him support godly plans which will ultimately result in victory. “And being assembled together with them, He commanded them not to depart from Jerusalem, but to wait for the Promise of the Father, “... you shall be baptized with the Holy Spirit not many days from now.”” (Acts 1:4-5). Of course, the result was seen “When the Day of Pentecost had fully come, they were all with one accord in one place... And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak with other tongues, as the Spirit gave them utterance” (Acts 2:1-4).

  1. Living in the Spirit - Romans 8: 5, 11

Living in the Spirit is actually a triumph of the Spirit over the flesh! And it's a practice that can only be correctly associated with those who are responsible to the Holy Spirit (1 Co 6: 19 - 20), those who express the reality of Gal 2: 20 on daily basis! It's hoped that we agree that living and walking in the Spirit are two ways of saying the same thing. There are obvious advantages of walking in the Spirit which the scriptures point out. Besides the avoidance of the consequential pains of walking in the flesh, others include the righteous requirement of the law being fulfilled, spiritual sensitivity being optimally at work, abundant life being assured, and peace that passes human understanding is guaranteed. It also includes the advantages of physical quickening of believers’ bodies which go beyond now unto eternity. But here and now, perhaps as a proof that "gospel also includes God's care for the physical body", Holy Spirit still continues in the healing ministry of (quickening as it were) the dead (sick) bodies in honour of Christ Jesus. Healing of the deaf, the lame, the stroke patient and such like in our days can only confirm this further. The Spirit of God can still hover over every dark spot around us to restore hope! (Gen 1: 2). Living by the Spirit provides believers in Christ with the opportunity to access the immense benefits in the ministry of the Holy Spirit.

It is equally important to know that one of the essential ministries of the Holy Spirit to believers is the sealing, and this is seen in the context of Eph 4: 30. The Greek word used for seal here is sphragizo and it has two-fold meaning. Firstly, to seal is to "close up and make fast with a seal signet such as letters or books so that they may not be read" by unauthorized persons. Secondly, it means "to set seal or a mark upon a thing as a token of its authenticity or approvedness". In this context therefore, the seal of the Holy Spirit is for protection and identification - believers are identified as joint heirs with Christ, whose lives are protected against any intruders.

C. Leading of the Spirit - Romans 8: 14; Matthew 4: 1; Luke 4: 1; Galatians 2: 20; 1 John 4: 4; John 16: 33;

Like Beth Moore puts it: "Every time you realize God has somehow pricked your heart to discern direction, the Holy Spirit has manifested Himself through leadership." Let us sum up the secret of the triumph of a spiritual man thus: The spiritual man is the person in which the mind of Christ is activated. He or she is a person who delights in the meat of God and not just the milk. His/her mind is open to those things which "no eye has seen, nor ear has heard, no mind has conceived.” The spiritual man lives and thinks in a capacity far beyond that of a mere man. Leading of the spirit in a spirit-filled life is a perfect function of yieldedness. Moore further expressed that "the issue of spirituality rests upon yieldedness to the Holy Spirit's control. Spiritual life will never come naturally." It's only in the matter of sin nature that you don't make any conscious choice to sin, because it comes naturally.

Conclusion & Life Application

Beloved fellow pilgrims, because "those who are in the flesh cannot please God", living in the Spirit has no acceptable alternative for us. (Rom 8: 8). It is therefore a necessity for every heavenly-bound believer in Christ to “... Walk in the Spirit, ... For the flesh lusts against the Spirit, and the Spirit against the flesh; and these are contrary to one another, so that you do not do the thing that you wish" (Gal 5:16-17). In the counsel of Paul's passionate appeal to the Galatians Church, fellow believers in Christ must "live according to the direction of God's word" in order to "bring their lives into harmony with the direction of the Spirit" (Dickson pg. 1535).

It is in a spirit filled life that one can boast of being led to know and grow in God, and being empowered to fight victoriously the life's inevitable battles with the world, the flesh, and the devil. In every way and at every turn, we are utterly dependent on the Holy Spirit of God.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

Natural persons have no means by which to be truly victorious over sin. They are driven solely by their own nature. We should not be surprised at the depravity that surrounds us. God no doubt wills us to "grow in the knowledge" of the Lord Jesus Christ, that we may be thoroughly furnished. Yet, the issue of spirituality rests upon yieldedness to the Holy Spirit’s control. It must be understood that it is always a result of deliberate choice to yield, to consciously surrender. The spiritual life will never come naturally. Surrender must be a daily choice. We can be spiritual one day and not the next, or even in the morning and not in the night that follows. We are spiritual when we are out of control and the Holy Spirit is in control. Beloved, God longs to pour out His blessings upon every believer who will not quench the Holy Spirit: by saying yes to God, nor grieve the Holy Spirit: by saying no to sin.

Lesson 43

Topic : The Spirit-Filled Life: Character versus Charisma

Text: 1 Corinthians 13; 12; Romans 12:1-21

Supplementary References: Eph. 4: 1, 5: 15 – 17; Gal 4: 19, 5: 22 – 23; Matt 7: 16, 20; Rom 3: 9 -20; Gal 3: 10-14; 1 Kings 19: 11 – 13; Prov. 4:23.

Central Truth :

Manifesting the gifts of the Holy Spirit without character is of no value, God is more interested in character (fruit) than charisma (gifts).

Memory Verse: Romans 12:9.

Lesson Outline

I. Charisma in the Spirit-filled Life

II. Character in the Spirit-filled Life

III. Excellency of Character over Charisma

Lesson Introduction

We believe that born-again believers should be people who pray and who allow the Holy Spirit to shape their values and behaviours every day; their lives should exemplify love, honesty and sincerity (Ephesians 4:30-32).

Lesson Commentary

Just like the Bible says, there are diverse kinds of the gifts of the Spirit, with visible benefits to the Church. Therefore, manifestation of the gifts of the Holy Spirit is a normal expectation in a spirit filled life. However, the evidence of the Spirit's control is revealed better in the people of character. The manifestation of the gift of the spirit would most likely promote charisma in individuals, but it is only in a person of character that the fruit of the spirit becomes visible. It therefore seems to suggest that manifestation of the fruit of the spirit serves a better measure of a spirit-filled life. I believe that Paul was in the process of giving very specific instructions about the purpose and practice of the gifts of the Spirit which were being abused in the Corinthian Church when he penned down 1 Corinthians 13.

The people of Corinth loved philosophy and listened all the more if it was presented with depth and colour - they might have even considered a discourse most impressive if they couldn’t comprehend a single word of it. They were more often interested in the expression of language than its content. But Paul admitted that he did not come before them with great polish and fluency. We can imagine the shock of their lives. Paul, responding further to their obsession with words said: “If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love”, my words will be meaningless noise. He then went on to refer to the spiritual gifts of prophecy, knowledge, and faith, which were proudly being misused.

I. Charisma in the Spirit filled Life

If we agree that every believer is indwelt by God's Spirit, we must also realize that the extent of His rule is determined by the extent to which each exercises his freedom to comply with the demands of the Spirit in him. Think of this as a voluntary choice to surrender your life to the Holy Spirit's control—in other words, to be sensitive and submissive to His leadership and guidance, obedient to His promptings, and dependent upon His strength. The degree of surrender determines the level of transformation. And those who have yielded their lives to His leadership are continually being transformed into His likeness.

A. A Case for Charisma

Charisma may seem like affluence, charm and having an “anointing” or energetic personality. It is often mistaken as a character trait. Perhaps this explains why people who function in such screaming spiritual gifts like healings, works of miracle or eloquence draw a lot of crowd to themselves. Their influence on people can be quite electrifying. Being charismatic is not necessarily a bad idea. It is only when it is devoid of character that it loses value. As a matter of fact, combining charisma with character has been considered invaluable assets in most human endeavours, spiritual inclusive.

B. Limitation of Charisma

Granted that spiritual gifts manifest themselves in different ways, one must nevertheless not understand the diversity of the function of the gifts in a way that would undermine the supremacy or oneness of God. Miraculous gifts were not given for the purpose of self-exaltation as the Corinthian believers were conducting themselves with one another. They were given for the benefit of edification – Rom 12: 6 – 8. The very purpose for which the gifts were given was based on serving the needs of others. Believers must not fall into Corinthian mode of using for self-exaltation what God has given for the purpose of administering service to others. This requires one yielding to the Holy Spirit, an area where charismatic personalities are often deficient. Even though good works and faithful service are a result of being filled with the Spirit, they are not necessarily signs of it. When we talk about what we do, that is about charisma. How much noise do we hear around us today, which may lack the presence of God! 1 Kings 19: 11 - 13. "Charisma doesn't ... impress God", if it impresses you, be careful (Rick Warren).

It is Swindoll who maintains that if you get involved in a ministry that glorifies itself, instead of Christ, the Spirit of God is not in that ministry. If you follow a leader that is getting the glory for that ministry, instead of Christ, the Spirit of God isn’t empowering his leadership. If you’re a part of a Christian school or mission organization or a Christian camping ministry in which someone other than Christ is being glorified, it is not being empowered by the Spirit of God. Mark it down: THE SPIRIT GLORIFIES CHRIST.

II. Character in the Spirit filled Life

A. Spirit-Controlled Life Produces Character

The key to rightly living the Christian life is being controlled by the Holy Spirit, who provides energy for walking "worthy of the calling with which you have been called" (Eph. 4:1). Unless you are controlled by the Spirit of God, you can never walk in humility, love, unity, light, and wisdom. The life of God in the soul of man is the only way anyone can live a righteous life. To walk without the Spirit is to walk without wisdom (cf. Eph. 5:15 -17) and by inference, without character.

It's easier to function in the areas of some giftedness than to love the unlovable or be patient with difficult people. How do you feel seeing a professed believer in Christ who says all the right things but his personality but the life he lives seem disgustingly at variance! This is where lines are drawn between charisma and character.

B. Distinctive of the Fruit of the Spirit

The most distinguishing factor between a man of character and that of charisma is the evidence of the fruit of the Spirit. Beth Moore defines this fruit as: “the supernatural outcome of being filled with the Spirit or the living proof that the Spirit of God dwells in us”. Gal 5: 22 – 23 and 1Cor 13: 4 – 8 seem to be in agreement on the matter of the fruit and its qualities. Since it is “by their fruits” (Mat 7: 16, 20) we will know those who are truly led by the Holy Spirit, it becomes easier for us to understand why a man of character exhibits much more naturally the fruit of the Spirit.

Of course, the Spirit is believers’ Helper, producing in us the fruits that make us people of character.

No wonder therefore that the doctrine of the infilling of the Holy Spirit is critical to fellowship with God in obedience, without which the believer cannot properly function in the Christian life.

The necessity of a proper understanding of the filling/controlling ministry of the Spirit is amplified by Paul's prayer in Gal 4: 19, "My children—I am again undergoing birth pains until Christ is formed in you!” Included in the meaning of the Greek word translated “formed” (morfow) is the concept of giving outward expression of inward character. The form means the essential form rather than outward shape. The idea is therefore of real Christ-like character." It is about Christ being outwardly expressed in the believer, from within through the power of the Spirit. Mere external conformity is not the goal. We must note also that the verb “formed” is passive. This means the subject receives the action rather than produces the action.

In Rick Warren’s view, the maturity we need for our Christian service is not about skills alone. It's also about character. And the maturity of character is not about age, it's not about appearance, it's not about achievements, nor about mere academics. It's about attitude - character! That is the determining factor of spiritual maturity. Character is the habitual ways we respond to the situations of life. It is not reputation. Reputation is what other people think you are, but character is what you really are. D. L. Moody believes that "Character is who you are in the dark." Character is who you are when nobody's looking. Character is who you are when you're not on stage. Character is the real you. And God knows your character. Character is something that you have and something that you are.

III. Excellency of Character over Charisma

Agape (unconditional love) as the fruit of the Spirit brings to the fore front the beauty of living a spirit-filled life. It produces character which manifests itself in different forms - Gal 5: 22 - 23. Of course, the way of love is more excellent than the function of miraculous gifts. Love is more excellent than prophecy, languages or healings. According to Paul’s counsel to the Corinthian believers, they should be striving after love instead of competing over the use of their gifts. Paul seems to be telling them (and indeed all believers) that their goal for the church should be to grow beyond the ministry of miraculous gifts, to where each member can become a servant to other members in the spirit of love. When a closer look is taken at the Greek word used for love in 1 Cor 13 and its characteristics as presented by Paul to the Corinthians, unconditional love (agape) becomes quite a useful premise for us to examine the excellence of character over charisma. In contrast to philos, agape is not a feeling or relationship based on common interests. It is always propelled by the highest interest. Two individuals can agree on something (as Pilate and Herod did) and both be wrong. In contrast, agape always flows from what is right and best, it is not as much a feeling as it is a response. When God commands man to ‘agape’, this is not towards a feeling but to surrender to an act of His Spirit which results in obedience.

The fact that agape is not fuelled by the desire of its recipient, but by the need of others makes character its most distinguishing feature. It is patient, kind, does not envy, not boast, not proud, not rude, not self-seeking, not easily angered, keeps no record of wrongs, does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth, always protects, always trust, always hopes, always perseveres, and never fails. These are often either non-existent or in short supplies in the life of a man of charisma.

Conclusion & Life Application:

So often, we tend to run off to do this or that in our own steam because we are all so prone to trust in our own resources. The truth is because of human weakness, no one can live the Christian life any more than one can perfectly keep the Law of Moses (Rom 3: 9 -20; Gal 3: 10-14). There is no aspect of the Christian life, Bible study, prayer, witnessing, growth, etc., that is not vitally dependent on the enabling ministry of the Holy Spirit of God. Though God gives us spiritual gifts and a new capacity for life through the Spirit’s work of regeneration, it is the Holy Spirit, as our divine Enabler, who empowers our lives for both worship and service. When the Spirit is in charge of our lives, He does through us what we cannot do for ourselves. Each believer decides who rules his life, by either actively surrendering to Christ or deliberately going his own way. It may be said that the fullness of the Spirit and godly character await those who choose God over self.

Someone with a lot of charisma usually requires attention and recognition. When charisma is present but there is a lack of character, the charismatic person must feed on the praise of others to feel important. Such a person must put on the cloak of charisma to be liked, upheld and regarded as one of importance. Charisma can easily be a cover for a lack of character.

The person of character is contented to live behind the scenes and be effective without being noticed by others. Character is the substance of the person, but charisma is the magnetic appeal or draw of a message. We must note that there are people of tremendous character who speak or minister and also communicate with charisma. This doesn't necessarily disqualify them from being people of character.

When you hear a message, a speech, a sermon, a radio broadcast, a television interview, etc., don’t turn off your discernment filter. Get past the charismatic delivery and really look at the content of the message and the character of the one who is speaking. This sounds like a Berean Christian model, and it still works! We cannot afford to become callous or numb and fail to guard our hearts diligently as wisdom commands us to (Prov. 4:23).

Questions & Discussion:

1. With reference to 1 Kings 19: 11 - 13, discuss the issue of crowd-pulling Christian events in our days?

2. When does charisma become a desirable virtue?

3. What message in your personal opinion does the absence of character in a Spirit filled believer convey?

Integrity, Faithfulness, Honour, Upstanding reputation, Deeds/actions that are pure and right, evidence of the fruit of the Spirit in words and actions (Galatians 5:22), one who searches Scripture to see if something is true (Acts 17:11), those with clean hands and pure hearts, who don’t make vanity the purpose of their lives or swear oaths just to deceive (Ps 24:4-6), those whose word can be trusted (1 Tim 3:8).

Just as iron sharpens iron, a person sharpens the character of his friend. Romans 5:4 "... endurance produces character, and character produces hope;” Ephesians 3:14, 15 "...For this reason, I fall on my knees before the Father, from whom every family in heaven and on earth receives its character.”

Lesson 44

Topic: The Gifts and Fruit of the Spirit Part 1

Text: I Cor. 12:1-11; Romans 12:6-8; Eph. 4:11-15

Supplemental References: 1 Peter 4:10-11; Galatians 3:5

Central Truth:

God gives to His people the gifts that corresponds their calling and offices.

Memory Verse: Eph. 4:11-12

Lesson Outline

I. Definition and Design of Spiritual Gifts

II. Distinctives of the Gifts

Lesson Introduction

Just like it was with the Corinthian church in the first century, there are misunderstandings about the gifts of the Spirit in 21st century church (I Cor. 12:1). For the church take full advantage of the endowments of the Holy Spirit, it is important for believers to be constantly reacquainted with the distinctive and purpose of these gifts so that each person may serve the church in the capacity in which God has placed him or her and that there may be no mix-up and breakup in the body of Christ. This is exactly the emphasis of this lesson. Also, it is important to establish the link between the fruit and the gifts of the Spirit.

Lesson Commentary

I. Designation of the Gifts

The gifts of the Holy Spirit are those supernatural abilities bestowed on Christians for special services in body of Christ. The three texts of this lesson delineate specific gifts that God's Spirit gives to Christians. Basically, they are distinctive, divinely originated endowments to serve the Triune God for the common benefit of his people, the church (Rom 12:4-5; 1 Cor. 12:7; Eph. 4:12-13). In Pauline theology, a spiritual gift is essentially an act of God’s Spirit, a concrete manifestation of God’s grace through an individual for the benefits of others. It is observable that the lists of spiritual gifts demonstrate significant overlap as well as important variations. This indicates that none of the lists, taken either individually or collectively, is intended to be comprehensive. Rather each is suggestive of the diversity of ways God endows Christians for spiritual service. Broader classifications may therefore suggest other gifts not specifically listed. It is important to note that in Paul’s thought, all believers are bearers of God’s graces. Anyone open to and is led by the Spirit (Rom. 8:14), will inexorably manifest the grace of God in some way, expressing the power of God in particular words and deeds within the community of faith.

II. Distinctives of the Gifts (I Cor. 12:1-11)

Significantly, it is important to note that the dynamics, administrations and operations of the gifts are diverse and are the outworking of the Godhead (I Cor. 12:4-6). What this means is that:

a. There are different kinds of gifts. The gifts Paul listed in I Cor. 12:7-10, Rom 12:6-8 and Ephesians 4:11-15 are not necessarily exhaustive and some of them even overlap. A comparison of these texts reveals that Paul was not out to give a comprehensive list, but a selective example relevant to his purpose.

b. Just as there are varieties of gifts, the gifts has different purposes and functions. For example, Peter was an apostle, just as Paul was an apostle, but Paul’s apostolic work was targeted at the gentiles.

c. The gifts are of divine origin. They are generally designated as graces (charismata) of the Spirit. This signifies that the gifts are not the products of human efforts or institutional investiture. In other words the manifestation of any of the gifts does not require any human embellishment to validate it as being divine. It is not a talent show. It does not have to be ecstatic to be charismatic. Similarly, it is not the church’s appointment or ordination that confers gifts on believers.

d. The distribution of the gifts is sovereign. The Holy Spirit distributes to each one as He wills, a gift to a particular person and another to someone else. This means that no one person receives all the gifts and no one gift is imparted to all.

e. The gifts are given for the common benefits of the church and not for selfish advantage, personal significance or exclusive profit. All the gifts are intended to build up the members of the body of Christ, for the work ministry. The leadership gifts as highlighted in Ephesians 4:11-12 are specifically meant to equip the believers, for the work of ministry.

f. The diversity of the gifts and the unity of their source, is symbolic of the Church’s concept of unity in diversity as illustrated with the human body by Paul in Romans 12:4-5 and Ephesians 4:7-8. To the extent that the various gifts are from one Source, they are meant to foster the growth and unity of the church. The gifts are therefore to be complementary and not competitive in unifying the church. The less visible or recognized gifts are as important as the more visible or recognized ones. There should not be any undue overemphasis of one over the other. Just as the physical body functions well only when each part is healthy and working well in unity, so also with the gifts of the Spirit in the body of Christ.

g. Finally, the outworking of the gifts is Christocentric. Jesus Christ is head of the Church and distributor of all gifts of the Spirit (Eph. 4: 11). He distributes gifts from the fullness that he himself possesses, as the result of his incarnation and exaltation (Eph. 4: 8). From Christ’s fullness of the Spirit believers receive a measure “as Christ apportioned it” (Eph. 4:7). The gifts enable believers to fulfil the mission of Christ, as they are particularly capable of leading people to faith in Christ, as believers themselves are being groomed and grounded in the knowledge of Christ, maturing to become like Him (Ephesian 4:13). The indwelling of the Spirit and manifestation of the gifts in the believer’s life affirm and extol the Lordship of Christ (1Cor. 12:3).

Conclusion & Life application.

The nature of the church requires its members to recognize their respective capabilities and to begin functioning in the areas of their particular ministries. Just as the physical body is one organism having many parts, each one with their respective functions, which operate in different manners, but at the same time in harmony and unity, so is the Body of Christ, the Church; unity in the midst of diversity. If the Church is going to function as an organism, then it is necessary for the members to minister or serve one another faithfully with all the capabilities that the Holy Spirit has given to each one (Ephesians 4:16).

Questions & Discussion

1. Express your own definition of spiritual gifts.

2. How do spiritual gifts relate to the unity and diversity of the body of Christ?

3. How are the gifts essential to the common good of the Church?

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

It is very important to acknowledge that the list of gifts provided in the New Testament, in Pauline writings and elsewhere are not conclusive and they overlap. This is to correct the notion that the spiritual gifts are limited to the nine in 1 Corinthians 12:8-10 or the leadership gifts in Ephesians 4:11. Just as there are variety of gifts and functions, there are also variety of classifications, depending on the location where the gifts are in use, the period in the history of the Church, the perspective of a church tradition or denomination and a number of other reasons. However based on the texts of this study, we may categorize the gifts as follows:

1. Gifts of Inspired Speech: Prophecy or Prophesying, Tongues and Interpretation, Teaching, Exhorting

2. Gifts of Inspired Knowledge: Word of Wisdom and Knowledge, Discernment of spirits

3. Gifts of Inspired Action: Faith, Healing, Miracles

4. Gifts of Service: Serving, Giving, Care (showing mercy), Administration

5. Gifts of Leadership: Apostles, Prophets, Teachers, Pastors, and Evangelists

The principle of the diversity of functions applies in interpreting or understanding the functions, intensity and variations of some of these gifts during the apostolic age as contrasted with their applications in today’s church. For example, the gifts of Apostles and prophets in the first century were foundational to the life of the Church (Eph. 2:20). Obviously, these gifts do not have the same dimension of operation today.

Lesson 45

Topic: The Gifts and Fruit of the Spirit Part 2

Text: I Cor. 12:1-11; 28-31; 13:1-8; 14:1-6; 12-40; Romans 12:6-8; Eph. 4:11-16

Supplemental References: Galatians 5:22-23; I Cor. 13:8-13

Central Truth:

Only in the context of love can the Church effectively enjoy the benefits of spiritual gifts as it was in the apostolic days.

Memory Verse: 1 Corinthians 14:1

Lesson Outline

I. Relationship between the gifts and the fruit of the Spirit

II. Preeminence of the fruit of the Spirit

Lesson Introduction

As Paul discussed the gifts of the Spirit, he suddenly changes his focus to ‘love’ as being the “most excellent way” of obtaining the gifts. What is the reason for this sudden change? Why is love the most excellent way? What is the relationship between love and the gifts of the Spirit? These questions will shape the direction of today’s lesson .

Lesson Commentary.

I. Relationship between the gifts and the fruit of the Spirit

The first part of 1 Corinthians12:31 reads, “But eagerly desire the greater gifts.” (NIV) Some Biblical scholars, have suggested that this verse should be read affirmatively, “You earnestly contend about the best gifts; but I show unto you a more excellent way. This is a likely reading, given the fact that Corinthian church was very contentious about the gifts than about the fruit of the Spirit. Earlier, Paul refused to recognize certain charismatics in the church as spiritual, because their gifts, not accompanied by love, had become distorted by jealousy, strife, arrogance and false wisdom (1 Cor. 3:1-3). Love (agape) as used here is not a human attribute, but the work or fruit of the Spirit. Love embeds the other characteristics or kinds of the fruit of the Spirit as Paul highlighted in 1 Corinthians 13:4-7 and Galatians 5:22-23. The principle Paul establishes here is that without love the gifts do not profit the recipients (13:1-3). Love is the context in which the gifts have their full effectiveness. Elsewhere, in his teaching, Paul showed the relationship between the gifts and the fruit of the Spirit, essentially, love Rom. 12:6-9; Eph. 4:2, 7-11, 15).

II. Preeminence of the fruit of the Spirit

No doubt, the gifts of the Spirit are essential for the Church, nevertheless Paul gives the fruit of the Spirit a certain preeminence as 1 Corinthians shows. The following reasons may be adduced for the Apostle emphasis on the matter:

a. The fruit represents the Character of Christ (in his epistles Paul associates Christ with love. He interchangeably speaks of putting on love or Christ— Col. 3:14; Rom.13:14; or of being rooted in love or in Christ— Col. 2:7; Eph. 3:17), while the gifts manifest particular ministries of Christ.

b. The fruit will abide eternally in the personality of the redeemed, while the gifts are bestowed only in part and for the mission of the Church in this present age.

c. The fruit of the Spirit is what all Christians have or should have in common. The gifts, on the other hand, represent a diversity in which one believer’s gift may not be fully understood or appreciated by another. The gifts can bring about unity of the body, only when they are used to complement one another. The fruit, however is the undergirding, common factor that enables the gifts to be used in this manner.

d. The expression of the fruit and not the manifestation of the gifts of the Spirit determines the spirituality or spiritual status of the Christian. Paul did not recognize the highly gifted Corinthian church as spiritual because they were lacking in the fruit of the Spirit. Whenever Christians are more impressed with dramatic gifts rather than authentic fruit, there are major problems. We need to always keep in mind Jesus’ warning, “You shall know them by their fruit.” (Matt. 7:20) He did not say, “You shall know them by their gifts.” Basically, the gifts are unrelated to maturity of the believer in the ‘growth of grace’. They do not primarily arise out of an inward life, but the sovereign act of the great Giver.

e. Where love abounds, decency and order will pervade the manifestation and demonstration of the gifts in the community of the Spirit (I Corinthians 14:32-33). Where there is no love, chaos abounds (13:1b).

Questions & Discussion

1. Some argue that some gifts like tongues and working of miracles have ceased with the apostolic age, what do you think?

2. How does the fruit of Spirit reinforce the unity of the body of Christ, as its members manifest the spiritual gifts?

Life Application.

Understanding the relationship between spiritual gifts and the fruit of the Spirit, gives us the wisdom to balance our walk with God and our work for God through our gifts. Love for God and for God’s people, commits us to a deeper and sincere relationship with Christ and with other Christians (Rom 12:9-10), as we earnestly desire to see the Church become more strengthened and Christ glorified as we show forth our gifts.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

As we have learnt the gifts are given as the Spirit determines (v. 11). One may seek and pray for certain gifts (1 Cor. 14:1, 12), but God makes no guarantees of giving any one particular gift as requested, as His sovereignty supersedes our desires. All the gifts are valuable to the building up of the church; none may be discounted as nonessential (vv. 14-26). There is a ranking of gifts (vv. 27-28) but the sequence is more of chronology than of priority. There is no scriptural grounds for the claim in some circles that any of the gifts have ceased. They are God's characteristic endowments for Christian service in the New Testament age, although there may be variations in intensity and functions as applied to the Church of today. Against the view that maintains, from the lack of the more supernatural gifts throughout much of church history, that these graces were limited to the apostolic age, three points must be noted: (1) these gifts did not end at the close of the first century, but continued well into the third; (2) their subsequent diminution can best be attributed to a growing, unscriptural institutionalization of the church and an overreaction to the abuse of the gifts in heretical (most notably Montanist) circles; (3) even then, no era of church history was completely without examples of all the gifts.

It will be followed by the revelation of Christ leading to the war of Armageddon as this present age passes away to give room to the coming age in which Christ, with his saints, will rule the world in the millennium. It is no more a secret to many Bible believing Christians that we are deep into the period the Bible has identified to us as the LAST DAYS. The events marking the Great Tribulation are well articulated in many Bible passages both in the Old and New Testaments. An attempt to peruse through these passages will be too voluminous for this lesson. Not to be neglected are the Lord Jesus’ sayings referring to the specific time of the Great Tribulation in Matthew 24: 15-31. We can also see Daniel 7:8-15, 19-27; 9:27. (i) The signing of the 7 years peace treaty between the Antichrist and Israel, which actually opens us to the specific period of the Great Tribulation. Everything that will be experienced in the world before this event will just be part of the last days events and the distress upon the nations of the world.

(iii) When the Jews would realize his true colour and refuse to acknowledge him as God, he will break the treaty and then unleash the worst and greatest persecution ever on the Jews as the chosen people of God.

(iv) As this is going on, God Himself, will be raining his judgement on the world as seen in the breaking of the seals, the trumpets and the bowls; judgements of the book of Revelation.

We believe that everyone will stand before the Judgement seat of Christ. Born-again believers will be sent to a place of everlasting life, and unbelievers will be sent to a place of everlasting punishment (Rev. 20:12, 20; I Cor.5:10).

Lesson Commentary

Most of the modern day rejection of the Bible and the God of the Bible is based on the immutable truth expressed in the Scriptures that there is only one way of salvation and the fact that men will give account of their lives to God. To the incorrigibles, this is not acceptable. All they want is the liberty to do whatsoever they like without any form of restraint or caution of afterlife accountability. This is part of the lies and deceits fashioned from the pit of hell to make human beings to be careless and carefree as to the eternal destiny of their souls. Another lie that has been perpetuated among believers today is the over-emphasis on the grace of God in Christ Jesus that tells the child of God that once you have accepted Christ as your Lord and Saviour, your sins that you have committed, and those which you may ever commit have been paid for. While this is true, the effect of it is that the needed watchfulness necessary for a sober life is removed and thus equating the need for practical holiness as walking or living in the law (I Cor.5:9-13, 6:9-11).

I. The Certainty Of God’s Judgement

The Scripture is full of the expressions of God’s judgements over sin right from the moment that Adam and Eve fell from their state of innocence due to disobedience to God’s law. God’s dealings with men as revealed in the Bible indicate to us that every form of disobedience to the express will of God will always incur God’s wrath and judgement. God has made it clear to us in his Word that sin is a terrible thing and as a holy and righteous God, there is no way he can close his eyes to it whenever and wherever it occurs (Hab.1:13; Prov.1:22-33; Ecc.9:9-10, 12:9-14; Romans 2:1-11).

In order to show his mercy to the sinful man, God brought the Judgement of our sins on the Lord Jesus Christ who gave himself as a propitiation for our sin that we can receive God’s mercy and forgiveness. Someone was made to pay the terrible price for our sins in order for God to show his love for us. This great unfathomable love should not make us to take God for granted. This attitude will not benefit us as the Bible reveals (Heb.10:26-39, 6:1-8). Our greatest love for God is in doing his will. However, if we mistakenly sin, we should not allow the enemy to mock us. All we need to do is to get back to God and allow the blood to cleanse us as we repent and forsake such sins (I John 1:5-2:2).

II. The Various Judgements

God’s Judgement on sin is death (Gen. 2:15-17, 3:15-19). This is both spiritual as well as physical. The first passage quoted above indicates spiritual death and the second passage indicates physical death. Spiritual death is the separation from God even when one is alive. One is cut-off from the life of God. In the present dispensation anyone outside of Christ is spiritually dead even though he may still be breathing. Spiritual death describes the condition of all men born through Adam until they are regenerated by the Spirit of God through faith in Christ Jesus. This is the reason why the Bible says that anyone who rejects the offer of grace through Jesus Christ is condemned already. The wrath of God abides on Him perpetually. For any man to reject Christ in this world until he dies (physical death), his state of spiritual death remains forever as he proceeds to hell to suffer a total and an eternal separation from God. Physical death is the lot of all men due to Adam’s rebellion.

However, all those who belong to Jesus Christ will share in his victory over the grave as he will resurrect them to eternal life in a glorified body and to live with him forever. Therefore, the Judgement of God upon sin is both spiritual death and physical death. Spiritual death is the lot of all men through Adam, and this defines the state of all people outside of Christ unless they take advantage of God’s provision of salvation and are regenerated and become alive unto God by the power of the Holy Spirit. Physical death, which the Bible described as our greatest enemy has been defeated by the Lord Jesus Christ by his resurrection from the dead and all who belong to him shall share in this victory.

III. The Two Eternal Destinations

Divine Judgement upon sin has predestined two places of eternal abode for all his moral creatures (both men and angels). These are hell and heaven. Hell, the Bible revealed, will be the abode of Satan and all the angels that joined him in his rebellion against God in heaven. Also, all men that reject God’s offer of salvation in Christ Jesus shall be turned into hell where God made initially for the devil and his angels. The choice is for the individual to decide. Heaven is the abode of God. This has been destined to be the abode for all the faithful angels who did not join the devil in his rebellion. Even though they are presently there with him now, they will continue to live with God forever for all eternity. Also, all the redeemed of the Lord who have accepted God’s offer of salvation in Christ shall be there with him forever.

Conclusion and Life Application

The fact of God’s Judgement upon sin is a comfort to the redeemed of the Lord for all eternity to come. It is the prerogative of God’s mercy, love and grace that brought salvation to mankind as a lost race in Adam. However, this grace teaches us that we should say “No” to ungodliness and worldly passions and to live self-controlled, upright lives in this present age (Titus 2:11&12). The impunity with which men are transgressing the laws of God, rejecting his offer of salvation in Christ and also persecuting God’s people makes the Judgement upon sin inevitable (2 Thessalonians 1:3-10). It also serves to reinforce the resolve of the believers to continue to live for God’s glory as each person will give an account of all his actions to the Creator who has given us the privilege of life (Revelation 22:11&12).

Questions & Discussion

1. What is sin and why is it a dangerous thing for men and an abomination to God?

2. Why would a loving God decide to punish sin forever and reward those who trust Him with life eternal in His presence? Think about it very well.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

Encourage the students to read all Bible passages cited. Give adequate time for reflection. God’s Judgement is not only for unbelievers. The saints will be judged also as indicated in I Cor. 5:10 as to all that we have done in the body. We should also note however that when we continue to do like the unbelievers, live a life without restraint, and we become carnal, we cannot hide under the grace of Jesus Christ to avert the Judgement of God unless we repent before it is too late ( Ezekiel 18:24-32; Gal.5:16-21).

Lesson 51

Topic: Hell is Real

Text: II Thess. 1:8&9; Matt. 25:41&46; Heb.10:26&27; Luke 16:22-28

Central Truth:

One of the horrors of hell is the undying memory of a mis-spent life.

Memory Verse: Luke 16:23

Lesson Outline:

I. The Awesome Mystery of Life

II. Jesus Description of Hell

III. Clarifying Immortality

Lesson Introduction

We believe that hell is a place of darkness, deep sorrow and unquenchable fire, which was not prepared for man, but for the devil and his angels: it will become the place of eternal separation from God for all who reject Christ as Saviour (Matt.13:41, 42; Rev.20:10, 15).

Lesson Commentary

As it has been pointed in the last lesson, the Bible revealed to us that hell is a place God created for the devil and the other angels who joined him in his rebellion against the almighty God (Matt. 25:31-46). However, all men who rejected God’s provision of salvation in Christ Jesus and all who rejected God’s rule in their lives will also find themselves in hell. The great sacrifice God made for in Christ Jesus is such that no man will ever find himself in hell (John 3:16-18, 36).

I. The Awesome Mystery of Life

Many have wondered why such a loving God as our Father in heaven will consign people to hell when these ones do not actually ask to be created or be born. The answer lies in the fact that the gift of life is a marvellous and glorious privilege. It flows from the bosom of a loving God who has bonded Himself to cater for all whom he has brought to existence. Every moral creature of God (man or angel) was created that they may forever share the blessing of an eternal joy, love, grace and peace with Him (Psalm 119:68, 138; Rev. 4:11). Judging from the great length that our Father went in order to save mankind from the terrible effect of sin through the sacrifice of Jesus, the worth of a human soul to God is of an inestimable value and very precious (Matt.16:26; Mark 8:36&37; Psalm 49:7-9; Jer. 29:11). It is thus the height of ingratitude for any moral creature to treat the privilege of life with levity.

II. Jesus’ Description of Hell

The revelation of hell as a place of torment came from the graphic description of our Lord and Saviour, Jesus Christ that he gave to us in the classic story of Lazarus and the rich man recorded for us in Luke16:19-31. Apart from the mention of hell in many passages of the Scripture, it is this classic story that highlighted the reality of immortality of the soul, the consciousness of souls after leaving the body, the fact of different places for the righteous and the wicked between death and the resurrection of the body and the truth of torment for the wicked and bliss for the righteous. Many religionists would like to regard this story as a parable; however Jesus here mentioned the specific name of Lazarus indicating that it is an actual experience. A careful reading of the chapter in verses 22&23 shows that this is an after-life experience. Both men died. While Lazarus was carried by angels to paradise (Abraham’s bosom), the rich man having been buried, he consciously lifted his eyes in hell. In the Old Testament, the word translated as hell is the Hebrew word ‘sheol’ which means the place of the dead, the unseen world, the place of departed spirits after they leave the bodies at physical death. This same word was translated as grave and also as pit. In the King James Version, sheol is translated as hell, 31 times, as grave 31 times, and as pit, 3 times. It is this different translations that sometimes obscure the true meaning of sheol. The New Testament equivalent of sheol is Hades. This word is translated as hell, ten times and grave, one time. The many passages on hell revealed in the Bible indicate that it is a place reserved for the wicked and those who have rejected Christ and those who reject the rule of God in their lives.

III. Clarifying Immortality

We should know that resurrection refers only to the bodies of all men who died, and not to the souls and spirits which are immortal. All scriptures on future resurrection of the dead, without exception, refer only to the bodies which die and will be resurrected from dust again. (See the followings: Dan. 12:2; John 5:28-29; I Cor. 15; I Thes.4:13-17; Rev. 20:4-6, 11-15). There will be no spiritual resurrection, that is, a resurrection of the soul and spirit. All spiritual resurrection is accomplished in this life before the body dies (Eph. 2;1-10; Col.2:11-13). If one is not resurrected spiritually from death in trespasses and sin in this life, he will remain spiritually dead or separated from God (Heb.9:27; Rev. 20:11-15). All scriptures mentioning future immortality refer to the body, not the soul, which is now and will forever be immortal.

Conclusion & Life Application

It is upon our acceptance of Christ as our Lord and Saviour that our spirits become regenerated and made alive unto God by the express work of the Holy Spirit Himself. This is not possible by any reformation of character by man. Until that regenerating work of the Holy Spirit is done in the lives of men, all are dead in trespasses and sin spiritually even though they are living a normal life as a man. Thus any man who rejected Christ is separated from God in this life and will forever be separated from him when he dies physically in that condition. Any man thus separated, will be cast into hell for his name will be missing from the Lamb’s book of life (Rev. 20:11-15, 21:27).

Questions & Discussion

  1. Why a loving God would consign some of his moral creatures to hell or eternal torment.
  1. What provision has God made to avert this terrible destiny on any man?
  1. What happens to a man’s body, soul and spirit when he dies (Luke 16:19-21)?

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

Many of the confusions arising from the issue of life after death stem from the way many Bible translators translated the word “sheol” in the Old Testament. The word for “grave” in Hebrew is “qeber” and is always translated grave, burying place or sepulchre. It is never translated as hell and this is very correct. At death, a man’s body is buried in the grave while his soul and spirit either goes to heaven, if he has already been regenerated by the Holy Spirit or to hell if he dies in his trespasses and sin.

Lesson 52

Topic: Redemption by Dedication of the Firstborn (Christmas)

Text: Exodus 13:13–15; Luke 2:22–32

Central Truth:

One way that God could cause Israel to once again remember his redemption was through the dedication of the firstborn. In this obedience our redemption is anticipated.

Memory Verse: Luke 2:22-24

Lesson Outline:

I. Remembering Redemption -Exodus 13:13b-15

II. Anticipating Redemption -Luke 2:22-24

III. Celebrating Redemption -Luke 2:25-32

Lesson Introduction

If we want to understand the Old Testament, we must understand the Exodus. As we celebrate Christmas this year, we take note of the dedication of Jesus by Mary and Joseph. The couple’s obedience to the law for their firstborn son gave a unique occasion for a prophetic celebration of the salvation that Jesus brought.

Lesson Commentary

I. Remembering Redemption -Exodus 13:13b-15

God commanded Moses to communicate to the children of Israel the law of dedication and redemption of the first son, “… and all the firstborn of man among thy children shalt thou redeem.” (Ex 13:13). The firstborn sons and male animals were to be dedicated to the LORD (Num 18:15). Because human sacrifice was unacceptable the Hebrews' sons were commanded to be "redeemed." (Ex 12:26-27; 13:8). The Egyptian firstborn were slain, in judgment, while God spared the children of Israel. The consecration of the firstborn was a sign and symbol, a reminder of God's powerful and gracious deliverance from the land of bondage.

This explains the significance of the firstborn in the nation of Israel. Not only once a year at Passover were the Jews reminded of God's grace and power, but each time a first-born male, man or beast, came into the world, that firstborn issue had to be redeemed. All the firstborn belonged to God. They were sanctified, that is, set apart for God's exclusive possession. Moses explained how it should be handled (Lev 12; Num. 18:14-19). Parents were to bring their firstborn sons to the Lord and offer the appropriate sacrifice (Lev 12:6-8). When Mary and Joseph came to the temple to redeem the Redeemer, they brought the humble sacrifice as demanded by the Law of Moses (Luke 2:21-24).

When a firstborn son was redeemed, it gave adults the opportunity to explain how God had rescued the first-born in the land of Goshen on Passover night, and how He had slain all the Egyptian first-born, both humans and livestock. Every firstborn son in a Jewish family was very special to the parents and to the Lord.

II. Anticipating Redemption -Luke 2:22-24

Jesus was circumcised on the eighth day according to the Jewish ordinance (Gen 17:12). The law also provided for the purification of the mother after forty days (Lev 12) and the redemption of their firstborn (Ex 13:1-12). Jesus fulfilled the Law (fulfilling all righteousness).

Jesus was brought to Jerusalem, to present Him to the Lord. All the first-born males had been claimed as "holy to the Lord," or set apart to sacred uses. This was in memory of the deliverance of the first-born of Israel from destruction in Egypt, through the sprinkling of blood (Ex 13:2). It was in obedience to the "law of Moses," that Joseph and Mary presented their baby unto the Lord, 'in the east gate of the court called Nicanor's Gate, where she herself would be sprinkled by the priest with the blood of her sacrifice'. However, this was a shadow of what to come, in due time, we were to be redeemed, "not with corruptible things as silver and gold, but with the precious blood of Christ" (1 Peter 1:18-19).

In obedience to the law, they offered a sacrifice according to that which is said in the law of the Lord, a pair of turtle doves, or two young pigeons. The proper sacrifice was a lamb for a burnt offering, and a turtle-dove or young pigeon for a sin offering. But if a lamb could not be afforded, the mother was to bring two turtle-doves or two young pigeons; and if even this was beyond the family means, then a portion of fine flour, but without the usual fragrant accompaniments of oil and frankincense, because it represented a sin offering (Lev. 12:6-8; 5:7-11). From this we gather that our Lord's parents were in poor circumstances (2 Cor. 8:9), and yet not in abject poverty; as they neither brought the lamb, nor availed themselves of the provision for the poorest, but presented the intermediate offering of "a pair of turtle-doves, or two young pigeons."

III. Celebrating Redemption -Luke 2:25-32

When baby Jesus was presented at the temple in Jerusalem, there was a remnant of believing Jews, waiting for their Redeemer (Luke2: 25-32, 38); Simeon and Anna were among them. The Spirit of God both taught and led Simeon, so he was right there when Mary and Joseph came with their baby. The remarkable thing about his hymn of praise is that he included the Gentiles! He was now ready to die because he had seen the Messiah with his own eyes. Simeon blessed God, and he also blessed Mary and Joseph; but he did not bless the baby, because Jesus is the source of every blessing. Mary did feel "the sword" in her heart repeatedly as she watched her Son during His ministry and then stood at the cross where He died (John 19:25-27).

Mary and Joseph marvelled by the words of Simeon. Though they had been told that their Son was the Messiah, perhaps they had not comprehended the scope of His ministry (to the entire world - to the Gentiles as well as to the people of Israel). Simeon revealed to Mary that her Son would be opposed (a sign... spoken against) and that she would be hurt greatly (Luke 2:34-35). Her grief would be like a sword piercing her soul. The Son would cause the falling and rising of many in Israel. Throughout His ministry Jesus proclaimed that the only way to the kingdom, something the nation had long sought, was to follow Him. The ones who did so would receive salvation; they would "rise." But the ones who did not believe Him would not receive salvation; they would "fall". Simeon came by the Spirit into the temple, probably to fulfil the prophecy of Malachi 3:1, “The Lord, whom ye seek, shall suddenly come to his temple”- a light of the Gentiles.

Conclusion & Life Application:

God delivered the ordinance of dedication and redemption of the firstborn son to the children of Israel for implementation in the land of promise (Ex 13:11). The firstborn sons and male animals were to be dedicated to the LORD (Num. 18:15).

We learnt from the example of Christ's conformity to that aspect of the law. Viewed simply in the light of obedience, He has left us an example that we should follow His steps (1 Peter 2:21). But as He was made under the law only to redeem them that were under the law, His obedience was more than voluntary -it was strictly self- imposed obedience; and since it is by the obedience of this One that many are made righteous (Rom 5:19), it had throughout, and in every part of it, a substitutionary character, which made it altogether unique. The glorious child of God was born in a lowly state. The earthly parents were so poor that they could not afford a lamb for a burnt offering on His presentation in the temple. So, our poor background should not have negative influence on us or hinder our vision.

Questions & Discussion

1. Explain the ordinance of redemption in the Old Testament?

2. How does this ordinance translate in the New Testament?

3. Discuss the role of Jesus as our substitute.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

In the law of redemption, animals were included because "they too benefited from the redemption which God provided in the 10th plague". Since donkeys were considered ceremonially unclean animals (Lev 11:2-4) they could not be sacrificed, but they could be redeemed, "to buy back for a price" by lambs sacrificed in their place. Furthermore, since human sacrifice was unacceptable, the Hebrews' sons were also to be "redeemed" (Ex 12:26-27; 13:8). When the Egyptian firstborn sons were slain in judgment, the Israelite firstborn sons were redeemed by animals- an act of substitutionary sacrifice or redemption (for the sons). Concerning the Jewish race, one whole tribe, that of Levi, was accepted, and set apart to occupations exclusively sacred (Num 3:11-38). It was in obedience to this "law of Moses," that Mary presented her baby (Jesus).

Jesus established the Lord's Supper after He had led His disciples in celebrating Passover, for He is the fulfilment of the Passover as the Lamb of God who died for the sins of the world. Each time we share in the Lord's Supper, we look back and remember His death, and we also look ahead in anticipation of His coming again. When Jesus returns, a wonderful exodus will take place! The dead in Christ will be raised and the living believers will be caught up with them and taken to heaven to be with the Lord (1 Thess. 4:13-18). Hallelujah, what a Saviour!

Lesson 53

Topic: Preparations for Our Home in Heaven

Text: John 14:1-6, 2Corin 5:1-8; Philip 1:21-24; I Thess. 5:1-9, 15-23 Rev. 7:15-17

Supplemental Ref. Matt. 24:30-44

Central Truth :

The hope of heaven serves as a powerful incentive to Christian service and victorious living.

Memory Verse: John 14:1-3

Lesson Outline:

I. Our Present Home On Earth (1Corin. 5:1-8)

II. A Glimpse Of Our Home In Heaven (John 14: 1-6; Rev 7:15-17)

III. Need To, And How To Prepare For (I Thess. 5:1-9, 15-23)

Our Home In Heaven.

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that heaven is the habitation of the living God and the eternal home of born again believers (John 14:2; Revelation 7:15-17).

Lesson Commentary

The import of this lesson is a stark reminder that this world is not our home but heaven is our home. We are but strangers and pilgrims, or put in another way round, pilgrim and sojourners on earth (Heb. 11:13). Simply put, we are tenants in this world, and our sojourn is not forever. By way of analogy, it is comparable to a farmer, who goes to his farm early in the morning. At sunset, after the day’s enervating tilling of the land, he returns to his hut for a sweet rest, to recuperate his exhausted energy and enjoy his delicious meal. Thus, bearing this in mind, we should live soberly, while in our home on earth, do the good we can do without prevarication or vacillation. We should work while it is day time, for the night comes when nobody can work (John 9:4). This makes it imperative for us to bear in mind Ps. 90:12, to wit, “Teach us to number our days that we may gain a heart of wisdom.” We need to gain a heart of wisdom so as to know that our citizenship is in heaven! (Philip 3:20)

I. Our Present Home On Earth (2Corin. 5:1-8 John 14:1-2)

Our present earthly home is highly vulnerable. It is full of many deceivers (II John 7) who do not teach the true doctrine, thus, turning away people’s mind from Jesus Christ as the One who came in the flesh. It is also full of false prophets (I John 4:1) who prophesy out of their own imaginations. They deceive, they lie and they seduce to turn the hearts of the inhabitants of the earthly home from God. Just like sly foxes find homes among the ruins of cities, so also the false prophets find profit in our present home on earth (Ezek. 13:3). Our present home on earth is one that is permeated with evil (Hab. 1:3). It is one full of violence, strife, contentions, injustice and lawlessness. The wicked take delight/ relish in oppressing the righteous. Our present home on earth is akin to “poor cottages of clay”, in which our souls dwell, which are decaying, and whose foundations are in the dust, and susceptible to destruction without prior notification.

It is therefore not surprising that in this earthly home, believers are groaning, a groaning of sorrow under a heavy burden of load. Our earthly home is also one situated in a “wicked and perverse” environment.

As believers we are pilgrims and strangers in the world (Heb. 11:13). We sojourn here in our earthly home, walking by faith and not by sight, as we earnestly yearn for our home in heaven.

The troubles of this earthly home are diverse. We have worries of life (Matt: 6:25). We also have the worries of tomorrow (Matt 6:34). These worries make our spirits to be troubled, our souls to be troubled, as well as our hearts to be relentlessly troubled!

II. Glimpse Of Our Home In Heaven (John 14:1-6; Rev. 7:15-17)

Our Lord Jesus Christ told us, as His followers, when He told His disciples that Heaven is their everlasting rest (John 14:2)

He said he was going to prepare the place for them. This implies that our home is in heaven, and not on this earth where we are presently. Christ also said that He would certainly come back in due time, to fetch the disciples which also includes us as His believers to that blessed place which He was going to possess for Himself and prepare for us (John 14: 3).

The question now arises as to how this home in heaven looks like, that we have to be “specially” prepared to be put in possession of it. From the lenses and hopes of a believer, heaven is perceived as a ‘House’ or ‘Habitation’.

The heavenly home is a place of joyful service, and not of suffering (Rev. 7:15). It is also a home where the inhabitants are happy in their freedom from all the inconveniences experienced by the inhabitants of the earthly home. The Lord Jesus Christ ensures that we will have in possession everything that is pleasant to our souls. Thus, we shall no longer experience hunger and thirst. For the Great Provider will provide for all their needs through His riches in glory (Philip 4:19; Rev.7:17). Need we say more, except to add that the occupants of this heavenly home will also be free from all forms of sickness and pain? They are never to be scourged by the heat of the sun anymore (Rev. 7:16). The book of Revelation chapter 7 tells us that those whose home is in heaven are those that see themselves as being in low and desolate state (Matt.5) but because they are pure in heart and hunger and thirst after righteousness (Matt. 5: 8, 6) they become the possessors of the home in heaven.

Another trademark of the inhabitants of the heavenly home is that they had been persecuted by men for no just purpose. But the good thing is that as good and faithful followers of Christ, they did not suffer in vain, since their suffering was not for murder or stealing and they did not suffer for being evil doers, or for being busy-bodies in other men’s matters (I Pet. 4:15).

It must be stressed however, that the way to the home in heaven is not a smooth one, but one that is bumpy and “tarred” with many tribulations. But when successfully passed through, it will make the Home in heaven more glorious and more comfortably welcome.

From the foregoing glimpses, it is manifestly clear that our Home in heaven is a place to be desired, a place to strive for with all ‘spiritual weapons’. It is a place to be prepared for with unrelenting vigour and undiminished determination.

III. Need To, And How To Prepare For Our Home In Heaven ( 1 Thess. 5:1-9 )

Having had a glimpse of what our home in heaven looks like in the preceding section, there is no need for planning to be there. Planning, in this sense, is deciding in advance what to do to be counted worthy of occupying our home in heaven.

In our text, Apostle Paul pointed out to us the uselessness or the needlessness of enquiring about the exact time of our being “asked to come” and occupy the mansion. He disclosed that the “call” will be sudden and terrible for the wicked, but joyfully comfortable for the righteousness (Saint).

The following should be our guide in planning for our home in heaven. First, we should be watchful and be sober.

Second, we should exercise faith, love and hope. That is, we are admonished to live by faith, so as to remain watchful and sober.

Third, we should not indulge in spiritual sloth and idleness.

Fourth, we must be on guard against sin and temptation to sin.

Fifth, we should be well armed, by putting on the whole armour of God.

Sixth, we must get a heart inflamed with love.

Seventh, we should be on our knees often, praying without ceasing (I Thess. 5:17) without fainting and without weariness.

Eight, we must be careful not to quench the Holy Spirit (vs. 19).

Ninth, we should abstain from every appearance of evil and from being deceived by false doctrines (vs 22).

Tenth, we should abstain from sin, and everything that looks like sin, leads to sin and borders on sin.

Eleventh, we should endeavour to maintain purity of heart and integrity of life.

Twelfth, we should beware of false teachers, who are hypocrites and liars! (I Tim. 4:1-2). Preparations is inexhaustible, it will not be out of place to supplement with a few more. Any believer, yearning for the upward call to the everlasting home in heaven, must turn from godless living. He must also turn from sinful pleasures and lusts like lust of the flesh, lust of the eye, and pride of life (II John 2:15-16).

Conclusion & Life Application

This lesson is to arouse us from our spiritual slumber and spiritual lethargy. It is to make us to be on our guard, because we don’t know what day the Lord will come “for the Son of Man will come when least expected.” Therefore, we must be prepared at all time. For us to get prepared, the lesson is divided into three inter-related sections. The first section is a brief but insightful analysis of our present home on earth, pointing out that our sojourn therein is brief and snappy. The second section is a pre-bird’s eye-view of what our home in heaven looks like. It points out some of the “goodies” therein as our “everlasting home.

The third section is about planning to take possession of the home in Heaven, as a “palace that far excels all the palaces on earth” including that of wise and famous king Solomon of the Old Testament fame. This section gives a full insight into what sort of planning and preparations to be made so as to be found worthy, and so as not to be caught unawares, panting profusely.

Questions & Discussion

1. Compare and contrast our home on earth with our home in heaven.

2. The way and manner we live in our homes on earth is an indication of whether we will possess or be dispossessed of our homes in heaven. Discuss.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

In teaching this lesson, our students should be asked to distinguish between the characteristic features of the home on earth vis-à-vis the home in heaven. They should also be asked to take stock of the behavioural and attitudinal dispositions of the present inhabitants of the earthly home. They should be asked to put this side by side with the characteristic features of the heavenly home earlier undertaken.
