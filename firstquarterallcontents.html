Lesson 1

A Model for the New Year

Text: Luke 2:13-20

Central Truth:

God is satisfied with us only when we are in His Son and when we allow His Son to live His life through us.

Memory Verse : Ephesians 2:10

Lesson Outline

I. Response of The Heavenly Host (2:13-14)

II. Response And Actions Of The Shepherds, Mary, And Others (2:15-20)

Lesson Introduction:

We come to the new year of 2017. On behalf of the Leadership, I welcome you and your loved ones into this brand new year. Most people generally look at the New Year as a fresh start, as an opportunity to overcome a habit, or do better on something they count worthwhile and which needs to be improved upon. It may be serving as a parent, being a better marriage partner, building or buying a house. It may be developing different priorities, using and managing our time better, or whatever things that people would like to change. So, people all over the world talk about New Year's resolutions; resolutions which are usually broken in a matter of weeks. Many people are very pessimistic about the whole issue of making resolutions, and they may be right because many resolutions that have so many good intentions are followed by failures. They crash-land within the first quarter of the year.

Regardless of such failures, the New Year is a time to reflect and think about what the past year has brought and what the New Year could bring. It is a time to stop and analyze, to take stock of our priorities, values, pursuits, and goals. As Christians, it is a time to seriously think about our stewardship of time, talents and treasures. This is very important because God has so much planned our lives and our stewardship to revolve around and through the life of His Son, Jesus Christ. On this note, I implore you to take this year seriously as we all know that from every indication, our redemption draws nearer than we think. The responses of those concerned in our lesson of today matter to us in making resolutions.

Lesson Commentary:

I. Response of The Heavenly Host (2:13-14)

A. The fact and time of the response:

Immediately after the announcement in verses 11-12, there was a heavenly response by a host of angels. This certainly sets an example for us humans for whom Christ was born. Christmas and all of life should be a time of thankful praise to God for the person and work of Jesus Christ if one truly understands the significance of His life and death to both God and man (Eph. 3:10; I Tim. 3:16; I Pet. 1:12).

B. The nature of the response:

The response was an act of praise and adoration to God manifesting at least three things:

1. It recognized God as the source or origin of the birth of Christ, the supreme gift of God, which manifested God's glory.

2. It revealed the key outcome to mankind, which is peace to men as follows:

a) Peace with God (reconciliation from enmity to harmony).

b) The peace of God (of fellowship and provision).

c) The peace of harmony and unity with one another.

d) Universal peace through the reign of Christ on earth.

3. It revealed the recipients of this peace; those who know His Son by faith and walk in fellowship with Him.

II. Response and Actions of The Shepherds, Mary And Others (2:15-20)

A. The Circumstances and Time of the Response:

Revelation from God always demands an adequate response in man. This means we become even more accountable before God according to the revelation we have.

This news and information became the center of the Shepherds’ conversation. Likewise, the Sunday morning message, for instance, could become a topic for the family on their ride home or at the dinner table.

B. The Character of Their Response (2:15b-16)

1. They Spoke Excitedly

It means they began and continued to speak with one another as men who were excited about what they had heard.

2. They Spoke Reciprocally

This demonstrates how we are social beings and how we need to give and receive input from one another. Two principles are involved here:

No man is an island, we need others and others need us.

We need to share Jesus Christ and talk of Him with one another.

3. They Spoke Encouragingly

This gives us a good example of how in this setting we need the exhortation, comfort, encouragement, challenge, and sometimes loving rebuke of others. We need it from others and they need it from us.

4. They Acted Purposefully

Without biblical goals based on biblical values, people never go straight. As we face this New Year, with our compass in hand (our Bibles), let's examine our lives. Where have we been and where are we going? What kind of goals do we have and should we have? Let us chart our course and set some new goals based on the values and priorities of the Word of God.

5. They Acted Immediately

They were determined, undeviating, and non-procrastinating. As a result of this action, the shepherds found their way to Mary and Joseph, and the baby. And as a result, their lives would never be the same.

6. They Acted Insightfully

They not only saw Him with their eyes, but they went away with spiritual insight having seen the Lord, who was announced to them by the angels. What can we learn from their example?

First, we each need the same kind of interest and hunger for spiritual things that led these men to go and search for Jesus Christ.

Second, God rewarded their efforts to go and see the Christ Child. And so He does with us.

Third, knowing Jesus Christ, seeing and understanding spiritual truth, gives insight for living. It renews our minds and enables us to re-evaluate our lives, our reasons for living, our values, goals, priorities, and the whole works.

7. They Acted Evangelistically

Here is the most important information in the world that needs to be told and shared. When something good happens to us, we generally can't wait to tell others.

8. Mary Acted Reflectively

The word "pondered" indicates a continuous action. Pondering the things of Christ must become our practice, a constant part of our lives.

In this, Mary gives us an example of the principle of meditating on the things of God that we have read, studied, heard, and memorized for better understanding and application. Why do we learn the Word? So that we might reflect on it for greater understanding and application.

Let me say here that biblical meditation is gradually becoming a lost art. People rarely take time for meditating on God's Word. They simply do not have the right perspective and time for it. We seem to think only from the mind-set of activity and business.

9. They Acted Worshipfully

These men returned with a song in their hearts and praise on their lips. Their lives would now be filled with a new dimension of worship and dramatically transformed life. These shepherds were common men living very common and ordinary lives.

Conclusion & Life Application:

Let's not fail to note that they went back to their sheep, to their occupation, and undoubtedly also to their families; but they went back changed men.

As we face this New Year; how are we going to respond to the messages of Scripture? What difference is it going to make in our lives? Remember that it takes two to make a good message (the bearer and the hearer) that reaches our hearts and has an effect on our lives.

Every January, millions of people begin their year with New Year’s resolutions on different things; but what about making spiritual New Year’s resolutions?

We make commitments and tell our friends and families on January 1st, only to give it all up by January 3rd. But as humans, our struggle for consistency goes way beyond resolutions. Making commitments we can’t keep runs all through society, back to Biblical times. Life is not always easy. Following through on simple commitments can even be difficult when bombarded by every day affairs. In addition, the attacks of the evil one will challenge us at every turn.

In the Bible, James spoke of some people who can teach us a valuable lesson about great intentions that aren’t aligned with God’s will (Jam.4:13-17).

Questions & Discussion:

How about us?

· Can people see a difference in our lives because we have been

with the Saviour?

· Are we preparing our hearts for the Word so that there can be a

proper response when we study or assemble together?

· Do we get excited about our Lord and what God has done in Christ and wants to do in us, or has it become an old hat?

· Do we talk with one another about Him, or are the majority of our conversation on other things?

· Are we goal oriented, undeviating and determined in our pursuit to meet with the Saviour in the Word; or are we turned aside by other things because of wrong priorities?

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

The preceding verses, 2:1-12, give us the account of the birth of the Saviour with the revelation of His birth to the shepherds by an angel of the Lord. Our study passage of Luke 2:13-20 gives us a tremendous precedent for the New Year and the kind of issues we might consider. We would like to focus our attention on what followed the announcement to the shepherds (vss. 13-20). In these verses, we see both heaven and earth responding to the news of the birth of Jesus Christ in such a way that it exhorts and challenges us in our lives: in our attitudes, priorities, values, pursuits, and actions as it pertains to worship. It also challenges us as to our behaviour towards spiritual things and even, our reasons for living.

The response by the shepherds as seen in this passage sets a precedent on how we should respond in view of the person and work of the Lord Jesus. The response and actions of the angels and the shepherds to the news of the birth of Christ provide some tremendous lessons and examples for us. Let us note that first, it was not the angels that excited the shepherds, but the news, which they viewed in faith as from God. Second, Scripture teaches us that it is not miraculous experiences that change men, it's the Word and the Spirit of God (Luke 16:27-31). The response of the shepherds was the result of the proclamation of truth (Scripture). This gives a good analogy for us to hear the Word on a regular basis, and to act on what we learn and know.

Better Resolutions

Our plans may or may not be God’s plans. If we want to be successful with any New Year’s resolution, we must seek God’s will.

God would be honoured if we made spiritual resolutions.

Read the Bible, get involved in ministry at church, share your faith with friends, take a step of faith that is so big that only God can make it happen. Resolve to represent him no matter the cost.

Best Resolutions

Here are a few possible resolutions we might consider.

Seek God’s face, and pray David’s prayer every morning for the next year (Ps.51:10-11).

Let’s resolve to give our attitudes to the Lord.

Commit to making a difference at home, in the workplace, the marketplace, on the road, and even at church.

You could make Colossians 3:14-15 your resolution text.

Lesson 2

The Fall of Man-Part 1(Origin and Nature of Man)

Text: Genesis 1:26-31

Supplemental References: Gen. 1:27; 6:12; 3:9-19; Rom. 5:15-19; 8:7; 1:18,32; 2:1-16; Jn.3:6; Ps. 51:5; Is. 53:6; Eph. 2:3; Mt. 20:15; Gal. 3:10; Ez.18:19,20.

Central Truth:

Man is wonderfully and fearfully created

Memory Verse : Genesis 2:7

Lesson Outline:

I. God Created Man To Reflect His Image.

II. God Created Man To Rule Over Creation.

III. God Created Man To Reproduce Godly Offspring.

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that man was created in the image of God, before whom he walked in holiness and purity, but that by voluntary disobedience and transgression, he fell from the Eden of purity and innocence to the depths of sin and iniquity, and that in consequence of this, all mankind are sinners sold unto Satan, sinners not by constraint but by choice, shapened in iniquity and utterly void by nature of that holiness required by the law of God, positively inclined to evil, guilty and without excuse, justly deserving the condemnation of a just and holy God.

Lesson Commentary:

I. God created Mankind to reflect His image(Gen.1:26-27)

The repetition of the plural pronouns in reference to God here is very striking: “Let Us make man in Our image, according to Our likeness” (1:26). Some Jewish scholars had explained this away from the truth. They said God was talking to the angels. But Scripture is clear that God did not consult the angels when He created man (Isa.40:12-26; 44:24). The plural pronoun here teaches us the doctrine of the Trinity which is revealed later in the revelation of that doctrine. Here we see a consultation among the persons of the Godhead prior to the creation of man. There is one God, but He exists in three eternal, co-equal Persons; the same in substance, but distinct in subsistence. This is impossible for our finite minds to grasp. But it is the clear revelation of Scripture. Man in God’s image is one (“him”) and yet is plural (“male and female”); and in body, soul and spirit.

Another thing is that, it is affirmed three times over (1:27) that God created man “in His image” which is emphasized twice. There is no room for harmonizing the Genesis account with an evolutionary origin of man as some scientists had propounded. God created man distinct from animals. Only man is made in God’s image. We are made in the image of God by reflecting God-likeness in a limited way with His personality, intelligence, and ability to know and relate (Eph.4:24; Col.3:10).

Also, part of the divine image involves the submission of the Son to the Father in the context of equality. The Father and the Son are equal in personhood, both possessing all the attributes of deity. But in order to carry out the divine purpose, the Son voluntarily submitted to the Father.

The fact that human beings have been created in God’s image has many practical implications.

First, unless you are rightly related to the Creator, your life has no lasting purpose. You are born, grow up, live a few years trying to make a comfortable existence, but your body too soon grows old and you die (assuming you don’t die sooner)!

That every human, male and female, is created in God’s image also means that human life is valuable and every person must be treated with respect. The unborn baby is not to be killed because it is not convenient to have a child, or because the parents prefer having a boy instead of a girl. Even if that child is deformed or mentally deficient, it is still human life, valuable in God’s sight. At the other end of life, the elderly, even those who can no longer think clearly, must be treated with dignity and care. Abortion and euthanasia cheapen the value of human life. This doctrine also is the basis for treating women with the same respect granted to men, because the text is clear that the female, as well as the male, is created in the divine image.

II. God created Mankind to rule over creation (Gen.1:28)

This passage speaks of dominion. God gave man the right of dominion over all living things. The command to subdue it (1:28) implies that there was work involved, even in the perfect setting of the Garden, to bring the creation under man’s rightful dominion. This dominion involves a stewardship of the earth and its resources under the sovereignty of God.

Man ruled creation before the fall. But when Satan got man to obey him, then Satan became the ruler of this world. For man to regain his rightful place of dominion over this world, he must exercise dominion not only over the material world, but also over the spiritual forces of darkness (Eph. 6:10-20). This can only be gained by becoming a member of the body of Christ, the Head, who through His resurrection has been elevated to the place of dominion over all things (Eph. 1:19-23). And so a major part of our purpose as the church is to exercise dominion for Christ over Satan and his forces through spiritual warfare.

The practical implication of our ruling over creation is that we must put on the full armour of God and, especially, become people of prayer (Eph. 6:13-17, esp. 18-19). So God put us on this earth to reflect His image and to rule over creation.

III. God created Man to reproduce godly offspring (Gen.1 :28-31)

“Be fruitful and multiply, and fill the earth, and subdue it” (1:28). This is both God’s pronouncement of blessing as well as His delegation of responsibility. The blessing of sharing in God’s creative process by producing offspring was given to other living things besides man (1:22, 24-25). But man alone was commanded not only to fill the earth, but also to subdue it. This requires not only bearing children, but rearing godly children who will properly subdue the earth under God. By producing ungodly children, man only fills the earth but does not subdue it.

In applying this verse to our present day, the Roman Catholics have taken it to mean that birth control is wrong and that the major purpose of marriage is to bear children. Also, many Protestants would advocate not practicing any form of artificial birth control but to have as many children as possible. But we need to be careful to apply the verse correctly. I saw in the social media today, a picture of a couple with 13 boys but still want to continue until they will have a girl. What if God does not give them a girl, should the woman go on until she wears out and die?

Clearly, the text does not mean that every person must get married and have children to fulfil God’s purpose. Neither Jesus nor the apostle Paul would have qualified if it means that. But it means that children are to be viewed as blessings from God. That needs to be said in our day when many couples choose not to have children so they can selfishly pursue their careers and materialistic lifestyles. Our children are one of the greatest blessings and biggest responsibilities God entrusts to us. We need to take the time and effort to see each child come to know Christ and to be trained in His ways. I pray God will put increase into our efforts of raising godly children. Mankind cannot reflect God’s image, rule over His creation, and reproduce godly offspring unless they live under the lordship of Jesus Christ.

Conclusion & Life Application:

God made us individually as male and female in our marriages and corporately as His church, to reflect His image by being godly people. He made us to rule over His creation as responsible stewards of the earth, and to rule over the ruler of this fallen world as we exercise the authority of Christ our Head through prayer. He made us to reproduce godly offspring, both in our families and in our church family through bearing spiritual children. That’s why God created people. You will be restless, confused, or lacking in fulfilment until you begin living in line with God’s purpose for creating you.

Questions & Discussion

  1. Does our being created in the image of God provide a biblical basis for “self-esteem”? Why/why not?
  1. If someone argued that seriously deformed babies are not made in God’s image, how would you answer? What Scriptures apply?
  1. To what extent are spiritual forces behind everyday events? Should we attribute all problems to demonic influence?
  1. Some Christians think that all artificial means of birth control are wrong. Agree/disagree? Support your position biblically.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

In the beginning, God created; that is, God worked. But God worked for the joy and pleasure of it (Gen.1:31a).

As soon as God created Adam and Eve, He commissioned them to continue the creative work He began through procreation (vv. 26–28). Here, God commands humankind to have dominion and subdue the created world. God commissions Adam and Eve into the work of creating and caring for the earth. This is an example of power sharing. The Hebrew term for dominion (radâ) means caregiving and nurturing, not exploitation.

Similarly, the Hebrew term for subdue (kabaš) refers to developing the created order. Thus, God commands us to nurture and develop the world to its fullest potential. He calls us to continue developing what He first created. Work, as modelled by God in Genesis, is a creative, caring process. God’s expression of work includes power sharing with others. Lastly, work should create opportunities for humankind and creation to flourish in the dominion power.

Lesson 3

Topic: The Fall of Man-Part 2 (Sin entered human race)

Text: Gen. 3:1-6; Jam. 1:14-15

Supplemental References: Is.53:5, 55:7;1:18; Tit.2:14; Heb. 7:25

Central Truth:

Man’s choice brought sin into human race.

Memory Verse: James 1:14-15

Lesson Outline

I. Immediate Results Of Their Sin

II. Long Term Results Of Their Sin

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that man was created in the image of God, before whom he walked in holiness and purity, but that by voluntary disobedience and transgression, he fell from the Eden of purity and innocence to the depths of sin and iniquity, and that in consequence of this, all mankind are sinners sold unto Satan, sinners not by constraint but by choice, shapened in iniquity and utterly void by nature of that holiness required by the law of God, positively inclined to evil, guilty and without excuse, justly deserving the condemnation of a just and holy God.

Lesson Commentary:

I. Immediate Results Of Their Sin

A. Eyes were opened (Genesis 3:7)

They got to know good and evil and in that sense became like God (3:22). So, the serpent’s promise came true, but they got MORE. While God has knowledge of good and evil, evil never has a controlling part. This shows God’s remarkable will and holiness! But for man, this knowledge of good and evil came by becoming subject to the control of evil, transfer of their rulership to Satan and loss of the very ideal of life as God designed it.

In knowing good and evil, they lost the free access to the tree of life, which if they had not eaten would have lived forever.

The irony is that the excellence of their faculties was infinitely depressed. This is where mankind is today. He does not even have a proper perspective of God, His creation, the future, goodness and the other attributes God originally gave to man. His will, mind and heart were all at once stained. Man might consider himself good, but just by observing the world in general or a person in particular, the debate is quickly over. With ample proof, man has fallen into a world of sin.

B. Guilt, shame, loss of innocence.

Considering all that resulted from the fall of man, one can list the following as some of such results which emanated from their disobedience relating to their guilt.

1. They knew they were naked (Genesis 3:7 cp. 2:25)

They were ashamed. At least, they were honest about that. They tried to cover up their nakedness with sewed fig leaves. God considered it inadequate and provided better set of clothes with the animal skin (Gen.3:21).

2. They hid from God’s presence (Genesis 3:8 -10)

They had to hide because of sin. Sin does not make us want to go to God as it brings a fear of God’s presence (distinct from the healthy fear that aspires into God’s presence). “For everyone who does evil hates the light, and does not come to the light, lest his deeds should be exposed” (John 3:20).

3. They passed the blame (Genesis 3:12)

Other consequences include blaming others (‘buck-passing’) and denial of one’s personal responsibility. This hiding of sin is just another form of hiding from God. One is not willing to admit to one’s fault unless he feels hopelessly exposed. It is much easier to point someone’s attention to another than to admit that what has been done was due to one’s own wrong doing.

4. They deceived and made excuses (Genesis 3:13)

The woman also passes the buck but at least admits that she was deceived. Unfortunately, this could be noticed only after the deception had worked its end; when it was too late to change the fact. Usually, this is the only time people will confess their sins when they are confronted and know there is no escape.

C. Deception

This is the essence of the enemy of God and of our souls!

Deceit is admission to inferiority. The serpent is evil because he deceives to get what he otherwise could not have. The serpent only could bait mankind through deceit. It was through deceit that he himself fell and used it to seed rebellion among mankind. So, what did the serpent get for his deception? He got control which was only for a short time, “He must be released for a short time” (Revelation 20:3) before his catastrophic and humiliating end. The light by nature is powerful where darkness is only present because of an absence of light. The evil one therefore only can continue as he continues his dark understanding. When God’s truth is revealed, he wilts away.

The death they experienced could in no way be compared to the fruit of obedience. The contrast is so great that we can hardly comprehend why not only Eve but Adam fell into disobedience. The unfolding of the ‘surely die’ will continue on in the following chapters. There is a physical death that is delayed. Spiritual death took place instantly. This death pervades through human history. Only in Christ Jesus do we see God interrupting this cycle of death with life in Christ.

II. Long term results of their sin

Adam subjected all his progeny to the dominion of sin for eternity. In fact, we shall see later that it is God, in His wisdom, mercy and grace, who immediately took steps to prevent this from happening in a way that could not be reversed (Genesis 3:23-24).

A. Man was chased out of the Garden

God gave him orders to depart immediately; sent or put him away as a man does his wife, when he divorces her; or as a prince banishes a rebellious subject. However, he did not send him to hell at once, as he did the apostate angels.

B. Man was to eat out of his sweat

Man became a tiller of the ground, from where he was taken and where he will return in order for him to eat and get provisions. He must labour hard, in digging and ploughing, in planting and sowing, so that he might get a livelihood. He was driven to a field of land probably near Eden. Some say it was a field near Damascus.

C. Man has fallen

We see the shame, the loss of the glory of God, rebellion and disconnect. The Lord in His wonderful and gracious way has made a way through Christ where our stain is not only removed, but we gain the righteousness of Christ through which we can live as if we are righteous. Only when we see Him, however, will this redemptive process be fully completed to His glory and grace.

Conclusion & Life Application:

In choosing to ignore God while exercising their free will, Adam and Eve suffered the very consequences of God’s warning. Man is responsible to God even though he has a free will. With freedom, there is responsibility. With responsibility, there is accountability. As a people, we need to trust Him enough to obey Him or be consumed by our desires to gain what is vain though inordinate ambition.

If only Eve and Adam had taken God at His word, they would not have fallen. They only had to put the Lord and His will above their own, and all would have been fine. In making decisions, small or great, we need to persistently prioritize putting the clearly defined will and principles of God in action. God will never ask us to set aside His will to obtain His promises (John 17:17). Natural man is no longer innocent but fallen. However, in Christ, the second Adam, through faith, the people of God are alive and able to live in obedience.

Discussion Questions

1. Why would anyone deceive another?

2. What other effects of sin can be enumerated?

Teaching Overview/Insight

The Fall of Man

Volumes have been written about the fall of man. Man was made in the image of God. People differently state what that means largely because it depends upon what they think man lost. If for example, we consider the ability to make decisions through reason is part of that image of God, then we will believe man has not completely lost the image of God after the fall. He can still make such decisions even though they are based after a corrupt will. The basic argument is whether man has completely lost the image of God or partially lost this image. Again, one’s interpretation of image of God largely defines a person’s conclusion in whether he has completely or partially lost the image of God.

Fortunately, Colossians 3:10 lets us know that we now are being renewed in that image as Christ lives in us (Colossians 3:10). John tells us, however, that this image will not be completed until we see Christ (1 John 3:2).

Lesson 4

Topic: The Result of the Fall of Man

Text: Genesis 3:14-19; Romans 5:12-21; 6:23; Ezekiel 3:18; I John 3:4-9

Supplemental References: Romans 1:9-31; 3:9-12

Central Truth: Sin is the transgression of the law.

Memory Verse: Romans 5:12

Lesson Outline:

I. Physical & Spiritual Death of Mankind

II. Changes in The Earth, Its Life forms and in the Serpent

III. There Is Continual & Bitter Conflict On the Earth

IV. Women subjection to Pains at Birth and to their Husbands

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that man was created in the image of God, before whom he walked in holiness and purity, but that by voluntary disobedience and transgression, he fell from the Eden of purity and innocence to the depths of sin and iniquity, and that in consequence of this, all mankind are sinners sold unto Satan, sinners not by constraint but by choice, shapened in iniquity and utterly void by nature of that holiness required by the law of God, positively inclined to evil, guilty and without excuse, justly deserving the condemnation of a just and holy God.

Lesson Commentary:

The vast majority of people believe that the earth has always been as it is today. They maintain that in order to determine what happened in the past, one must presume that the earth always functioned basically as it is now with conflict and death, vastly different climates, radical changes in the weather, catastrophes, natural disasters, survival of the fittest and such like. But the Bible, however, refutes this false presupposition. When God completed His 6-day creation process, He declared that everything was "very good", (Gen 1:31). But then, when Adam and Eve disobeyed God, mankind and the earth were decreed by God to reflect mankind's fallen condition (Gen. 3:14-19).

I. Physical & Spiritual Death of Mankind

At first Adam was warned by God that disobedience would lead to physical and spiritual death (Gen. 2:16-17). The word, "you will surely die!" can also be seen in I Sam. 14:44; 22:16 and 1 Kgs. 2:37, and the intended meaning is one of emphatically declaring the death of an individual. And because of the two natures of the man who was created in the image of God - physical and spiritual- this emphatic certainty of death implies two kinds of death, evidently immediate spiritual death; then eventually, physical death (Gen. 2:7). Adam did not immediately die physically, but he began to die gradually. He lived to be 930 years old (Gen. 5:5). Adam's immediate spiritual death, which is spiritual separation from God, is evident in Scripture when he and Eve attempted to hide from God who they used to understand as Omniscient, which means all kn owing. No man can hide from Him (Gen. 3:8).

So, "on that very day" (Gen 2:17), Adam and Eve died spiritually as evidenced by their trying to hide from an Omniscient God, thus showing an ignorance and an alienation from Him, whereas before, they were in an intimate relationship with Him. And "on that day" Adam, as the representative of the human race, ensured that all of humanity would be born spiritually dead and that all new born babies would inherit a sin nature and spiritual death through his seed as it passed on from generation to generation. And so, mankind and nature were sadly changed. Man's offspring would now reflect this change so that no longer could God treat man as He did before (Gen 3:22-23).The Lord God banished him from the Garden to work the ground from which he had been taken."

II. Changes in The Earth, Its Life forms and in the Serpent

Then everything changed, mankind, all living things and the whole earth itself. So, the passage of Gen. 3:14-19 which is now under consideration starts with God's pronouncement on the serpent which serves the purpose of reminding future generations of Satan's continual deceptions and mankind's fallen condition. Part of this pronouncement includes God's curse on mankind, as well as on plant and anim al life and on the earth itself (Gen. 3:14). It might not 'eat dust' in a literal sense, of course, except in the sense that its prey would have to be consumed directly off the ground in front of it. The expression is mainly a graphic figure of speech indicating its humiliating judgment and fall.

Lest anyone complains at God's seeming “injustice”, since the serpent as an animal was not to blame for Satan's corrupt possession of its body, he should remember that the 'potter hath power over the clay.' Each animal had been made for a specific mode of life and with specific structures appropriate to such a mode. There were many other 'creeping things,' and God now made the serpent to join this group, for reason of the symbolism involved; but snakes, as animals, are no more capable of resentment at this lot than are moles and worms.

It should be noted also that all other animals were brought under the curse at this time, though none of them had 'sinned.' The serpent was merely cursed 'above all' the rest, but 'every beast' henceforth had the 'sentence of death' in its members. Each was a part of man's dominion and it was by man's sin that death came into the world, infecting everything in that dominion.

III. There Is Continual & Bitter Conflict On the Earth

In the same way, by faith, Eve is the spiritual mother of all believers throughout the ages. First of all, Eve herself is a believer having trusted in God's plan of salvation . And as a result of the faith expressed by Adam and Eve in God's plan of salvation, i.e., the coming Messiah Jesus Christ, God provided His born again children, Adam and Eve, animal skins which symbolized their acceptance of the sacrifice that God alone would make for the sins of all mankind (Gen. 3:21).

Just as it states in Gen. 3:15, other Scripture passages also indicate that there is tremendous enmity between believers and Satan throughout all the ages [1 Peter 5:8]. So, God refers to this adversary of Satan as being the seed of the woman, a human being born of a woman's seed. Since the man provides the seed, this will be a supernatural birth, none other than Jesus Christ, the only One who was so born.

Not only would victory not be as easy as he thought, but ultimately he would be completely defeated and destroyed. 'There will come One who will not be of the man's seed, and who therefore will not be under your dominion. He will be uniquely the Seed of the woman, miraculously conceived and virgin-born. Though you will succeed in grievously injuring Him, He will completely crush you and all your evil ambitions.'

This great promise in Genesis 3:15 has long been known as the ‘prot-evangelium’ (the 'first gospel''), promising the ultimate coming and victory of the Redeemer.

But first, there is seen a time of conflict and even apparent victory on the part of the serpent, who is able to 'bruise the heel' of the woman's seed. Specifically, it appears that Satan's seed consists of those who knowingly and willfully set themselves at enmity with the seed of the woman. They partake in a very specific sense of the character of the Adversary (John 8:44; Ephesians 2:2, 3) and seek to oppose God's purposes in creation and redemption.

The 'seed of the woman' on the other hand, would refer in the first place to those in the human family who are brought into right relationship with God through faith, children of the Father. The primary seed of the woman is, of course, the Lord Jesus Christ.

This promise is, of course, fulfilled in Jesus Christ. He appeared to be mortally wounded when He died on the cross, but He rose again and soon will return to cast the devil into the lake of fire (Rev.20:10). The promised Seed would one day be born of a human woman, but Satan was left in the dark as to which woman and at what time. Both he and Eve may have thought initially it would be her firstborn son. Later on, as the centuries passed, Satan continued his attacks against all the males born in the promised line, particularly those who were objects of special prophetic interest (e.g., Noah, Abraham, Jacob, David), in case one of them might be the promised Seed."

IV. Women subjection to Pains at Birth and to their Husbands

It had been appointed to her to be the 'mother of all living' (Gen.3:20), but now, her children to all generations would suffer under the curse. Their very entrance into the world would be marked by unique suffering, serving as a perpetual reminder of the dreaded effects of sin.

The function of reproduction and motherhood was originally given as the joyful fruition of God's purpose in her creation. However, it is now marred so severely by her 'lust' for withheld knowledge, which conceived and brought forth sin and death (Jam.1:15). It would thus be marked by unique suffering in its accomplishment. Furthermore, she who had acted independently of her husband in her fateful decision to taste the desired fruit, must henceforth exercise her desire only to her husband and he would bear rule over her. The special curse on woman associated with child-bearing can, in fact, be turned to a blessing for a woman yielded to the will of God. Jesus said: 'A woman when she is in travail hath sorrow, because her hour is come: but as soon as she is delivered of the child, she remembered no more the anguish, for joy that a man is born into the world' (John 16:21).

Conclusion & Life Application:

"In conclusion, we find ourselves faced with an important issue. The earth which had previously cooperated readily as the man 'tilled' and 'dressed it' (Genesis 2:5, 15), now became reluctant to yield its food. Instead, it began to yield thorns and noxious weeds, requiring toil and sweat and tears before man could 'eat of it.' And finally, in spite of all his struggle, death would triumph and man's body would return to the dust from which it was taken.

Discussion Questions

1. What are the effects of the fall?

2. Discuss how you can bring mankind in your circle back to God.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

The curse on man himself was fourfold:

1) sorrow, resulting from continual disappointment and futility;

2) pain and suffering, signified by the 'thorns' which intermittently hinder man in his efforts to provide a living for his family,

3) sweat, or tears, the 'strong crying' of intense struggle against a hostile environment; and finally,

4) physical death, which would eventually triumph over all man's efforts, with the structure of his body returning to the simple elements of the earth.

But Christ, as Son of Man and second Adam, has been made the curse for us (Galatians 3:13). He was the 'man of sorrows' (Isaiah 53:3); acquainted more with grief than any other man, He was wounded, bruised, and chastised for us (Isaiah 53:5), and indeed wore the very thorns of the curse as His crown (Mk.15:17); in the agony of His labour, His sweat as it were drops of blood (Luke 22:44), and 'offered up prayers and supplications with strong crying and tears' (Heb.5:7). And, finally, God brought Him into the 'dust of death' (Ps. 22:15).

Therefore, because He bore all the curse Himself for us, once again, the dwelling of God shall someday be with men and 'there shall be no more death neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain: for the former things are passed away" (Rev.21:4; 22:3).

Although the complete removal of the curse awaits the return of Christ to purge and renovate the earth (II Peter 3:10; Revelation 20:11; 21:1), He has already paid the price for its redemption (I Peter 1:18-20), not with 'corruptible things' (which could never redeem anything under the 'bondage of corruption') but with His 'precious blood,' by the 'incorruptible word' (I Peter 1:23).

Lesson 5

Topic : The Plan of Redemption

Text: Exodus 12:1-7, 12-13; Leviticus 16:1-34; 17:11; Isaiah

53:1-12

Supplemental References: Matt.3:13; Romans 3:19-20; 5:8-11;

8:1-3

Central Truth:

Redemption, through God’s love to mankind is the great song of the Bible.

Memory Verse: Isaiah 53:5

Lesson Outline:

I. Redemption by Blood – Leviticus 17:11

II. Redemption in the Old Testament – Exodus 12:1-7

III. Redemption in the New Testament – John 1:36

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us, the Just for the unjust; freely, and by divine appointment of the Father, taking the sinner’s place, bearing his sins, receiving his condemnation, dying his death, fully paying his penalty, and signing with His life’s blood, the pardon of every one who should believe upon Him; that upon simple faith and acceptance of the atonement purchased on Mount Calvary, the vilest sinner may be cleansed of his iniquities and made whiter than driven snow.

Lesson Commentary:

In this lesson, we shall be discussing the necessity of the blood in atoning for sin. Redemption can be done with money or other materials in some cases, but not for sin, which pertains to human life. So, life is required for the redemption of life. However, we shall see how the blood of animals is insufficient for cleansing sin.

I. Redemption by Blood – Leviticus 17:11

Redemption, according to dictionary definitions, can mean any of the following: to set one free by paying a ransom; to buy back something pawned or mortgaged; or ‘to tear loose’ from a dominating oppressing force. In 1Peter 1:18, we read, “Forasmuch as ye know that ye were not redeemed with corruptible things as silver and gold, from your vain conversation received by tradition from your fathers.” Here, we are made to understand that only blood is sufficient for redemption from sin. This is confirmed in Leviticus 17:11 which states that, “The life of the flesh is in the blood and I have given it to you upon the altar to make atonement for your souls: for it is the blood that maketh an atonement for the soul.” We can safely conclude therefore that without the shedding of blood, there can be no forgiveness of sin (Hebrews 9:22).

II. Redemption in the Old Testament – Exodus 12:1-7

After man sinned in the Garden of Eden, God covered his nakedness with animal skin which implies that God made a sacrifice with animal blood to bring about the reunion between Him and the fallen mankind. God desires to fellowship with His children and for this to continue, regular sacrifice was necessary. Hence, God introduced the use of animal blood to serve as a type of the once and for all sacrifice that will be made with the blood of the Lamb of God.

For continuity in the fellowship with, and the worship of God, Cain’s sacrifice was rejected; an indication of fellowship with God. While instituting the Old Testament Covenant with animal blood, Moses was instructed by God in Exodus 24:1-8 to pour half of the animal blood on the altar while the remaining half should be sprinkled upon the Israelites. However, we are told in Hebrew 10:4, “For it is not possible for the blood of bulls and of goats should take away sins.” Why then did God introduce the Old Testament sacrifice? As someone puts it, “animal sacrifice is to serve as credit until Christ comes to settle the debt with His own blood.” Meyer Pearlman puts it this way, “There was nothing in the blood of an irrational creature which could effect the spiritual redemption of souls; that could only be by the offering of a perfect human life.” Only the blood of Jesus cleanses from sin.

III. Redemption In The New Testament – John 1:36

In Hebrews 10:5, it is written, “Wherefore when He cometh into the world, He saith, SACRIFICE AND OFFERING THOU WOULDETH NOT, BUT A BODY HAST THOU PREPARED FOR ME:” Jesus was given a human body so as to have blood for a perfect sacrifice. Paul declared in Acts 20:28 that God purchased the church with His own blood while in John’s first Epistle, it is written that the blood of Jesus Christ, cleanses from sin (1John 1:7b). We can therefore humbly and gratefully acknowledge with faith that:

1. Christ died for the unjust – Romans 5:8; 1Peter 3:18.

2. Christ took the sinner’s place -Titus 2:14; Isaiah 53:5.

3. Christ paid the penalty for sin – Gal. 1:4; 1John 2:2.

4. Christ signed man’s pardon with His blood-1Peter 1:18-19; Romans 5:9.

5. Christ provided cleansing for the vilest sinner – Isaiah 1:18; John 3:16; Hebrews 7:25.

Conclusion /Application

In redemption, Christ took the place of the sinner, to bear the penalty of sin and to meet the demands of the law for His people. We should always remember that redemption is intended to bring us back to a place where we can actively fellowship, worship and serve God in holiness and righteousness, all the days of our life.

Questions & Discussion

1. What is redemption?

2. Why is blood essential in redemption?

3. What are the provisions and benefits of Christ’s redemption?

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

In Exodus chapter 12, God gave Moses instructions on how to observe the Passover. Though the feast was observed in each Israelite home, their united and simultaneous worship would help weld them together as a single community. The blood of the animals was to be placed on the doorframes of the houses as a sign and mark of separation. The sprinkled blood on the Israelites' houses provided protection from death when God destroyed the Egyptian firstborn. The slaying of the animals, and the sprinkling of blood prefigured the substitutionary death of Christ. He is "our Passover Lamb" (1 Cor. 5:7), "a Lamb without blemish or defect" (1 Peter 1:19; cf. John 1:29).

The sacrifice of Jesus is the means whereby individual believers escape the horrors of spiritual death. Thus under the protection of shed blood, the congregation was to be reminded of cleansing from sin (Heb 9:22). As the blood of an animal was the means of deliverance and of escaping death (in O/T), so Christ's blood is the means of redemption for believers (Rom 5:9; Eph 1:7), which brings to end the observance of yearly sacrifices for sin. “By the which will we are sanctified through the offering of the body of Jesus Christ once for all.” (Heb 10:10). The scripture concludes, “But this man, after he had offered one sacrifice for sins for ever, sat down on the right hand of God” (Heb 10:12).

Lesson 6

Topic: Salvation Through Grace

Text: Ephesians 2:1-10

Supplemental References: Rom. 3:10, 3:23; 6:23; Is. 64:6; Jn. 6:47;

Eph.2:13

Central Truth: The sinner can only be saved by God’s grace

Memory Verse: Ephesians 2:8

Lesson Outline

I. Background to the Lesson

II. Man’s Condition: Dead in Sin (Eph.2:1-3)

III. What We Should Do For God - Work For The Lord (Verse 10)

Lesson Introduction:

We believe that the salvation of sinners is wholly through grace; that we have no righteousness or goodness of our own wherewith to seek divine favour, and must come, therefore, throwing ourselves upon the unfailing mercy and love of Him who bought us and washed us in His own blood, pleading the merits and the righteousness of Christ the Saviour, standing upon His word and accepting the free gift of love and pardon.

Lesson Commentary:

I. Background to The Lesson

A. Purpose and power of God.

These are days of many bumper stickers that are used to preach the Gospel. There was one that was seen recently by a brother and it reads: “We could wreck the world”, but “Jesus saves”.

The Apostle Paul’s theology is not written on the back of a truck; it is recorded in Ephesians 2. There, he summarizes the condition of mankind, the kindness of God, and the nature of the salvation which He has provided for lost men in Christ.

In Ephesians chapter 1, Paul focused our attention on the purpose of God and on the power of God, which assure the Christian of the blessings which God has provided in Christ. The final verses of chapter 1 concern the great power of God which He has vested in Jesus Christ, through His resurrection and ascension.

B. Contents of Ephesians 2:1-10

This passage contains three main segments:

1) The fallen man, and his hopeless condition-dead (Vs 1-3).

2) God, with His mercy and grace for man’s salvation (Vs 4-6).

3) Purpose of salvation, to the praise of His glory (Vs 7-10).

All together, they spell out the essence of the gospel of Jesus Christ.

Although He had died for the sins of the world, God raised Jesus from the dead, and taking Him back into heaven where He seated Him at His right hand. He invested Him with authority and power greater than any and all other authorities. Along with this authority and power, He was appointed as Head of the church, which is the earthly manifestation of His presence and which fills up that which remains of His ministry on earth before His second coming.

II. Man’s Condition: Dead in Sin (Eph.2:1-3)

If Ephesians 1:19-23 takes us to the heights in exploring the power and authority of the risen and ascended Christ, chapter 2:1-3 takes us to the very depths, as Paul expounds the powerless, hopeless, lifeless condition of fallen men, who are enslaved by their own fleshly desires, and dominated by the world around them and by Satan.

Consider the contrast between Christ’s position and ours. Christ is alive because of His righteousness, but we are dead, because of our sins. Christ is exalted, seated in the heavens; we are on and of the earth. Christ has been given power and authority over all other powers and authorities; we were subject to the powers and authorities.

A. The Irony of fallen Man’s Dilemma

The irony of fallen man’s dilemma is that he doesn’t even realize his condition until after he is saved.

1. Lost men, blinded and deceived by Satan, think they are really “living,” when in reality, they are dead.

2. They think that by living in sin, they are enjoying life to its fullest, but they are not.

3. They suppose that they are free, subject to no one, but they are really enslaved.

B. The lost Men are Dead.

1. To be dead is to be lifeless.

2. To be dead is to be unable to help oneself.

4. To be dead is to be absolutely powerless.

5. To be dead is to be beyond hope (in the eyes of the world).

C. Death is ultimately the result of sin.

In today’s text, Paul examines some of the contributing factors to our sin from various Bible points.

1. First, men are sinners because they are born that way.

a. We were, “by nature, children of wrath.”

b. We were sinners, subject to the wrath of God because of our sin nature, which we obtained at birth (Ps. 51: 5; Rom. 5:12).

2. Second, men sin and are therefore sinners because they follow the world in its course of sin and rebellion.

a. Sinners love and seek companions, co-sinners, to share in the excitement and (unknowingly) in the penalty of sin (Prov. 1:8-19).

b. This is why the Bible instructs us to avoid being pressured by the world to conform to its values and lifestyle.

3. Third, unbelievers sin because they are unwittingly subject to the influence of Satan.

4. Finally, men are sinners because they follow the dictates of the flesh (Rom. 7:7-25; Gal.5:16-21).

D. Paul unites the Jews and the Gentiles in the common condition.

1. What is most interesting is that it is here that Paul chooses to unite the Jews and the Gentiles in the common condition of sin and death.

2. What affects the Gentiles also affects the Jews as well.

3. The Jews thought that they were born “special,” because of their physical descent from Abraham.

5. They were not better than the Gentiles.

6. In some ways, it was true that the Jews were less addicted to the fleshly sins of sexual immorality and idolatry compared to the Gentiles.

7. In and of ourselves, and apart from God, we are desperately and hopelessly lost.

8. We are not just “sick,” we are “dead.”

9. We are without life, without hope, without potential, without “worth.”

10. Any value we may have, or any hope, must come from Christ.

11. This is the good news of the gospel, and that which Paul explains next in verses 4-6.

III. God’s Grace: Alive in Christ (2:4-6)

The condition of men in sin is not hopeless or terminal because God has come to the rescue of fallen men through His provision in Jesus Christ.

Paul begins with the motivation of God, by His mercy which prompted Him to provide a way of escape from our condition of sin and eternal death.

A. Expression of God’s love.

The expression of God’s love for us is salvation by grace.

1. Grace - unmerited favour

2. Grace means that God provides salvation of a sinner without the sinner meriting salvation.

B. Man’s Acceptance of God’s love.

While God has provided the means of grace (Jesus) man has a part in that salvation - Faith.

1. Salvation by faith simply means accepting what God has told us to do concerning salvation and obeying Him. (Matthew 7:21; Hebrews 5:8-9).

2. God’s love is not a response, but a cause.

Our love for God is prompted by His love for us, a love which initiates our love in response: (1 John 4:19).

God’s love for us is vastly different from our love for Him.

He loved us while we were His enemies, while we were still dead in our sins and transgressions.

God’s mercy is not prompted by our potential or by any qualities we think we possess, but by our own pathetic condition.

Divine grace was not bestowed on us because we were so worthy, or because God found anything good in us, but because of the goodness which is in God Himself.

God sent Jesus Christ to the world, to suffer and to die in the sinner’s place.

God’s grace and salvation does not come to us in various forms, from which we may choose.

His grace has been poured out to us lavishly in Christ, and in Him alone.

IV. God’s Purpose: The Praise of the Glory for His Grace (2:7-10)

A. We are saved for His glory.

God’s purposes are not merely temporal, they are eternal. God’s purpose in saving sinners is not just to make men happy, to provide blessings, or to enable men to escape the torments of hell but for His Pleasure -Rev 4:11.

Whether it be in the punishment of the wicked or in the salvation of sinners by grace, God is working out all things to His own glory. God will not share His glory with any other being, and thus the work of salvation is entirely His work. Grace is divine favour which is undeserved.

B. We are saved to work with and for God as His workmanship-(Ephesians 2:10).

1. We are created for the purpose of good works (Titus 2:14).

2. God wants us to be active in good works (Col. 1:10; 2. Tit. 2:7; Matt.5:16).

3. We will be judged according to our works or continued obedience.

As saints, we are what we are because of His doing. We are a new creation in Christ (2 Corinthians 5:17), but we are His creation. He created us in Jesus Christ. Any good works which we might do as Christians are the works which He foreordained, which He planned and prepared in eternity past. We dare not take credit for them.

Conclusion & Life Application:

Our passage today sets before us the glorious difference between what we once were, apart from Christ, and what we now are, in Christ. The good news of the gospel is that we need not remain dead in our transgressions and sin, separated from God and destined for wrath. God has provided a way of salvation, one way by which sinners can become saints. And this “way” is Jesus Christ (John 14:6).

If you are a Christian, this text should serve as a reminder of what you once were, and of what you now are, in Christ. It should produce both humility and gratitude. It should stimulate you to love and good deeds, knowing that even the good works which you do are those which God has accomplished in and through you, for His glory.

If you cannot rejoice in these truths, then now is an opportunity for you to receive them for yourself. Christ died for our sins, so that we need not suffer the penalty of death. Christ rose from the dead and was ascended to the right hand of the Father. In Christ, we too are assured of our resurrection from the dead, and of our position with God in the heavenly realms. All we must do is to agree with God concerning our condition, as outlined in Ephesians 2:1-3, and to receive Jesus Christ as God’s provision for our salvation, as explained in verses 4-6. In Christ we cease to be what we once were, and we forever continue to be what Christ is. This is the message of the Cross and of our movement. It is one of our 22 tenets of Faith.

Questions & Discussion:

1. Can one be saved without understanding that Jesus Christ is Lord of all, and accepting Him as such?

2. What is the expression of that faith?

3. Discuss and explain your understanding of Grace and Mercy?

Teaching Overview/Insight

1. What We Were - We Were Dead Spiritually (Verses 1-3).

A. We were "dead in trespasses and sins"

1. We can be dead spiritually while still alive physically. (1 Timothy 5:6; Revelation 3:1)

B. We "walked according to the course of this world."

ii. Not by works of righteousness which we have done (Titus 3:5)

iii. Who is a God like You, pardoning iniquity (Micah 7:18)

3. What We Should Do for God - Work for The Lord (Verse 10).

That which makes it possible to be forgiven, to be cleansed, to be healed, that which makes it possible for us to receive our life back again, fresh and clean and new, is the power of God's Grace in the Cross of Jesus Christ (Romans 2:6-9; 2 Corinthians 5:10).

Lesson 7

Topic: Repentance

Text: Matthew 3:1-12

Supplemental References: 1 John 1:9, Romans 5:1, 2; 8:1,

Luke 1:77-79, John 6:37

Central Truth:

Repentance is one of God’s requirements for salvation.

Memory Verse: 1 John 1:9

Lesson Outline:

I. Hearing The Word

II. Believing The Gospel

III. Turning From Sinful Past To God

Lesson Introduction

We believe that upon sincere, godly sorrow for sin and a whole-hearted acceptance of the Lord Jesus Christ, they who call upon him may be justified by faith through His precious blood and that in place of condemnation they may have the most blessed peace, assurance and favour with God; that with open arms of mercy and pardon the Saviour wants to receive each penitent person who will in unfeigned contrition and supplication for mercy, open the door of his heart and accept Him as Lord and King

Lesson Commentary

Repentance is basically turning from one’s sinful path (past) and turning to God in a 180 degree change of direction. This turning however involves feeling of godly sorrow for sin and holding on to the special grace of Calvary without turning back to one’s old vomit. Repentance involves the exercise of human will to decide whether to turn back from the broad way of perdition to the narrow way of life. It requires a firm decision on whom to follow and serve. (Joshua 24:14-15).

I. Hearing The Word of God

Spiritual journey involves an encounter with the word of life. The Bible says, “So, faith comes by hearing, and hearing by the word of God” (Romans 10:17). Verse 14 says “how then shall they call on him whom they have not believed and how shall they hear without a preacher.” It is the word of God that generates faith and understanding. Repentance follows the hearing of the word of God, and responding to it rather than hardening our hearts (Psalm 95:7-8; Hebrews 3:15). The call of Jesus in Matt.11:28 is still fresh. He says, "Come to me all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest.” We must carry this message to the people so that they can repent by turning from the broad way of perdition to God for redemption.

II. Believing The Gospel

Repentance is always a product of personal conviction through faith. This faith flows from the word of God (Romans 10:9-10,14,17). Faith is a spiritual persuasion as it applies to the invisible. The Bible says, "For it is with your heart that you believe, and it is with your mouth that you confess and are saved" (Rom. 10: 10). It is after this that the power for adoption as a child of God is released. The Bible says, “Yet, to all who received him, to those who believed in his name, he gave right to become children of God, children born not of natural descent nor of human decision or husband’s will, but born of God” (John 1:12-13). The component of this belief includes the belief that:

i. Every man is a sinner (Romans 3:23)

ii. The wages of sin is death (Romans 6:23)

iii. Our righteousness cannot save us (Isaiah 64:6;Romans 3:30)

iv. Jesus has paid for our sins on the cross of Calvary (John 19:30)

v. The righteousness of God is now available to us by Grace (Romans 5:19; 6:21-22. Isaiah 54: 17)

vi. If we confess Jesus as Lord and believe in him, we shall be saved (Romans 10:9-10).

vii. Our salvation is not by the works we have done but by grace (Eph. 2:8-9)

III. Turning From Sinful Past To God

The basic world order is between evil and good or light and darkness. Apostle Paul said in Rom. 7:6, “The mind of the sinful man is death, but the mind controlled by the spirit is life and peace; the sinful mind is hostile to God. It does not submit to God’s law nor can it do so. Those controlled by the sinful nature can’t please God" (Rom. 8:7-8). When a person hears the word of God and is convicted of his/her sinful past, such a person has taken the first major step of repentance. Anyone who cannot make a “U” turn from the works of the flesh (Gal.5:19-20) to God (Matthew 11:28) would be like the wife of Lot who was engrossed by Sodom and could not fully escape for her dear life. The life of Apostle Paul in Acts chapter 9 is a vivid description of repentance. He turned from being a persecutor of the gospel to a dramatic advocate of the gospel he was fighting against. The voluntary element of repentance involves the exercise of the will for repentance in form of inward turning from sinful past and a wholehearted turning to Christ for forgiveness (Luke 15:18-20) and being adopted into the family of God. John 1:12.

Conclusion

Repentance is a critical step for anybody who wants a new life in Christ. It starts from hearing the word of God, believing it, having godly sorrow, confessing our sins, forsaking them and turning to God for a new relationship as a joint heir with Christ.

Life Application

A large gathering of Church leaders came for a seminar and spiritual impartation from a revered man of God. Many of the participants were young General Overseers. At a point, he was led to make an altar call. To his surprise, many of them came out for salvation! This shows that repentance is not about working for God, attending church services regularly or bearing Christian names. One could do all these or more and still end up in hell without repentance.

Lesson Commentary:

The world is filled with people who profess and claim to belong to God without submitting to Him. How do we reconcile a situation where people claim to accept Jesus as Saviour but He is not the Lord of their lives. They love to enjoy His grace but reject His Lordship (His yoke and light burden) Matthew 11:28-30. Jesus never came to dispense grace without responsible living and commitment. I. Repentance.

Repentance means turning away from sinful life unto God. It is a sincere and thorough change of the mind and disposition regarding sin. It includes a change of view, a change of feeling and a change of purpose. As a follow up to our lesson on repentance last week, we would like to discuss common errors and assumptions of people on repentance.

(a) What Repentance is not?

i. It is not reformation of sinners’ moral lives. Repentance is not amending one’s errors to become better and more responsible. This cannot change the inner man.

ii. It is not conformity. Repentance is not an attempt to conform to societal standards.

iii. It is not a change of value-orientation whereby somebody reviews his or her background and begins to delete or remove those considered as embarrassing and unacceptable as people do with New Year resolutions.

iv. It is not becoming religious by getting more committed to church programmes, religious rites and church responsibilities. Paul was zealous for God before conversion. Yet, he was not born again.

v. It is not being remorseful, which is human sorrow (unlike godly sorrow) or more pious and god-fearing without being born again.

(b) What Repentance is?

Repentance is putting one’s faith in Christ and cleansing one's soul from sinful defilements (1 John5:1, John 3:8, Titus 3:5). It is the turning from old ways of sinful life into a righteous life in Christ and thereby becoming a new creation (2 Corinthians 5: 17).

II. Acceptance:

Acceptance is a fundamental component in the process of salvation. To repent from our sinful ways and turn away from them to God is very crucial and important. However, the next issue is how to live a successful Christian life. Peter told the people who heard him on the day of Pentecost, “Let all Israel be assured of this: God has made this Jesus whom you crucified both Lord and Christ”

(Act 2:36). Therefore, every believer must accept Jesus as Lord and Saviour.

Any idea about Jesus, no matter how grandiose, once it does not include His being the Saviour, it cannot profit that individual in terms of salvation. Jesus is not just a Saviour. ii. Acceptance as Lord: (Act2:36). Jesus must not be accepted as Saviour alone, He must also be accepted as the Lord of our lives. Many seem to ignore this fact. Jesus is the Author and the Finisher of our faith. He is the Shepherd of our souls (John 10:1-5). When he said, “Come to me all you who are weary and burdened,” he also said, “Take my yoke upon you…” (Matt.11:28-30). This yoke is a bond that put our lives under His control as Master and Lord. Yoked animals have no room for distractions or independent ventures. Their destinies in their mutual assignment are bond together. Jesus only calms us down that “My yoke is easy and my burden is light” (Matt.11:30) so as to make us realize that His own Lordship is friendly and meant for our own good. Indeed, without Christ being the Lord of our lives, the devil will hijack our lives and destinies.

Conclusion

Repentance and acceptance are fundamental steps for us to reconcile and reconnect with God. Accepting Jesus as Saviour through repentance always transforms us into becoming new creation (2 Corinthians 5:17). To continue in this new life, Christ must be accepted as Lord of our lives to prevent Satan, the enemy of our souls and our old task master from taking charge of our destiny again.

There are several facts about the new birth (regeneration) that need to be understood and they are given to us in this passage. This first part will discuss two and the remaining ones will be discussed in the next lesson.

Lesson Commentary:

I. Reality of The New Birth

A. The usage of the word “born”

The word “born” is used eight times in John 3:1-9. Here Jesus was talking about the real experience – the reality of the new birth. The new birth is a reality; it is as real an experience as physical birth.

No one would deny his own physical birth, and we do not have to deny our spiritual birth. Jesus said that the new birth is a reality.

B. Selected few examples

If you would interview people who trusted Jesus, you would find out how real it is.

Ask Peter, he knew what it was to trust in Jesus Christ and to experience this new birth. In his letters, Peter tells us about this matter of being born again.

Matthew, the publican, had his life radically changed because he trusted Christ and experienced the new birth.

The thief on the cross turned to Jesus and said, “Remember me Lord, when You come into Your Kingdom” (Luke 23:42). Because of his faith in Christ, he was born again. Ask him whether or not it is a real experience. In John 4, the Samaritan woman had a purposeful conversation with Jesus Christ beside the well, at the end of the conversation, she left her water jar and ran to the city with her testimony of a change life. Ask her also, she will tell you it was a real experience.

Ask the Apostle Paul, as well as Saul of Tarsus, the leading young Jewish rabbi of his day, was persecuting the church when he met Jesus Christ (Acts 9:1-5). He put his faith in Jesus Christ, and was born again. It was a real experience.

The reality of the new birth, the good news of the Gospel, is this: You can start all over again! You do not have to stay the way you are; you can start over again. Because there is such a thing as regeneration, you can be born again.

II. Simplicity of The New Birth

A. Simple Illustration

Our Lord Jesus used birth as a simple illustration of what happens when a person is saved. Even though he was a great teacher of Israel, Nicodemus unfortunately misunderstood what our Lord was talking about. “How can a man be born when he is old?” asked Nicodemus. “Can he enter the second time into his mother’s womb and be born?” (John 3:4). Our Lord was talking about something spiritual, but Nicodemus interpreted this as something physical.

In John 4, when our Lord was talking to the woman at the well, He talked about living water. He said, “If you ask Me, I will give you water and you will never be thirsty again” (John 4:10, 14). He was speaking spiritually, but she took Him literally and materially, and she said, “How can you give me any water? You don’t even have a vessel to draw with (v. 11)

In John 6, our Lord was talking about eating the Bread of Life, feeding on His flesh and His blood. The Jews were offended by this. But He wasn’t speaking in material terms, He tells us Himself that it is the spirit that gives life, the flesh profits nothing (v. 63).

B. Sincere Explanation

When Jesus spoke about birth, Nicodemus took Him literally, Jesus replied in John 3:5,6. “Except a man be born of water (physical birth) and of the spirit, he cannot enter into the Kingdom of God. That which is born of the flesh (physical birth) is flesh.” Our Lord was saying, “Nicodemus, please don’t major on the physical and the material” – he was talking in spiritual terms.

Two parents create this new birth – the spirit of God and the Word of God, according to James 1:18 and 1Pet. 1:23. We are born again by the word of God, the living Word of God. The Spirit of God takes the Word of God and reveals the Son of God. And when you trust Jesus Christ as your Saviour, a miracle takes place in your heart – regeneration, you are born again. The two “spiritual parents” who accomplish this birth are the Spirit of God and the Word of God.

Conclusion & Life Application:

New birth is a reality, the term is simple. But the great question is “who needs to be born again?” The Bible makes us to understand that all need this experience without exception (John 3:3, 7, Rom. 3:24, 25). Do you have this unique experience?

Lesson 11

Topic: The New Birth Part 3 (Justification by Faith)

Text: Romans 3:20-28

Supplemental References: Romans 5:1-12

Central Truth: Justification is the basis of the new birth.

Memory Verse: Romans 5:1

Lesson Outline

I. The Meaning Of Justification

II. The Method Of Justification

III. The Marks Of Justification

Lesson Introduction

We believe that the change which takes place in the heart and life at conversion is a very real one, that the sinner is then born again in such a glorious and transforming manner that the old things are passed away and all things are become new; in so much that the things once most desired are now abhorred, while the things once abhorred are now held most sacred and dear, and that now having imputed to him the righteousness of the Redeemer and having received of the Spirit of Christ, new desires, new aspirations, new interests, and a new perspective on life, time and eternity, fills the blood-washed heart so that this desire is now to openly confess and serve the Master, seeking ever those things which are above .

The focus of this third part on new birth is justification.

Lesson Commentary

How can a man be just before God? Justification is one of the key words in the Christian life. The key verse is Romans 5:1 “Therefore being justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ”. We want to consider justification from three different angles: The meaning of justification, the method of justification and the marks of justification.

I. The Meaning of Justification

A. Definition: Justification is the gracious act of God in declaring righteous a sinner who believes in Jesus Christ. You should memorize this definition because it is important.

1. Justification is an Act.

Notice, please, that justification is an act, not a process. No one Christian is more justified than any other Christian. If you are saved and your sister is saved, your sister and you are justified in the same way, and you have the same righteousness. Instantly, the believer is given a righteous standing before God. It is the gracious act of God, it is not something that men do.

2. Justification is Unchanging.

Once God has declared that we are righteous through Jesus Christ, the sin question is settled once and for all.

Justification means not only that God forgets our sins – past, present and future – but justification means that God forgets we were sinners. He never again treats us as sinners.

II. The Method of Justification

What is the method of justification? How can a holy God justify sinners? How can a holy God, as it were, overlook sin; God deals with it. There are four phrases you want to mark in your Bible in the book of Romans, and these four phrases explain to us the method of justification.

A. Freely by His Grace

Romans 3:24 says, “Being justified freely by his grace.” How is God able to justify us? He justifies us by grace, not by merit. The word “freely” is translated “without a cause” in John 15:25. We are “justified without a cause by his grace.” There is nothing in us that makes God want to justify us. Justification is purely an act of God’s grace. Grace means favour that is not deserved and cannot be earned.

B. By Faith

Romans 3:28 says, “Therefore we conclude that a man is justified by faith without (apart from) the deeds of the law.” Not only are we justified by grace but we are justified by faith. Whenever you have grace, you have to have faith. Whenever you have law, you have to have works. A person cannot be justified by keeping the Law (see Romans 3:20).

We are justified by faith – faith in Christ, faith that comes from the heart.

C. By His Blood

Romans 5:9 says, “Much more then, being now justified by his blood.” Not only are we justified by grace and faith, but we are justified by His blood. Somebody has to pay the price for sin. In order for God to justify the ungodly, He has to deal with their ungodliness. In order for us to be justified by faith, there has to be a Saviour for us to believe in. We are justified by His blood.

D. Unto Life

Finally, Romans 5:18 says, “Therefore as by the offence of one (Adam) judgment came upon all men to condemnation; even so by the righteousness of one (Jesus Christ) the free gift came upon all men unto justification of life.” That is a marvelous phrase - justification which results in life. We are justified unto life. Justification results in a new standing and a new life. That new life is the result of regeneration.

III. The Marks of Justification

Now, we want to look at the marks of justification, and our key passage is Romans 5:1-5.

The marks of justification in the believer’s life are revealed in relationships. If you are truly justified by faith, then you are going to have a right relationship with God (vs 1-2), a right relationship to circumstances (vs 3-4) and a right relationship to other people (v. 5).

Conclusion & Life Application

Wow! It is a marvelous thing to be justified. If you have been justified, you have a right relationship with God; you have peace with God, not war, you have access to God; you have grace from God, and you can get through life with all His power; you can rejoice in the hope of that glory of God.

We believe that people who have been born again grow in sanctification—which means that they separate themselves from the value system of the world and embrace and live according to the values of the kingdom that Jesus proclaimed. As they do so, they increase in holiness, faith, power, prayer, love and service (2 Corinthians 7:1).

Lesson Commentary

I. The Solemn Call to Sanctification. 1 Thess.4:1-4

To sanctify is to make holy. It is to separate from the world and its worldly values to adopt the godly lifestyle of thoughts and actions. The journey to sanctification begins with the born again experience – the recognition and acceptance of Jesus Christ as personal Lord and Saviour (Jn.3:1-6, 16-18; Rom.10:9-11). Salvation rightly positions the believer for sanctification. The call to sanctification is a solemn call – requiring serious and sincere response from believers. The import of the call stems from the fact that sanctification which is closely associated with holiness is demanded in walking with and working for God who is absolutely holy. Armed with the assured promises of God to believers, the individual Christian is to cleanse, free and release self from the filthiness and pollution which comes with living in a depraved world – 2 Cor.7:1. God demands the sanctification of our entire being – spirit, soul and body (1 Thess.5:23) because these must be preserved blameless in readiness for the coming of Christ. Acceptable service to God demands the release of our whole being – Deut. 6:5; Rom. 12:1,2. Sanctification is a call for believers to grow and mature from the foundational and fundamentals of Christian faith to perfection (Heb.6:1). We all need to be perfected in Christ’s image as a proof of our spiritual maturity and preparation for His coming.

II. The Source of Sanctification

Sanctification is an activity of the divine. As a spiritual experience, it is available only to those who are connected with God. The goal of sanctification is separation of the saints from the secular and sinful, and setting them apart for the sacred purpose – Ex.13:1,2; 40:9-13. The sanctified are those consecrated to be holy, and their being so sanctified is intended not only to remind them of the necessity of spiritual cleansing but also of the gracious purpose of God to readily and genuinely accomplish the work of Christian’s utmost service - 2 Tim.2:19-21. Thus, sanctification graciously places the believer in the position of him or her being used honorably, gloriously and rewardingly in the pursuit of God’s agenda for the individual, and humanity as a whole.

If sanctification is understood in the sense of consecration and has to do with the way in which holiness is necessary for earthly people, or things to relate to the holy God, it is only reasonable and logical for us to accept that the means of obtaining this experience can only be the one provided by God who is holy, pure and righteous.

Primarily and intrinsically, sanctification derives from God. Consecration requires a special act of God to make a person or thing holy. God is the Sanctifier (1 Thess.5:23), sanctifying the full component of man for His eternal purpose. Sanctification occurs as a consequence of movement towards the Holy One.

The word – pure and sincere word of God is also the source of sanctification – Jn.17:17-19. Separation from, or laying aside profane tendencies and panting for the word of God positions the believer for sanctification – 1 Pet.2:1,2; Eph.5:26; 1 Tim.4:15.

We are also sanctified through the agency of the Holy Spirit – Rom.15:16; 1 Pet.1:2. By the Spirit, we are made a new creature – 2 Cor.5:17. Jesus Christ also accomplishes sanctification for the believer, even as God has made Him unto us sanctification – 1 Cor.1:30. Through His precious blood shed on the cross, sanctification was offered to the believer – Heb.10:10; 13:12.

III. The Signs and Sustenance of Sanctified Experience

Sanctification is the will of God for His church. It is the path the believer must walk in order to please God and abound in spiritual service – 1 Thess.4:1-7. The believer in Christ is called to a life akin to that of a disciplined soldier, a law-abiding, focused athlete and a tenacious, hopeful husbandman or farmer (2 Tim.2:3-6). If these must attain to their goals, then, great effort is required on their part. For the Christian pilgrim, sanctification is a “can’t-do-without”, they must-have antecedent to run the race well and successfully. God has not left man with the choice of whether to live the sanctified life or not as a means to please Him. There is the imperative call to all believers who anticipate the second coming of Christ to imbibe the sanctified experience –Titus 2:11-15. Since as believers we must necessarily put on the garment of sanctification, what are the checklists in our lives (the signs to watch out for) to prove that we are on the right track?

Ø Not walking as other Gentiles walk – Eph.4:17-20; Rom.8:5,6

Ø Putting off the old man – Eph.4:22

Ø Not indulging in self-exaltation but displaying love and humility – Rom.12:3,10

Ø Renewing our spirit and mind – Eph.4:23; Rom.12:2

Ø Promoting unity and co-operation – Rom.12:4

Ø Putting on the new man – Eph.4:24

Ø Maintaining conscientious deployment of spiritual gifts – Rom.12:5-8

Ø Speaking the truth and speaking to edify – Eph.4:25,29

Ø Shunning evil and doing good – Rom.12:9

The sanctified experience is that which ought to be kept by every believer until the day of Jesus Christ. Sanctification prepares and preserves the believer for the coming of Christ in glory – 1 Thess.5:23. God shines His light on the path of the righteous and it is a continuous, progressive walk in sanctification that keeps our spiritual life intact and perfected – Prov.4:18. Spiritual highway of sanctification and holiness is the path God requires from those who will walk in His steps – Isa.35:8.

Conclusion & Life Application

Sanctification involves separation from that which is unholy, profane and worldly and consecration to that which is pure, holy and godly. Man must consciously co-operate with God to effect this. The Christian life is not one to be locked up in a box and hidden somewhere. On the contrary, the Christian life is to be on constant display for others to see – Matt.5:14-16. People of the world are not ready to read the Bible theoretically but are more interested and eagerly waiting for Christians to live out the content of the Bible practically. In essence, what catches the world’s attention is more of what we do than what we say, preach or teach. It is time for practical Christianity!. What will promote this brand of life which the world eagerly wants and waits to see through us (Christians) is the sanctified experience. A life of sanctification is a catalyst for the promotion of the Gospel and advancing the second coming of Christ.

God will never demand from us beyond that which we can give. All the people that God has ever called – Moses, Aaron, Paul etc. and used for His glory, sanctification was the hallmark of their lives. Jesus Christ is also the one sanctified by the Father and sent into the world (Jn. 10:36) to execute God’s mission. As disciples of Jesus Christ, if we are to continue that mission and do it creditably (Jn. 14:12; 15:16), we too must be sanctified. That is, we must be brought into that intimate relationship enjoyed by the Father and Son. God’s standard has not changed – He is looking for sanctified vessels that are meet for the Master’s use and prepared unto every good work – 2 Tim.2:19-21. The presence of the Holy Spirit is the key to experiential reality of sanctification. We must constantly live in total surrender to God and be humble to invite the grace of sanctification to be displayed in our lives at all times so that we can fulfil His mission on earth.

Questions and Discussion

1. Why is sanctification so important in the life of a Christian?

2. Can sanctification, as a Christian experience, be lost?

3. What are the steps to restore a Christian who has lost his/her sanctification experience?

4. How do you minister to a Christian who does not believe in the doctrine of sanctification?

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

All Christians have been called to sanctification. The level of growth in sanctification experience differs from one Christian to another. We are enjoined to provoke one another to love and do good work. Sanctification is a good work God wants to accomplish in the life of all believers. As individual Christians, when we daily live the life of Christ before the rest of the world, we are invariably projecting the sanctifying power of Jesus Christ – the power that keeps believers above sin, Satan, flesh and its allures. We must realize that it is the sanctification experience that will keep our garments spotless until the appearing of our Lord and Saviour, Jesus Christ.

Lesson 13

Topic: Daily Christian Living – Part 2 (Love and Faith)

Text: 1 John 4:7-21

Supplementary References: 1 Cor.13:1-13; 1 Tim.1:5; Heb.11

John 13:1,35; Jn.15:9-13; Matt.21:18-22; 1 Jn.3:9-18.

Central Truth:

Love is the evidence to the world that we are truly God’s children.

Memory Verse: 1John.4:11

Lesson Outline

I. Love and Faith Explained.

II. Love and Faith Enjoined.

III. Love and Faith Exemplified.

Lesson Introduction

We believe that people who have been born again grow in sanctification—which means that they separate themselves from the value system of the world and embrace and live according to the values of the kingdom that Jesus proclaimed. As they do so, they increase in holiness, faith, power, prayer, love and service (2 Corinthians 7:1).

Lesson Commentary

I. Love and Faith Explained

The scriptures describe and present love and faith as attributes of God. These two virtues are necessary in Christian’s walk with God and work for God. Love and faith are not abstract elements but are indeed real, cogent and demonstrable – Jn.13:1; Heb.11:1,8-10. As human beings, we derive our conceptions of God from the special revelation which He has given of Himself and one of the clear images presented to us is the fact that God is love – 1 Jn.4:8,16. The love of God is strongly declared to us as that of His existence. Love is the highest characteristic of God, the one attribute in which other characteristics of God reflect graciously. Love, whether human or divine, is the deepest possible expression of the personality and of the closeness of personal relations. Love is rooted firmly in the personal character of God Himself and is the basis of the divine covenant He made with Israel and all of God’s people. The covenant relationship between Israel and God was premised upon God Himself, who took the initiative and chose Israel because He loved her (Deut.4:37; 7:7,8; Isa.43:4).

The marks of love (listed in 1 Cor.13:4-8) is the pattern by which the Christian life is to be marked as true image and ambassadors of God. Love for God and for fellow human being is to be the motive for our actions (Matt.25:34-40), and the reward of faith is the end of those actions (Heb.11:39,40). True and genuine love, when expressed towards God and humans places us above the law for all the laws are summed up in love (Rom.13:8-10).

With respect to faith, the Old and New Testaments present faith as a positive and prominent attitude of God and which God expects to find in all who relate with Him (Heb.11:3,6). The word “faith” is also expressed in such terms as believe, trust or hope. Faith is trusting in God, in the Person of Christ and in the Word. Faith is one of the fruits of the Spirit (Gal.5:22) and it is the typical attitude expected of Christians. Faith has its rewards both for now and in the future. Faith endears us to God and empowers us for relevance in God’s service and kingdom. It is the key by which we access the presence and provisions of God including salvation, healing, peace, joy and other breakthroughs. Love and faith are two attitudes compelling for the Christians. To be deficient of these is to miss the great spiritual and material blessings.

II. Love and Faith Enjoined

The Scriptures is united as to the relationship between love and faith. Love and faith as attitudes of the Christian life are to be practiced side by side in the journey of this life and that which looks towards eternity. Like Siamese twins, the Christian life is to be lived concurrently by love and faith in order to bring about a glorious end.

In several passages of the Scriptures, God enjoins the practice of love and faith as strong and necessary pillars for Christianity. What should predispose the Christian to embrace these attitudes?

Ø The realization of the fact that we are children of light and that the second coming of the Lord is a reality that should be expected and prepared for – 1 Thess.5:8.

Ø Walking in love and faith renders observance of ceremonial law unnecessary – Gal.5:6.

Ø It is the character demanded of Christians – 1 Tim.1:5.

Ø It is the proof of abiding life in Christ – 1 Jn.3:23.

Ø It is the bond depicting Christian’s commitment to the life in Christ – Rom.8:35-39.

Ø It is the eternally abiding graces – 1 Cor.13:13.

III. Love and Faith Exemplified

Faith works by love. We display our faith in God through love. When we show love, we are proving that we have faith in God. Love and faith are two catalysts that are essential to faithful Christian living. We see examples of this practice in the Scripture.

A. i) Love – God’s Example.

God is love – 1 Jn.4:7. Love is God’s greatest attribute. He made us in His image so that we can manifest His perfect reflection. How did God give expression to love?

- In bringing Israel out of bondage in Egypt – Deut.7:8. God kept the oath He made with Israel’s forefathers by redeeming them out of the hands of their oppressors – Ps.105. God also demonstrated His love by sending His only Begotten Son (Jesus Christ) to die for mankind – Jn.3:16; Rom.5:8. God extends His love to us in showing us mercy and quickening us with Christ – Eph.2:4,5. God also bestows on believers the privilege of sonship – being called sons of God – 1 Jn.3:1; 4:9,10. Our Heavenly Father equally freely gives the blessings of nature to all, irrespective of individual relationship to Him – Matthew 5:45.

ii.) Love – Jesus Christ’s Example

Jesus Christ is love personified. Christ’s self-sacrificing commitment to us is seen in him laying down his life – Jn.15:13. Christ shares divine nature with God, the Father, one of which is love. This token of love was extended to His disciples – Jn.13:1; 15:9.

iii.) Love – The Christian’s Duty

The Christian is encouraged to love with the entire being – Deut.6:5; 10:12 because God will not accept half-hearted devotion to love. He is to remain (abide) in love while anticipating the coming of Jesus Christ – Jude 21. When Christians walk in love, it is a proof of discipleship – Jn.13:35. The Christian, in loving one another, is the standard Christ has prescribed for His church – Matt.22:39; Jn.15:22. Sincerity is also demanded in the display of love – Rom.12:9.

B. Faith

The Christian walk is a walk of faith. The Christian journey begins with faith and must be continued in faith until the entry into eternal life. This faith comes by hearing – the hearing of the Word of God – Rom.10:17. Whatever is not of faith tends to sin – Rom.14:23. The disciples of Christ recognized the need for them to grow in faith – Lk.17:5. Growing in faith is synonymous with spiritual progress, which every Christian must desire and cultivate.

Conclusion & Life Application

Obedience is better than sacrifice – 1 Sam.15:22. We are blessed and we avoid unnecessary pains of life when we walk with God in obedience. Hosea 6:6 says, “For I desired mercy, and not sacrifice; and the knowledge of God more than burnt offerings.” God is more interested in our living a life marked by love and faith as a right response to His demands, rather than our offering of useless sacrifices. Obedience to follow God’s instruction will ensure that we eat the good of the land – Isa.1:19. This is talking about the blessings of now and hereafter. As God’s children, God has shown us what is good and what He requires of us. It is for us to do that which is just, love mercy, and walk humbly with Him – Mic.6:8.

This is a call to a walk of love and faith. As we walk with God and relate with others around us, it must be done in the atmosphere of love and faith. Remember the admonition of Beloved John (1 Jn.3:14- 18 “14 We know that we have passed from death unto life, because we love the brethren. He that loveth not his brother abideth in death. 15 Whosoever hateth his brother is a murderer: and ye know that no murderer hath eternal life abiding in him. 16 Hereby perceive we the love of God, because he laid down his life for us: and we ought to lay down our lives for the brethren. 17 But whoso hath this world's good, and seeth his brother have need, and shutteth up his bowels of compassion from him, how dwelleth the love of God in him? 18 My little children, let us not love in word, neither in tongue; but in deed and in truth.” Love works no ill (evil) to his neighbour because love is the fulfilling of the law. The pure religion and the undefiled life God requires from us as Christians is to visit the fatherless and widows in their affliction and to keep ourselves unspotted from the world. When we constantly fix our eyes on the glorious hope – the hope of the appearing of the great God and our Saviour, Jesus Christ, we would be motivated to reject all that is opposed to a life of love and faith. Jesus Christ taught His disciples to pray: Thy kingdom come, Thy will be done on earth, as it is in heaven (Matt.6:10). Love and faith are two great virtues God wants enforced in the earth so as to hasten the coming of His kingdom. As Christians, we are to spread the message and manner of love and faith to our present generation.

Questions and Discussion

1. A life marked by love and faith is God’s demand. What enablers do we require or need to achieve this?

2. Is it possible to live a life of love without faith, or faith without love? What is the implication of this?

3. List four blessings of a life of love and faith.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

The Christian is under obligation to radiate to love and faith in the practice of his belief. A life lacking in these is not what Christ lived and died for. A natural person cannot satisfy God’s righteous standard. Therefore, the help and assistance of the Holy Spirit must be sought and harnessed. The Holy Spirit is the Chief Enabler and Enhancer of a life filled with love and faith. If we agree that love and faith are gifts of the Spirit, it necessarily means that it is only the life saturated in the Spirit can produce these graces. One of the reasons we render services to God and to fellow man is the expectation of reward. Indeed, there is nothing wrong or out of place for us to expect reward for what we do because the gracious God has already assured us about this in His word (1 Cor.15:58). We must however note that the basis of God’s reward for service rendered is motivated by love and faith (1 Cor.13:1-3). What drives your passion for the work you are doing for God and others? It should be love and faith if it is to receive God’s compensation. Let us clothe ourselves in humility, love and faith as Christ did (Phil.2:5-11) so that we can gloriously complete our race here on earth.
