Lesson 27

Topic: Church Relationship Part 1

Text: Acts 2:46-47; 9:31; 13:1-3; 14:21-23; 10:47; 20:17-20, 28 .

Supplemental References:

Central Truth: The Church is the assembly of the “called out” ones

Memory Verse: Acts 2:46-47

Lesson Outline

I. Membership of The Church

II. Mode of Church Relationships

III. Mandate of the Church

Lesson Introduction:
We believe that it is essential for all believers to join with and become part of a congregation of other believers. It is important to worship together, observe the ordinances of the church, encourage and support one another, and work together to advance the Lord’s kingdom (Hebrews 10:24, 25).

Lesson Commentary:

I. Membership of The Church:

A. Definition Of Church :

According to the dictionary, a Church is “the whole body of Christians” (1 Cor. 12:12). The Church is a congregation of people who believe in Jesus as Lord and Saviour. Founded in Matthew 16:13-18 but came into physical manifestation in the Acts of the Apostles after Jesus was taken up to heaven. The Church lives in obedience to the Word of God and worships God in Spirit and truth. The Church is either invisible or visible. The Invisible Church is the universal body of Christ of which we become part of from the time of getting born again. However, the Visible Church is a local assembly where believers gather together for worship and manifests as denominations under different names. It is possible to be a card carrying member of a denomination and not be a part of the universal body of Christ.

B. Defence for Church Membership

Scripturally, the criteria for membership of the invisible or visible Church is found in John 6:28-29. The primary criteria is salvation and not abilities or the services we are able to render in the Church. Becoming a member instead of casual attendance at church services is of immense benefit to all believers (Ephesians 2:19). Church membership is akin to blocks that are brought together to form a building. It is very easy for an idle block that is not cemented to the wall of a building to be stolen or destroyed. However, once cemented into the wall, it becomes increasingly more difficult for the block to be taken away. Belonging and getting involved in a local church is for the personal security of individual believers in addition to other benefits that accrue from the relationships that are built in the Church.

II. Mode of Church Relationships

The Church as part of her discipleship agenda should encourage people to grow until they become matured to the point of service (Acts 14:21-23).

A. Meeting Intents

Stronger bonds are established as believers interact in worship services, midweek and house fellowship meetings (Acts 2:46), through inter-church and inter-city Church relationships (Acts 9:31) and leadership accountability interactions (Acts 20:17-20, 28). These interactions recorded in the bible form the biblical basis for house fellowship, membership meetings, workers’ meetings and leaders’ meetings etc. in the Foursquare organization. Instead of being overly critical and/or deviant about these meetings, it would be expedient that everyone works at exploring ways of making the meetings more productive for the benefit of the church. In one way or the other, these meetings help to strengthen individual believers, deepen the knowledge of God, and provide comfort in addition to synergy building and strategy development.

B. Mixed Generations & Cultures

It is vital that Church relationships transcend tribe, language and cut across generational divides. God operates a trans-generational agenda. He is the “God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob.” This is the reason Psalm 71:18 declares, “Even when I am old and gray, do not forsake me, O God, till I declare your power to the next generation, your might to all who are to come.” The old and young generations within our churches all have clear roles to play in ensuring the fulfillment of the scripture, “One generation shall praise thy works to another, and shall declare thy mighty acts” (Psalm 145:4). The Church should build synergies across generational and cultural lines to promote unity and advancement of the gospel.

III. Mandate on Church Relationships

This mandate includes fellowshipping with Jesus and being sent forth to preach (Mark 3:14).

A. Reliance on God: The church has a clear mandate to relate with God from whom strength, grace and victory come since without Him, we can do nothing (John 15:5). The Church in Antioch had men who had lived in the king’s palace and those that had the grace of prophets and teachers, yet, they took time to fast and pray to God (Acts 13:1-3). It was during this time that the Lord spoke to them to separate Barnabas and Saul for the work of ministry. It is important that our leaders and followers rely on God for His leading and direction before appointing people that will lead church planting teams and other assignments within the Church instead of relying on mere physical endowments.

B. Relationship with Unbelievers: The Church also has a clear mandate to relate with unbelievers in a manner that they remain unspotted while pulling them out of the fire (Jude 22-23) into a relationship with Jesus (Eph. 2:12-13). Adequate care must therefore be taken by the Church to nurture relationships in a manner that keeps our light aglow so we can remain relevant to the world at all times (Matt. 5:16).

Conclusion & Life Application:

The Church of God is a single, indivisible, indestructible universal body of believers that the Lord unleashed as an agency to carry out His work on earth. The Lord wants believers everywhere to build a solid network of relationships that cut across denominational, language, cultural and generational divides. Discovering the biblical basis, various meetings and practices in the Church will help to enrich and grow our commitment and dedication.

Questions & Discussion:

1. What is the difference between being a member of a local Church and being a member of the universal Church of Christ?

2. Enumerate two to three practical benefits:

a) You have personally derived from being a part of the Church.

b) Of regular attendance to Bible Study, Sunday Service, Workers’ Meeting, and Leaders’ Meeting etc.

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

Before teaching this lesson, make sure you understand clearly what the Church means and the difference(s) between local Church membership and being part of the universal body of Christ.

You will also need to be abreast with the roles the Church is called to play on daily basis with respect to God, the body of believers and those who are yet to embrace the gospel as well as the nature of relationships the Church should cultivate in order to deliver on these fundamental responsibilities.

Lesson 28

Topic: Church Relationship Part 2 (Benefits of Church Membership)

Text: Acts 2:41-47; 15:22

Central Truth:

Church membership depends upon the genuine Christian experience of the candidate

Memory Verse: Acts 2:46-47

Lesson Outline:

I. Commitment and Continuity: v 41, 42, 46a; Acts 10:47

II. Commonality and Cooperation - v 44, 45, 46

III. Commission of the Church - v 43, 47, Acts 13:1-3; 15:22

Lesson Introduction
We believe that it is essential for all believers to join with and become part of a congregation of other believers. It is important to worship together, observe the ordinances of the church, encourage and support one another, and work together to advance the Lord’s kingdom (Hebrews 10:24, 25).

Lesson Commentary:

I. Commitment & Continuity: v 41, 42, 46a; Acts 10:47

Thousands of people who gladly received the word stayed committed to Jesus Christ and were added unto them (Acts 2:41). “Them” means the Church (body of believers). It is not enough to be identified with Christianity without maintaining fellowship with other believers.

a. Basis for Continuity : Notice the emphasis on continuing steadfastly (Acts 2:42) and “continuing daily with one accord” (vs. 46). It was not enough that they heard, believed and confessed Jesus as their Lord and Saviour. We are called not to be hearers only but doers of those things that we learn from studying the Bible individually or hear through the preaching of God’s Word (James 1:22-24). The practice of Christianity is a present continuous exercise. After believing, it is important that we endure to the end so as to be saved (Matthew 24:13). Things we should continue to do include water baptism (Matthew 28:19; Mark 16:16; Acts 2:38), doctrinal beliefs (2 Tim. 3:16), fellowship (Heb. 10:25), breaking of bread (2 Cor. 11:24-25), and other spiritual exercises like obedience (Romans 6:16), worship (John 4:24), bible study (2 Tim 2:15), soul winning (Matt. 28:19-20), prayer and fasting (Acts 13:3). These practices are pointers to the fact that the believer can do nothing except he abides in Jesus Christ who is the Vine (John 15:4-6). The Church must get deep in the act of discipleship to help people who visit our churches to be retained or continue in the worship of God in the company of other believers (the Church).

b. Benefits of Church Membership : To individual believers, it include edification, exhortation, comfort, show of love (1 Cor. 14:3; Heb. 10:25) through fellowship with one another. In addition, relationships in the Church promotes intimacy (Acts 2:44-45), membership accountability (2 Thess. 3:7-9), welfare for the needy (Acts 4:34-37; 9:36-39; Rom. 12:13) and support during special events e.g. child birth, anniversaries, illness etc. (1 Tim 5:10; 6:18; Gal. 6:10).

II. Commonality & Cooperation - v 44, 45, 46

It is essential that everyone who comes in contact with the Church realizes the oneness and unity of the body. Every believer retains his or her individuality but becomes baptized into one body – the church – at conversion (1 Cor. 12:13; Eph. 4:4). There is beauty and pleasantness in unity (Psalm 133:1-3).

a. Working Together : Christians are supposed to come together, work together, break bread together, pray together, witness together, do things together and operate with singleness of heart. Believers should be lowly in mind instead of self-aggrandizement, strife and vainglory (Philip 2:3). The church is a place where there is neither Greek nor Jew, bond nor free, male nor female, circumcision nor un-circumcision, for all are one in Christ (Rom. 2:10; 1 Cor. 12:13; Col. 3:11).

b. Caring & Sharing : There are lazy and selfish people in today’s church as much as there were in the early church. Yet, the early church connected with those in need by selling their possessions to take care of those in need. Practical demonstration of this unity include wealth sharing (Acts 2:46). This is a real challenge for today’s church. It is a welcome development that a lot of our churches are getting more focused on care and welfare as a means of reaching out to the unsaved. In doing so, the practice of welfare is becoming so regimented and regulated. The picture of love and care is painted of the Church, yet, individual members of the Church are so far from the act of love. It is expedient the Church goes beyond the corporate exhibition of welfare to teaching and encouraging individual believers to learn the act of loving and caring for one another. This would be far more impactful.

III. Commission of the Church - v 43, 47, Acts 13:1-3; 15:22

The Church from the above passages of scripture is commissioned for the following:

a. Signs & Wonders : Signs and wonders are important proof of our faith in God (Acts 2:43; Mark 16:17-18). It is meant to be a tool that will help men to fear (dread, respect, submit) God (Hebrews 12:28-29). The Lord shows up with proof of His power to unbelievers and demonstrates love. There is no doubt that miracles are one of the things that authenticate the Bible. The Church should therefore not shy away from signs and wonders but earnestly pray to God for great manifestations of His power to bring about the healing of incurable diseases, opening of blind eyes and deaf ears etc.

b. Praise God : The Church must not be deficient in the act of praise-giving to God (v 47). Appropriate focus must be placed on the act of worship because God inhabits the praises of His people (Psalm 22:3). This implies that as we praise God, we create the atmosphere in which God loves to dwell. When we praise Him with all our hearts, He comes into our praises and activates miracles. The Church should be careful to make the Lord the center of attraction or focus in her acts of worship. The focus should be removed from men, no matter how powerful or endowed, because no man can do anything except it be given him/her by God. Indeed, all power belongs to God (Matt. 28:18).

c. Attract Favour: The Church is meant to attract favour, not repel favour (Acts 2:47b). Recollect how the children of Israel attracted favour and spoilt the Egyptians (Exodus 12:26). When the favour of the Lord comes upon the Church, the enemies of the Church will become weakened in their capacity to fight against the Church.

d. Soul Winning : The act of evangelism and discipleship becoming increasingly more unpopular in today’s Church whereas, that was what drove growth in the early Church (Acts 2:47c). The Lord added to the Church as every believer following Peter’s preaching caught the fire and began spreading the gospel in residential neighborhoods, work places, king’s palaces and everywhere they went. This was the reason fear came upon every soul. Prayer and fasting meetings were called to seek the face of the Lord for laborers to be sent into the harvest field (Acts 13:1-3; Matt. 9:38; Luke 10:2). Through the acts of evangelism, the indestructible Church of God will continue to multiply (Acts 9:31) and scale all odds (Acts 20:19-20). It is the responsibility of Church Leaders to lead initiatives that will drive commitment to evangelism and the growth of the Church (Acts 15:22).

Conclusion & Life Application:

The Church was equipped with all that is needed to make her succeed. All believers should commit themselves to become responsible members of the Church so as to experience the realization of the full potential that God designed for the Church.

Psalm 110:1-2 declares, “The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou at my right hand, until I make thine enemies thy footstool. The LORD shall send the rod of thy strength out of Zion: rule thou in the midst of thine enemies.”

Ephesians 3:10 says, “To the intent that now unto principalities and powers in heavenly places might be known by the church the manifold wisdom of God.”

Questions & Discussion:

1. What will make it possible for believers to fulfill their roles in the Church? (Acts 2:42, 46)

2. How can the Church improve her efficiency and impact in our world today? (Acts 2:44-46)

3. Outline at least 3 things the Church was commissioned to do (Acts 2:43, 47)

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers’ Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

Study the Bible references and other passages of scripture to dig out why believers should join and operate in a Church after committing their lives to Jesus. Also, identify the benefits of church membership.

Script how you think the Church should operate, detailing the merits and demerits of believers working alone and together. Then, clearly identify what you think the Church has been commissioned to do. What are the hindrances to the non-pursuit or fulfilment of the commission and how can the Church scale the obstacles?

Lesson 29

Topic: Civil Government (The Christian’s duties and obligation)

Text: Rom.13:1-8; I Tim.2:1-2; I Pet. 2:13-17; Acts 5:29.

Central Truth: Submission to the powers of state does not mean

union with the state.

Memory Verse: Rom.13:1 (NKJV)

Lesson Outlines

I. The Biblical rules of obedience to Civil Government

II. The Reasons for obedience to Civil Government

III. The exceptions to the rules of obedience to Civil Government

Lesson Introduction

Christians are blessed to be citizens of a heavenly kingdom; our citizenship is in heaven – Philippians 3:20. We have been conveyed into the kingdom of God's dear Son, and as such, we are described as "sojourners and pilgrims" in this world - 1Pe 2:11. As "pilgrims", we live and work under the governments of men... with a variety of political systems: democracies, kingdoms, dictatorships, etc. offering varying degrees of freedom, responsibilities etc. What is our duty to such governments? The Christian church and organization began and thrived during the Roman Empire, to Christians in the capital city of Rome Paul wrote of their responsibility, to Christians dispersed in outlying areas of the Empire, Peter did also, the Christian's duty to government is made very clear by the apostles.

Lesson Outline

I. The Biblical Rules Of Obedience To Civil Government

Apostle Paul stressed these twice "Let every soul be subject to the governing authorities" - Rom 13:1 "Therefore you must be subject..." - Ro 13:5! Peter likewise taught this duty - 1Pe 2:13-14- "Therefore submit yourselves..."! Submit here means "be subject to" signifying "to place one's self under subjection; to render one's self subordinate".

We are to submit "to every ordinance of man", not to a particular law passed by government, but to the civil government or institution itself, that we are to submit to every human institution (whether it be a monarchy, democracy, totalitarian state, etc.) -- Our responsibility is clear: "Let every soul be subject" – Rom. 13:1

We are to pay taxes: as an act of submission we should pay our taxes - Ro 13:6 and other fees that are due, such as customs - Ro 13:7. We may not approve of how the taxes are spent, but I doubt the early Christians approved of how Nero spent the government's money either.

We are to respect whom respect is due - Rom 13:7, such as police officers, judges, and if you do evil, be afraid! - Ro 13:4, honor to whom honor is due - Ro 13:7; cf. 1Pe 2:17, such as presidents, kings, governors, local leaders, out of respect for the office, if not for the man (or woman) our duty is not limited to those whose political or personal behavior we approve.

For Kings and all in authority, we are to offer supplications, prayers, intercessions - 1Ti 2:1-2, praying for those who lead, not only our country, but those around the world, praying that they rule with wisdom, righteousness, and mercy.

We are to offer thanks - 1Ti 2:1-2, taking time to thank God for those who rule well, thanking God for when we live in peace and prosperity, and for protecting us when we do not. Invaluable contributions Christians can give their country are their prayers.

II. The Reasons For Obedience To Civil Government

That we might lead good lives; good in a material sense, to enjoy quiet and peaceful times- 1Th 4:11; He 12:14a; good in a spiritual sense, free to be godly and reverent - He 12:14b. As God works through the governments of men to bestow peace, prayer should be a priority for those who wish to live in peace.

Governing authorities that exist have been appointed by God! – Rom. 13:1, the book of Daniel 2:20-21; 4:17 emphasized that even those that are evil, which God often uses for His divine purposes and then replaces - cf. Exo. 9:16 (Egypt); Isa 10:5-12 (Assyria)

Therefore to resist government means to resist God Himself! - Rom 13:2-4, to resist is to bring judgment upon one's self, for government is a minister of God, designed to avenge evil. Peter adds two good reasons - 1Pe 2:15. First and foremost, "this is the will of God" also, "for the Lord's sake" - 1Pe 2:13.

This will suffice for all true servants of God, that we may "put to silence the ignorance of foolish men" because of their allegiance to a heavenly King, Christians are often falsely accused of sedition or treason - e.g., Act 17:5-8, and by doing good (e.g., by submitting), we can "silence" ignorant charges, therefore to avoid wrath and have a good conscience, "you must be subject" - Rom 13:5.

III. The Exceptions To The Rules Of Obedience To Civil Government

There are occasions on which Christians not only may, but must disobey the civil authorities, anytime a civil government requires a Christian to do what God forbids or forbids them to do what God commands, then the person must disobey.

It is not whenever government is oppressive, consider the government and conditions when Paul and Peter wrote, the government was totalitarian, under Nero's evil and despotic rule as emperor, under his reign, Christians suffered greatly - 1 Peter 4:12-13; 5:8-9, Paul and Peter were eventually martyred.

The only exception: we must obey God rather than man! As illustrated by Peter and the apostles - Acts 4:18-20; 5: 27-29! When government tries to force us to disobey God, we must disobey the government, even then, we may break only the particular law designed to force disobedience to God, we have no authority to break other laws in protest to the unjust law, when government seeks to stifle our service to God, we must obey God rather than man!

Every human government is accountable to God and is accountable to maintain its affairs with justice and with righteousness. When the government is no longer acting justly and no longer protecting life—sanctioning abortions, same sex marriages, for example—then it is the task of the church to be the prophetic voice, to call the state to task and tell the state to repent and do what God commands it to do.

Conclusion & Life Application

That government must assume some form, the administration cannot be left to chance, there must be a constitution, clearly defined, and generally known and approved. The first form of government was that of the family. But, as families multiplied, each having a variety of rights, out of which would emerge differences not to be easily settled, some more general form became necessary, Government by patriarchy having fallen through, many other forms are possible, and have become actual. Which then is the one ordained of God? This does not concern the apostle.

Questions & Discussion

1. Why are civil laws necessary? How does Paul define failure to obey the civil laws of society? Romans 13:2-4

2. What just punishment do law breakers deserve? See Romans 13:3-4

3. What two reasons does Paul give for obedience to the civil law? See Romans 13:5.

4. What example does Paul give under civil law which Christians are obliged to be obedient to?

5. Can you think of three examples in the Gospel where Scripture supports obedience in paying taxes? - Luke 2:1-5, Matthew 17:24-27, 22:12-22; Mark 12:13-17; Luke 20:20-26; 23:2

6. For what reason does Jesus tell His disciples they should pay the required tax in Matthew 17:27?

7. Under what circumstances civil law would not be binding upon the conscience or actions of Christians? Matthew 22:18-22

8. Are Christians obliged to obey civil laws that are contrary to the Law of God?

9. Ultimately, from who do civil authorities receive their authority and to whom are unjust rulers and judges answerable and how will they be judged?

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

Those civil authorities who hold the responsibility for maintaining civil order and for providing just laws only serve according to God's pleasure. Therefore, Apostle Paul maintains, it is our responsibility as Christians to support and respect the civil authority and the laws enacted through them.

These civil authorities also we honor with our obedience. And we do so not only out of fear but out of love; not only for reasons of human convenience but because our conscience tells us to act in this way. The reason for this is based on the fact, as the Apostle says, “that all authority comes from God; therefore, one must give everyone what is his due."

The government is made for the people, and not the people for the government. To the masses it matters little what form of government obtains, but it matters much indeed whether the government rules according to wise or unwise principles. Yet, after all, any government at all is better than none, and none is possible if no obedience is to be secured.

These duties are enforced on two grounds — that they are ordained of God, and therefore ought to be obeyed as a matter of conscience. This implies that it is according to God's purpose that society should be organized into self-governing communities for —

(1) Protection against aggressions from without.

(2) For the restraint of wrong-doing and the promotion of prosperity within.

Lesson 30

Topic: The Holy Scriptures (Part 1)

Text: Ecclesiastes 12:9-14; II Tim.3:12-17; Psalm 19:1-11

Central Truth:

The Bible is God’s revelation to man that brings God and man together.

Memory Verse: 2Timothy 3:16, 17

Lesson Outline

I. Introduction to the Holy Scriptures (Ecclesiastes 12:9-14)

II. Inspiration of the Holy Scriptures (II Tim.3:12-17)

III. The Purpose of the Holy Scriptures (Psalm 19:1-11)

Lesson Introduction

We believe that the Bible is the inspired Word of God; it is absolutely true, enduring, and unchangeable (2 Timothy 3:16, 17; Matthew 24:35).

Lesson Commentary

I. Introduction to the Holy Scriptures (Ecclesiastes 12:9-14)

The Bible is commonly referred to as the Holy Book. It introduces God to us, tells us how to relate with Him and instructs us on godly living. Solomon's teaching in the book of Ecclesiastes, like other books in the Scripture, provides a guide and stimulus to godly living and a secure basis for living (Acts 26:14; Jer. 10:1-5). In Psalms 95:6-7, the concepts of Shepherd and Creator are combined as they are in Eccl. 12:1,11. Because of the peculiar value and authority of the words, Solomon warned his son and all his readers not to seek answers beyond those God had given through the wise/His word. If they would keep looking for answers in many other books, they would wear themselves out (Prov. 1:8,10,15; 2:1; 3:1,11,21; 4:10,20; 5:1,20; 6:1,3,20; 7:1; 19:27; 23:15,19,26; 24:13,21; 27:11).

Someone has said that life is like a school. God teaches us primarily from His Word; but He also teaches us through creation, history, and the various experiences of life. Solomon explained the characteristics of his own work as a teacher of God's truth. His teaching was also orderly (Eccl. 12:9). After studying a matter, he weighed his conclusions carefully, and then arranged them in an orderly fashion. He claimed that his words were inspired/given by God, the One Shepherd (Eccl. 12:11). Inspiration was the special miracle ministry of the Holy Spirit that enabled men of God to write the Word of God as God wanted it written, complete and without error (2 Tim. 3:16-17; 2 Peter 1:20-21).

II. Inspiration of the Holy Scriptures (II Tim.3:12-17)

The Scriptures are true and dependable. "All Scripture is God breathed". That is, given by the inspiration of God. The doctrine of the inspiration of Scripture is vitally important, and a doctrine that Satan has attacked from the beginning (Gen. 3:1). The Holy Spirit of God used men of God to write the Word of God (2 Peter 1:20-21). The Spirit did not erase the natural characteristics of the writers. In fact, God in His providence prepared the writers for the task of writing the Scriptures. Each writer has his own distinctive style and vocabulary. Each book grew out of a special set of circumstances.

Biblical inspiration means the supernatural influence of the Holy Spirit on the Bible's writers, which guaranteed that what they wrote was accurate and trustworthy. Revelation means the communicating of truth to man by God; inspiration has to do with the recording of this communication in a way that is dependable. Whatever the Bible says about itself, man, God, life, death, history, science, and every other subject is true. This does not mean that every statement in the Bible is true, because the Bible records the lies of men and of Satan. But the record is true.

III. The Purpose of the Holy Scriptures (Psalm 19:1-11)

All of the Scriptures are God's witness to us of who He is, what He has said and done, and what He wants us to be and to do. The witness God bears of Himself in the written Word is sure and reliable. Through the Word, we become wise concerning salvation (2 Tim 3:15) and the principles of successful living (Prov. 2; 8:33; 10:8). The "simple" are the childlike people who humbly receive God's truth (Matt. 11:25; Luke 10:21-24). The statutes are God's detailed instructions concerning the practical matters of everyday life. The commandment of the Lord means “that which is appointed”. Because the Lord loves us, He commands us on what to do and warns us what not to do, and how we respond is a matter of life or death (Deut. 30:15-20). God's commands are pure and lead to a pure life, if we obey from the heart.

The Bible is the Holy Scriptures (Rom. 1:2; 7:12; 2 Tim. 3:15), and therefore His Word is "very pure" (Ps. 119:140; Prov. 30:5). We are enlightened and learn God's truth when we obey what He says (John 7:17) and not just when we read it or study it (James 1:22-25). The Word of God is the road map to guide us (Ps. 119:19). Like a traveler on the highway, we need the divine guidance of God to reach our destination. To walk with God, we need to have reverence for Him. The fear of the Lord reminds us that we cannot learn the Word of God unless we show reverence and respect for the God of the Word. To teach the Bible is to teach the fear of the Lord (Ps. 34:11; Deut. 4:9-10), and the mark of a true Bible student is a burning heart, not a big head (Luke 24:32; 1 Cor. 8:1). The fear of God is clean and maturing. We do not decay or deteriorate as we walk in the fear of the Lord (2 Cor. 4:16-18).

Conclusion & Life Application

Paul noted that the Scriptures are able to make one wise with regard to salvation, a lesson Timothy had learned long before. He pointed out that all Scripture is God-breathed, "inspired", that is, God's words were given through men superintended by the Holy Spirit so that their writings are without error. We must lay a good foundation for our children following Timothy’s example.

Then Paul asserted the "usefulness" of the Word. For each aspect of Timothy's ministry, whatever it might be - teaching (instructing believers in God's truths), rebuking those in sin (1 Tim 5:20; 2 Tim 4:2), correcting those in error (2 Tim 2:25; 4:2), and training (child-training) in righteousness (guiding new believers in God's ways) - for all of these and more, the written Word of God is profitable.

Through the word, the man of God (one who must provide spiritual leadership to others) must be "complete, capable, and proficient in the sense of being able to meet all demands." Paul added that he must be equipped (furnished) for every good work (2 Tim. 2:21). Like Timothy, we must have confidence in God's ability to supply all our needs through the Word.

It was important for all Christians to realize that persecution awaits everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ (John 15:18-21). Paul wrote that we could actually expect the situation to get worse and the pressure to intensify in the last days. False teachings would increase as evil men and impostors (charlatans) go from bad to worse, deceiving and being deceived. We must resist feeding on error.

Questions & Discussion

1. What is the importance of the Word of God?

2. What do you understand by the inspiration of the Scripture?

3. How can we use the Word of God to profit us?

Teaching the Lesson (Teachers Site)

Teaching Overview/Insight

In Psalms 19:7-9, David described the efficacious nature of the Law of the Lord. In the Old Testament, the Law was the dominant element in God's specific revelation. The perfect Law of God (flawless) can change people (Ps. 12:6; 18:30; Prov. 30:5). It revives the soul and the Law's statutes can be trusted to make one wise. The precepts of the Law give joy to the heart and its commands enlighten one's eyes, that is, brightens his life and guide him (Ps. 19:8).

The statutes of the Lord detailed instructions concerning the practical matters of everyday life. For the Old Testament Jew, the statutes related to what they ate, how they dressed, how they kept clean, and so forth. Love is the most important and pre-eminent virtue in the Christian walk because:

c. It is the summation of all the laws and commandments of God – Matt.22:36-40

In seeking to define love from the Christian perspective, the “All about God” website submits that love is not the ‘warm fuzzy feeling’ that characterizes the world’s definition of love, that one may not have that ‘warm fuzzy feeling’ for everyone that you meet, but that you must love everyone that comes your way. (Some of the points below relate more to family relationship, while some are common to both family and friendship relationships)

c. Each family member playing his or her God-given role faithfully – Eph.5:22-33; 6:1-4

Through maturity and spiritual growth, characterized by: being spirit-filled with the Holy Spirit and with the Word of God (including the study of other helpful materials), being reflective and learning from experience, having a teachable spirit, having the fear of God, having the consciousness of the law of sowing and reaping, and being driven by thoughts of heaven and of eternity – I Cor.3:1-4; Eph.4:11-15

I. Trinity of the Godhead

The doctrine of Trinity can easily be understood from Romans 1:20, “For the invisible things of him from the creation of the world are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made, even his eternal power and Godhead; so that they are without excuse.” Divine Trinity means that there are three separate and distinct persons in the Godhead (the Father, Son and Holy Spirit). It expresses one in unity as well as one in number (1 John 5:7; John 17:11, 21-23). There are three separate persons in divine individuality and divine plurality. As individual person, each can be called God and collectively they can be spoken of as one God because of their perfect unity.

- The Father is called God (1 Cor. 8:6).

- The Son (Jesus Christ) is called God (Isaiah 9:6-7; Heb. 1:8; John 1:1-2, 20:28).

- The Holy Spirit is called God (Acts 5:3-4).

In the scripture, Trinity describes the three-in-one nature of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, we also see all the three members of the Trinity together:

- In Mark 1:10-11, the Holy Spirit descended like a dove on Jesus, and the voice proclaimed the Father’s approval of Jesus as His divine Son.

- In Acts 1:4-5, it describes the unique relationship of the Trinity - God the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit.

- In Matthew 28:19, Jesus’ word affirms the reality of the Trinity.

II. God the Father (John 3:14-36)

In the Christendom, God is conceived of as "Father". The scripture declares, "Our Father ... in heaven" (Matt 6:9,14,26), "the God and Father of the Lord Jesus" (2Cor. 11:31). It expresses the tenderness of relation and wealth of love and grace embraced in God. Pagan religions also could speak of God as "Father", and in the general sense of being the Creator, God has a universal fatherly relation to the world (Acts 17:24-28). In the Old Testament, God was revealed as Father to the chosen nation (Ex. 4:22). God’s fatherly love is declared to be the image of His pity for those who fear Him (Ps. 103:13). The meaning of Fatherhood in God is made clear in John 1:18.

The concept of Father as applied to God can be seen in these passages (Ex. 4:22; Deut. 32:6; 2 Sam. 7:14; Ps. 89:27,28, etc.). It also denotes His covenant relation to the Jews (Jer. 31:9; Isa. 63:16; 64:8; John 8:41, etc.). Believers are called God's "sons" (John 1:12; Rom 8:16; Matt 6:4,8,15,18; 10:20,29). They also call him "Father" (Rom. 1:7; 1 Cor. 1:3; 2 Cor. 1:2; Gal 1:4). In John 3:16 the eternal love of the Father was expressed in sending His begotten Son to pay the wages of man’s sin and purchase eternal redemption for humanity.

The name Jesus signifies saviour. It is the Greek form of JEHOSHUA (Joshua). The name Christ signifies anointed (John 1:41), the name given to the long-promised Prophet and King whom the Jews had been taught by their prophets to expect (Matt 11:3; Acts 19:4). Other names are sometimes added to the names Jesus Christ: "Lord," "a King," "King of Israel," "Emmanuel," "Son of David," "chosen of God." Jesus Christ was born of the Virgin Mary, God being his father, at Bethlehem of Judea. The twofold designation “Jesus Christ” combines the personal name ‘Jesus’ and the title ‘Christ’, meaning "anointed" or "Messiah." The significance of this title became clear during the scope of His life and ministry.

Matthew chapter 16 portrays Jesus as offering Himself as the Lamb of God. He healed the sick, He fed the crowds, He continued to warn against the Pharisees who had been unable to interpret the many signs of the King's presence. And then, Jesus asked the fateful question: "Who do men say that the Son of man is?" And the disciples report confirmed what had already been made clear. The nation had rejected Jesus. Peter's confession of faith, "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God" (Matt. 16:16), was a confession which Israel could not and would not make. After Peter's affirmation, Jesus called Peter blessed. God had revealed Christ's identity to him. And Jesus went on to say, "On this rock I will build My church." We need personal revelation of Jesus Christ to excel in our Christian race.

The co-existence of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit in the unity of the Godhead (divine nature or essence) is termed as Trinity. The doctrine of the Trinity means that within the being and activity of the one God, there are three distinct persons: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. This explained why God demands brotherly love from us which promotes unity, oneness and understanding of one another with a view to gender peaceful co-existence. The early church manifested the power of God in such great dimension because they were in one accord. In unity lies the power.

The Father gives all authority to the Son and bears witness to Him, as does Jesus to Himself (John 8:18). Yet, the Son claims nothing for Himself; He gives all glory to the Father who has sent Him (John 12:49-50). The key to unlocking the mystery of the Trinity is to observe how the persons of the triune family give themselves to one another in selfless love. They are always at one another's disposal. We must be our brother’s keeper.

Lesson Commentary

I. Baptism is by Immersion

There are some types of baptism apart from that of immersion. They emerge from ecclesiastical tradition. A notable one is effusion or sprinkling. This could be done on anyone wanting to be baptized. It is often done in infant baptism which has become controversial. Baptism by immersion has very strong scriptural basis hence it is the one accepted by Evangelicals and Pentecostals. Our Lord Jesus Christ is the model for the baptism by immersion done at the age of accountability. The weakness in doing infant baptism is in the fact that the child being baptized does not know what is happening to him or her, whereas baptism is to be on the basis of the testimony of the person to be baptized, having received the Lord Jesus Christ as his or her Saviour, which is the sure salvation experience.

As against the wrong doctrine held to by some individuals baptism is not salvation. Baptism is an outward manifestation of an inward work of grace that had been done. There the commanded phenomenon of water baptism is made clear. Water baptism is not pronounced in the Old Testament. The intertestamental period must have produced the ritual cleansings in Hebrew history prior to the advent of Jesus Christ. Peter re-echoed the command to be baptized in his sermon on the Day of Pentecost (Acts 2:38, 41). Throughout the book of Acts, the Apostles observed the ordinance, baptizing their converts: Acts 8:12; 8:36-38; 9:18; 10:47,48; 16:15; 16:33; 18:8; 19:5,6; 22:16). The early church continued obeying the command of getting those who believe in the Lord Jesus baptized. The church faithfully observed water baptism as a sacrament. D. The book of Acts and Paul’s epistles gave us much insight to the early church observance of that sacrament.

George E. Rice informed us about baptism in the early church that “the evolution of Christian baptism through the centuries has been recorded in mortar and bricks, paint and mosaics. Among the ruins of early Christian structures, and also in ancient churches still in use, the history of Christian baptism can be traced. Paintings in catacombs and churches, mosaics on floors, walls, and ceilings, sculptured reliefs, and drawings in ancient New Testament manuscripts add details to this history, as well as raising interesting questions that need further investigation.

The record left by these various witnesses overwhelmingly testifies to immersion as the normal mode of baptism in the Christian church during the first ten to fourteen centuries. This is in addition to the evidence found throughout the writings of the church fathers that immersion was the early church's common mode of baptism. Most students of church history are acquainted with the early written record about baptism, but what do the mosaics, the mortar, the brick, and the paint say?

For some time scholarly circles have been discussing the origin of Christian baptism. Some see its origin in the mystery cults that flourished during the first century A.D. Actually, it is not necessary to go beyond the religious heritage in which Christianity has its roots—the religion of Israel.”

Naaman rated River Jordan as having not a great status as the rivers of his nation. His healing however came to be as a result of eventually humbling himself. May we pursue humility and always aim to be like Jesus our Lord. He subjected Himself to be baptized by John the Baptist in River Jordan a less esteemed river. Jesus submitted to immersion to fulfil all righteousness. May we pursue righteousness.

Baptism with the Holy Spirit or in the Holy Spirit in Christian theology is a term describing baptism (washing or immersion) in or with the Spirit of God and is frequently associated with incorporation into the Christian Church, the bestowal of SPIRITUAL GIFTS, and empowerment for Christian ministry.

Before the emergence of the holiness movement in the mid – 19th century and Pentecostalism in the early 20th century, most denominations believed that Christians received the baptism with the Holy Spirit either upon conversion and regeneration or through rites of Christian initiation, such as water baptism and confirmation.
